# Chapter 1: The Dream Before the Machine (Cybernetics -> Dartmouth)

## Thesis
The early conceptualization of artificial intelligence was fundamentally constrained and later liberated by the physical infrastructure of post-war computing. The paradigm shift from the analog, biologically-inspired models of Cybernetics to the digital, symbolic logic of the Dartmouth consensus was not merely a theoretical debate, but a pragmatic adaptation to the newly available, programmable digital infrastructure like the IBM 701 and 704.

## Scope
- IN SCOPE: The Macy Conferences on Cybernetics, early analog computing limitations, the architectural shift to von Neumann digital computers, and the planning of the 1956 Dartmouth Summer Research Project (specifically the reliance on IBM's digital infrastructure).
- OUT OF SCOPE: The actual events and outcomes of the Dartmouth Workshop beyond its initial proposal and setup (belongs to Chapter 2); the Perceptron (belongs to Chapter 3).

## Scenes Outline
1. **The Analog Constraint (1946-1953):** The Macy Conferences and the vision of Cybernetics. Early attempts to model intelligent behavior (like W. Grey Walter's analog tortoises) were biologically inspired but severely limited by the physical realities of analog circuits, vacuum tubes, and the inability to store large symbolic programs.
   - *Sources:* Wiener (1948), Walter (1953), Kline (2015).
2. **The Digital Paradigm Shift (1945-1950s):** John von Neumann's architecture and the transition from hard-wired analog systems to programmable digital machines (EDVAC, IAS machine). The realization that symbolic manipulation in stored memory could simulate complex logic, providing a new infrastructural foundation for AI.
   - *Sources:* von Neumann (1945), Dyson (2012), McCorduck (1979/2004).
3. **The Dartmouth Proposal (1955):** John McCarthy, Marvin Minsky, Nathaniel Rochester, and Claude Shannon draft the proposal for the 1956 workshop. The crucial role of IBM's backing (via Rochester) and the explicit anticipation of utilizing the IBM 704's unprecedented digital compute power to kickstart "Artificial Intelligence" as a distinct, symbolic discipline separate from Cybernetics.
   - *Sources:* McCarthy et al. (1955), IBM 704 Manual (1956), McCorduck (1979/2004).
