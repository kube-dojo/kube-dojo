# Brief: Chapter 33 — Deep Blue

## Thesis

Deep Blue's 3.5-2.5 victory over Garry Kasparov in May 1997 was the first time a reigning world chess champion lost a match to a machine under tournament time controls — but it was not, as the contemporary headlines claimed, an arrival of artificial intelligence. The win was earned by twelve years of single-purpose hardware engineering: a chess-only application-specific integrated circuit (ASIC) designed by Feng-hsiung Hsu beginning in 1985 at Carnegie Mellon, refined into the 1997 Deep Blue II as 480 such chips operating in concert with 30 IBM RS/6000 SP nodes that ran a hybrid software/hardware alpha-beta search. The system "thought" by examining 100-200 million chess positions per second across roughly 6-8 ply of base search and as many as 40 ply along forcing lines — fast enough to compensate for an evaluation function tuned by grandmaster consultant Joel Benjamin but knowable to no machine-learning algorithm. Two of the match's most famous moves — Deep Blue's bug-induced 44.Rxd3 in Game 1 and its panic-time 36.axb5 / 37.Be4 in Game 2 — illustrate the gap between what the machine actually did and what its human opponent thought it was doing. Kasparov mistook a fail-safe for omniscience and a panic-time search artifact for grandmaster intuition; the misreading shaped the rest of the match. Deep Blue won. It also could not play tic-tac-toe.

## Scope

- IN SCOPE:
  - The 12-year ASIC arc from Hsu's 1985 ChipTest at Carnegie Mellon through Deep Thought (1988-1991), Deep Thought II (1992-1995), the 1996 Philadelphia match (Kasparov 4-2 Deep Blue), the rebuild of the chip and SP system, and the 1997 New York rematch (Deep Blue 3.5-2.5 Kasparov).
  - The 1997 system architecture: 30-node IBM RS/6000 SP, 480 chess chips fabricated in 0.6-micron CMOS, 16 chips per node on two Micro Channel cards, hybrid software/hardware alpha-beta search with selective extensions, minimum-window alpha-beta in chip silicon, Joel Benjamin's grandmaster consulting role.
  - Two specific games as case studies in the human-machine misreading: Game 1 (Kasparov wins; Deep Blue's 44th-move bug) and Game 2 (Deep Blue wins; the 36.axb5 / 37.Be4 cheating accusation; the engineered "panic time" search behind the move).
  - Game 6 as the deciding miniature, with Deep Blue's knight sacrifice on e6 in the Caro-Kann Steinitz Variation; Kasparov resigns after 19.c4.
  - Aftermath: IBM declines a rematch, retires Deep Blue in September 1997, intends one rack for the Smithsonian.
  - The architectural-dead-end critique: the chess chip's silicon-encoded chess knowledge cannot be reused for any other game or domain; brute-force ASIC scaling solved chess but did not generalize.

- OUT OF SCOPE:
  - General-purpose machine learning. Deep Blue did not learn during the match.
  - The 1996 Philadelphia match's individual games beyond context-setting (covered in Newborn 2003 Chs. 6-7).
  - Pre-1985 computer chess history. Belle (Condon-Thompson, 1980), Hitech (Berliner et al.), Cray Blitz, Chess 4.5 — these are background, not subject.
  - The post-2017 AlphaZero / Stockfish-NNUE neural-evaluation era. Forward-pointer to Ch.48 (AlphaGo) and to the eventual neural-evaluation revolution; not the chapter's argument.
  - The cheating accusation as legal/ethical drama. The chapter cites Kasparov's accusation as historical fact and shows the engineering reality (panic-time search); it does not adjudicate the dispute beyond what primary sources permit.

## Boundary Contract

This chapter must NOT present Deep Blue as the moment artificial intelligence "arrived." Every primary source — Hsu's IEEE Micro chip-design paper, Campbell's AAAI search-control paper, Newborn's chronicle, Campbell's own 2017 retrospective — describes a single-purpose machine whose chess strength came from hardware-encoded chess rules and an evaluation function tuned by a human grandmaster, not from a learning algorithm or general representation of intelligence. The chapter's stance: Deep Blue's victory was a 12-year *engineering* triumph that proved a specific empirical claim — that 200 million chess positions per second and a tuned evaluation function can defeat a 2815-rated human under tournament time controls — and nothing more. It did not "understand" chess. It cannot generalize to other games. The IBM team itself declined a rematch and retired the machine within four months.

The chapter must also NOT invent dialogue, internal states, or claimed motives for Kasparov, Hsu, Campbell, or any other team member beyond what primary sources document. Newborn 2003 quotes Campbell directly on the move-44 bug and its psychological effect; that quote may be used. Beyond such anchored material, no reconstructed inner monologue is permitted.

The chapter must also handle three known conflicts honestly, not silently:
1. The IBM event site (32 nodes / 256 chess processors) versus Hsu 1999 IEEE Micro and Newborn 2003 (30 nodes / 480 chess chips). The post-match designer figure (30 / 480) is correct; the IBM marketing figure was either pre-match-anticipation or rounding. Cite the conflict.
2. The "200 million positions per second" headline (Hsu 1999 p.72, Newborn 2003 p.123) versus Campbell's own 2017 SciAm retrospective ("between 100 million and 200 million depending on the type of position"). Use Campbell's range as the honest version.
3. The "Game 2 36.axb5 was uncannily human" framing (Kasparov's accusation, repeated in much chess journalism) versus the engineering account (Newborn 2003 pp.159-160, citing Bruce Weber NYT 5/13/97: 36.axb5 was selected via panic-time deepening when the alternative 36.Qb6 dropped in evaluation). Use the engineering account; cite the accusation as Kasparov's interpretation.

## Scenes Outline

1. **The Long Approach (1985-1996).** Hsu's CMU project: ChipTest (1986-87) → Deep Thought (1988-91, won the 2nd Fredkin Intermediate Prize) → Deep Thought II (1992-95). The IBM hire of Hsu and Campbell in 1989. The 1996 Philadelphia match: Game 1 was the first computer win against a reigning champion in regulation time; Kasparov adapts mid-match and takes the rest 4-2. The team's takeaway: speed alone wasn't enough; chess knowledge needs grandmaster tuning. Joel Benjamin is hired as consultant; the chip is redesigned for the rematch.
2. **The Chip and the Cabinet.** The 1997 hardware: 480 chess chips in 0.6-micron CMOS at 40-50 ns cycle time, ~1 watt each; 16 chips per RS/6000 SP node on two Micro Channel cards × 8 chips; 30 nodes total. The hybrid search — alpha-beta with selective extensions in software on the SP processors handles ~8 plies, hands off to chess-chip hardware search for the last 4-5 plies (using a minimum-window alpha-beta variant). Sustained throughput ~200 million positions/sec; per-move search budget ~3 minutes ⇒ "20 to 40 billion positions" examined per move (AAAI 1999). The chess chip itself: move generator (8×8 silicon chessboard combinatorial logic), smart-move stack with O(n) repetition detector, fast/slow evaluation function, search control state machines. Why it had to be silicon: a single chess-chip "position" requires ~40,000 general-purpose CPU instructions; hardware turns that into 10 chip cycles.
3. **Move 44 and the Bug (Game 1, May 3, 1997).** Kasparov plays White, Réti / King's Indian Attack. Deep Blue, never behind in material, gradually deteriorates over the late middlegame; on its 44th move a known intermittent bug — five trigger paths, four fixed, one missed — causes a fail-safe random selection (44.Rxd3, eval drop ≈ 300 points). Hoane debugs it overnight. Kasparov's team analyses the move, finds alternatives also losing, and concludes (per Campbell) that Deep Blue must have searched 30-40 plies and seen everything lose. They do not consider that the move was simply a bug. The misreading carries into Game 2.
4. **Move 36 and the Panic (Game 2, May 4, 1997).** Deep Blue plays White, Ruy Lopez Smyslov Variation. After 35.Bxd6 Bxd6, Deep Blue evaluates 36.Qb6 at +87 across an 8-ply search. As the search deepens to 9, 10, 11 ply, the evaluation drops by more than a quarter pawn. Deep Blue triggers a "panic time" extension — extra wall-clock time. After ~100 more seconds it completes 36.Qb6 (final eval +48), then quickly evaluates the alternative 36.axb5 (eval +63), and commits. The move surprises Kasparov, who had expected the materialistic 36.Qb6. Two moves later, 37.Be4 — also positional rather than material-grabbing — convinces Kasparov that a human grandmaster must be intervening. He resigns Game 2 in a position later shown drawable by perpetual check (45...Qe3). After the match, Kasparov publicly accuses IBM of cheating. Modern engines (Houdini, Stockfish, Komodo) confirm 36.axb5 is the correct move on the merits — but it was selected by a frightened search routine, not grandmaster intuition.
5. **Game 6 and the Aftermath (May 11, 1997, and later).** Mother's Day Sunday at the Equitable Center. The match is tied 2.5-2.5. Kasparov, with Black, plays the Caro-Kann Defence — an opening he hadn't played in formal tournament play since the early 1980s — reasoning that Deep Blue's opening book will be weaker on it. Deep Blue plays the Steinitz Variation; on move 8, with Kasparov having played 7...h6, Deep Blue sacrifices a knight: 8.Nxe6. The sacrifice is a known refutation that Kasparov, expecting a less aggressive engine response, played into. Kasparov resigns after 19.c4, in fewer than twenty moves — the shortest game of either match. Final score 3.5-2.5. The honest close: IBM declined Kasparov's request for a rematch and retired Deep Blue by September 1997. Campbell told *Scientific American* in 2017 that the team "had achieved our goal." The chess chip was a single-purpose silicon artifact; its knowledge could not be reused. The win was a hardware victory, not a generalizable AI breakthrough. Forward pointer: the 2010s neural-evaluation revolution (Ch.48 onward) would solve a different problem in a different way.

## Conflict Notes

- **Node count: 30 vs 32.** IBM event-site (Wayback) and current IBM corporate history both say 32; Hsu 1999 IEEE Micro and Newborn 2003 both say 30. Use the post-match designer figure (30 nodes / 480 chess chips); flag the conflict in prose, do not erase it.
- **Throughput: a single number or a range?** "200 million positions/sec" is the headline but Campbell to *Scientific American* 2017 says the actual range was 100-200M depending on position type. Campbell-Hoane-Hsu (2002) slide deck reports 126M average / 330M peak. Use Campbell's 2017 range as the honest framing. Do not write "200 million" as if it were a deterministic single number.
- **Move-44 bug as cause of Game 2 loss.** Campbell himself (per Newborn 2003 p.151) advances the theory that Kasparov's team overestimated Deep Blue based on the bug move. Silver (2012) popularized the theory. Kasparov has not corroborated it in primary sources I've located. Frame as Campbell's *interpretation*, not a settled fact.
- **Move 36/37 in Game 2.** Two competing framings: (a) Kasparov's accusation that 36.axb5 / 37.Be4 was uncharacteristically positional, evidence of human intervention; (b) the engineering account in Newborn 2003 pp.159-160 (citing Weber NYT 5/13/97) showing the move was selected via panic-time extension when the alternative dropped in evaluation. Use the engineering account; cite the accusation as Kasparov's interpretation; cite the modern-engine vindication (Albert Silver, ChessBase 2015) as a separate independent fact.
- **Smithsonian / CHM rack disposition.** Newborn 2003 ref [10] confirms IBM retired Deep Blue in September 1997 and Newborn p.221-222 mentions the Smithsonian intent. The specific "one rack to Smithsonian, one to CHM" claim, popular in some sources, is Yellow until museum accession records verify.
