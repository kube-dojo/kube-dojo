## Conflict Notes

- **Node count: 30 vs 32** — IBM's pre-match site and current corporate-history page say "32 nodes / 32 processors." Hsu 1999 IEEE Micro pp.71-72 and Newborn 2003 p.122 (both post-match by the actual designers and a chronicler with team access) say "30 nodes / 480 chess chips." Use the post-match designer figure; document the conflict in prose.
- **Throughput: a single number or a range?** — "200 million positions per second" is the headline figure that has stuck culturally. Hsu 1999 p.72 anchors it as "sustained speed." Campbell to *Scientific American* 2017 said the actual range was 100-200M depending on position type. The slide-deck rendering of Campbell 2002 gives a 126M average and a 330M peak. The chapter should use the Campbell-2017 range as the honest version and treat 200M as the marketing-shorthand sustained number.
- **Game 1 move-44 bug as cause of Game 2 loss** — Campbell's own 1997-era retrospective (per Newborn 2003 p.151) advances this theory. Silver 2012 popularized it. Kasparov has not directly corroborated it in any primary source I've located. Frame as Campbell's *interpretation*, not a settled historical fact.
- **Rack disposition** — "Smithsonian" claim is Green for IBM's stated intent (Newborn 2003 ref [10]); the "one rack to Smithsonian, one to CHM" specific claim is Yellow until the museum accession records verify.
