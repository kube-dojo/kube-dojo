# Scene Sketches: Chapter 23 - The Japanese Threat

## Scene 1 - The Conference That Looked Like a Warning

Open with FGCS'81/early Western interpretation: a Japanese national computer
project announced around AI, logic programming, natural language, and future
hardware, arriving after Japanese success in semiconductors and consumer
electronics. The scene should show why foreign observers heard an industrial
challenge even when ICOT leaders were describing a research hypothesis.

Evidence: Fuchi 1992 pp.3-4; Fuchi 1984 pp.18-19; OTA 1983 pp.206-208.

## Scene 2 - The Software Crisis Inside Japan

Use Kinoshita's FGCS'84 speech: computers and personal computers spreading fast,
semiconductor costs collapsing, software demand exploding, and projected
software personnel shortages. FGCS should feel like Japan's attempt to solve an
internal bottleneck, not only an export strategy. Bridge this directly to the
logic-programming choice as a higher-level, declarative productivity bet, while
avoiding unanchored claims that it would let non-specialists program computers.

Evidence: Kinoshita 1984 pp.7-12; Furukawa 1987 p.1.

## Scene 3 - The Stack Diagram

Make Furukawa's conceptual stack the explanatory center: knowledge information
processing on top, logic programming in the middle, highly parallel architecture
below, VLSI underneath. Explain why logic programming was attractive as a bridge:
symbolic expressiveness above, machine-executable parallelism below. Where the
anchors permit it, make the parallel-logic rationale concrete as proof/search
work that can be distributed across a non-von-Neumann architecture, leading into
GHC/KL1 without turning the scene into a language survey.

Evidence: Furukawa 1987 pp.1-3; Kurozumi 1992 pp.9-10.

## Scene 4 - The Institute for a Hypothesis

Show committee meetings, MITI's budget process, ICOT, and the staged project
plan. This is not a startup and not a single lab demo. It is a state-backed
research machine with rotating researchers, vendors, conferences, budgets, and
stage gates.

Evidence: Kurozumi 1992 pp.9-12.

## Scene 5 - A Logic Operating System

Make the machines tangible: PSI as a personal inference workstation, ESP/KLO,
SIMPOS written in ESP, then KL1, Multi-PSI, PIM, and PIMOS. This scene should
show that "fifth generation" meant rebuilding the programming substrate, not
adding an expert-system shell to existing computers.

Evidence: Fuchi 1984 pp.22-23; Fuchi 1992 pp.4-6; Kurozumi 1992 pp.11-13.

## Scene 6 - Washington Reads the Signal

Use OTA's language as the U.S. perception scene. The fear is that Japan could
leapfrog installed software inventories and IBM compatibility by building a new
class of easy-to-use, knowledge-processing machines. Then use NRC's later account
to show that the threat stimulated U.S. responses, including DARPA Strategic
Computing, without claiming sole causation.

Evidence: OTA 1983 pp.206-208; NRC 2005 pp.36-38.

## Scene 7 - The Dream Gets Smaller

Fuchi in 1992 is the honest narrator: the 1981 image was exaggerated; solving
the hardest AI problems or human-scale machine translation in ten years was not
the actual manageable project. Applications were removed, pattern recognition
was narrowed, and parallel inference became the disciplined core.

Evidence: Fuchi 1992 pp.3-6.

## Scene 8 - Not Triumph, Not Nothing

Close with the evaluation workshop and follow-on project. Gallaire sees limited
real-world use and limited direct manufacturer impact, but real technical skill
in machines, parallel OS, load distribution, and logic programming. Uchida shows
the portability problem and the KLIC/free-software solution. The legacy is
research infrastructure and trained people, not market conquest. Use the final
paragraph as a hinge to Part 5: specialized symbolic infrastructure had taught
real lessons, but its portability and application limits leave the book ready
for the mathematical/statistical turn rather than another hardware-first promise.

Evidence: FinalEval p.4; Gallaire 1992 p.64; Uchida 1994 section 1.
