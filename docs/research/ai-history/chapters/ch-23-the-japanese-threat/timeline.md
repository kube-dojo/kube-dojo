# Timeline: Chapter 23 - The Japanese Threat

## Anchored Chronology

- **1979:** The Fifth Generation Computer Research Committee and subcommittee
  were established. Kurozumi later describes the 1979-1981 period as a
  preliminary study stage.
- **End of 1980:** The committee had decided project objectives and continued
  studies on technical matters, social impact, and project schemes.
- **October 1981:** FGCS'81 was held to announce committee results and discuss
  the topic with foreign researchers. Fuchi later said the conference helped
  generate a sensational, exaggerated international image.
- **1982:** MITI launched the FGCS national project and ICOT began as the core
  organization. Furukawa says the project began in 1982; Kurozumi says it
  launched after the three-year study stage.
- **Fiscal 1982-1984:** Initial stage. R&D focused on basic fifth-generation
  computer technologies. Kurozumi lists the initial environment as Prolog on DEC
  machines.
- **1984:** FGCS'84 conference. Fuchi restated the original philosophy and
  corrected misunderstandings; Kinoshita framed the project within Japan's
  advanced information society and software crisis.
- **Fiscal 1985-1988:** Intermediate stage. R&D shifted to small and medium
  subsystems. Kurozumi lists ESP on PSI/SIMPOS as the main environment.
- **1986/1987:** Furukawa's ICOT TR-228 described the project near its halfway
  point and emphasized GHC, CIL, logic programming, and program transformation.
- **1988:** Multi-PSI and PIMOS were demonstrated at FGCS'88, according to
  Fuchi's 1992 retrospective.
- **Fiscal 1989-1992:** Final stage. R&D focused on a total prototype system,
  KL1, Multi-PSI/PIM, and PIMOS. The final stage was extended to four years to
  allow evaluation and improvement.
- **1992:** FGCS'92 marked the end of the main project, demonstrated results,
  and announced free disclosure of ICOT software. The evaluation workshop
  collected international assessments.
- **April 1993:** The two-year FGCS Follow-on Project began to disseminate FGCS
  technology and port KL1/PIMOS-style tools to Unix-based sequential and parallel
  machines through KLIC.
- **1994-1995:** Uchida reported KLIC and other systems being distributed as
  ICOT Free Software, with maintenance/distribution planned after ICOT's closure
  in March 1995.
