# Brief: Chapter 18 - The Lighthill Devastation

## Thesis

The Lighthill episode was not a simple execution notice for all artificial
intelligence. It was a public policy review that separated AI into
application-facing automation, computer-based studies of the nervous system, and
a disputed middle category that Lighthill called the bridge activity of
"Building Robots." The devastation came from that middle category becoming
institutionally suspect inside the UK Science Research Council: basic,
general-purpose AI lost credibility just as researchers needed expensive
machines, languages, and software ecosystems to compete with US laboratories.

## Scope

This chapter should follow Ch17's funding-and-authority theme into the British
policy arena. It should begin with the SRC commissioning an outsider's survey,
then explain Lighthill's ABC classification, the technical substance of his
combinatorial-explosion critique, and the responses from Sutherland, Needham,
Longuet-Higgins, and Michie. The chapter should close with the aftermath: a
loss of confidence in UK AI funding, continued research under tighter
application framing, and the later IKBS/Alvey revival.

## Boundary Contract

- Do not say the Lighthill report rejected all AI. Lighthill explicitly saw
  defensible promise in categories A and C and expected work to continue when
  integrated with applications or psychology/neurobiology.
- Do not globalize the institutional effect beyond the evidence. The strongest
  anchored claim is UK/SRC loss of confidence; broader "worldwide AI winter"
  framing needs secondary attribution and should not drive the chapter.
- Do not say British AI stopped. Chilton and House of Lords sources both support
  a more precise claim: research continued, but major UK activity returned under
  different labels and policies, especially IKBS/Alvey.
- Do not present Lighthill as technically ignorant. His report correctly
  identified domain knowledge, narrow universes of discourse, and combinatorial
  explosion as core problems. The controversy is about classification,
  policy-weighting, and whether basic AI could be justified on its own terms.
- Do not present Michie/Sutherland as mere lobbyists. They made substantive
  arguments that "B" was basic intelligence theory and a source of languages,
  search methods, and programming ideas.
- Do not claim a precise budget cut unless a primary SRC/SERC budget document is
  added. Current evidence supports loss of confidence and funding diminution,
  not exact amounts.

## Narrative Spine

1. **The Commissioned Outsider:** SRC faces rising AI grant applications and
   asks Lighthill for a two-month outsider's view.
2. **The ABC Trap:** Lighthill sorts the field into A, B, and C; the fight is
   over whether B is a vague bridge or the scientific core of AI.
3. **Combinatorial Explosion as Policy Argument:** machine translation,
   theorem proving, chess, robotics, and Winograd's blocks world become evidence
   that general methods collapse outside constrained domains.
4. **The Rebuttal Table:** Sutherland reframes B as basic research; Michie
   attacks the survey method and argues for "Intelligence Theory."
5. **Machines, Software, and Dependency:** Michie's DEC System 10 point shows
   the infrastructure stakes: British labs needed access to the US AI software
   ecosystem, not merely more local enthusiasm.
6. **Winter Without Extinction:** the report helps produce a UK loss of
   confidence and a colder funding climate, while AI continues under application
   pressure and later returns through IKBS/Alvey.
