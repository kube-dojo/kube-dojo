# Timeline: Chapter 18 - The Lighthill Devastation

- 1947: Turing's "Intelligent Machinery" appears in Lighthill's report as the
  notional starting point for AI's twenty-five-year history.
- 1966: The ALPAC report on machine translation becomes part of Lighthill's
  evidence base for disappointed expectations in language work.
- 1972: SRC commissions Sir James Lighthill to prepare an independent outsider
  report on AI after increasing grant applications across engineering,
  mathematical, and biological areas.
- July 1972: Lighthill completes "Artificial Intelligence: A General Survey."
- August 1972: Sutherland writes a response defending the central category as
  Basic AI research rather than merely a bridge activity.
- 1973: SRC publishes *Artificial Intelligence: a paper symposium*, including
  Lighthill's report and responses from Sutherland, Needham, Longuet-Higgins,
  and Michie.
- June 1973: BBC broadcasts the Lighthill Controversy debate at the Royal
  Institution with Lighthill, Donald Michie, Richard Gregory, and John McCarthy.
- 1970s: UK AI continues, but the Chilton overview reports a loss of confidence
  in the academic and funding establishment lasting almost a decade.
- September 1982: The Research Area Review Meeting on Intelligent
  Knowledge-Based Systems marks the next major UK attempt to mount an AI-related
  activity.
- 1980s: IKBS becomes part of the Alvey Programme, reframing AI under a more
  application-linked and industrially legible policy label.
