# Timeline: Chapter 28 - The Second AI Winter

- **1982:** Japan's Fifth Generation Computer Systems project begins, aiming at knowledge-processing computers for the 1990s and later KL1/PIM work (FGCS 1992 pp.iii-v).
- **1983:** Strategic Computing is sold partly against Japan's Fifth Generation challenge; Roland/Shiman describe the Japanese program as a concern and political lever for U.S. computer-research funding (Roland/Shiman pp.5, 37, 56-57).
- **Mid-1980s:** Expert systems spread through industry and defense technology-transfer programs; knowledge acquisition remains the limiting step (Atkinson 1990 p.161).
- **1986-1988:** DARPA expert-system funding rises under Amarel, from $3.6M in 1986 to $5.2M in 1988 (Roland/Shiman p.276).
- **1987:** DARPA leadership review under Schwartz becomes skeptical of AI scaling and "new ideas" from crash programs; AI/robotics budgets begin to fall (Roland/Shiman pp.274-276).
- **1988:** Neumann's XCON case study records the maintenance burden of a large rule-based success: 6,200+ rules, ~20,000 parts, and about half the rules expected to change annually (Neumann 1988 p.34).
- **May 1988:** Symbolics layoffs and losses make specialized AI hardware economics visible: 225 of 640 workers laid off nationwide, $24.8M nine-month loss, and pressure from cheaper Sun/Apollo workstations (Bates, LA Times, May 24, 1988).
- **1989-1990:** DARPA Strategic Computing's AI/expert-system center of gravity weakens; expert-system funding is cut to $3M by 1990 (Roland/Shiman p.276).
- **1990:** Atkinson's IAAI paper says knowledge acquisition remained the major stumbling block to efficient expert-system development during the 1980s (Atkinson 1990 p.161).
- **1992:** FGCS'92 presents final ten-year project results and prototype Fifth Generation systems (FGCS 1992 pp.iii-v).
- **1993:** Strategic Computing disappears as a DARPA budget line and is effectively transmuted into High Performance Computing (Roland/Shiman p.285).
