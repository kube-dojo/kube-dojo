# Scene Sketches: Chapter 4 — The Statistical Roots

Five scenes map 1:1 to the Prose Capacity Plan layers in `brief.md`. Each scene names its evidence anchors and stays inside its budgeted word range. No invented dialogue, no invented internal states.

## Scene 1: The Theological Provocation (1902–1905)

- **Setting:** Russia at the turn of the twentieth century. St. Petersburg University on one side, Moscow University on the other. Two empires of probability theory, separated by the Pulkovo meridian and a long-running dispute over what mathematics is permitted to claim about human action.
- **Beat:** Pavel Nekrasov, a mathematician at Moscow University with a theological-seminary education, publishes an argument: the law of large numbers applies only to independent events; crime statistics conform to the law of large numbers; therefore individual human acts must be voluntary — free will is mathematically demonstrable. From St. Petersburg, the 49-year-old Andrei Markov reads the argument as a misuse of probability theory and writes — to A. A. Chuprov, in private correspondence preserved in Ondar 1981 — that Nekrasov's work is "an abuse of mathematics." Markov is not, by temperament, a measured opponent: he had asked the year before to be excommunicated alongside Tolstoy; he had refused tsarist honors after the 1902 Gorky veto; he will, in 1913, organize a counter-anniversary against the Romanov tercentenary.
- **Pedagogical use:** Establish that the chapter's mathematical motivation is theological and political, not linguistic. Markov did not set out to model language. He set out to refute Nekrasov.
- **Narrative use:** Set up the chapter's arc — the theorem comes before the demonstration. Frame Nekrasov via Markov's reading; the chapter does not have direct primary access to Nekrasov's 1902 paper.
- **Anti-padding rule:** Do not embellish Markov's character beyond the four documented anchored anecdotes (Tolstoy excommunication 1908; Gorky veto protest 1902; Chuprov "abuse" letter; *Ars Conjectandi* counter-anniversary 1913). Resist any temptation to invent a Petersburg-vs-Moscow scene.
- **Word target:** 700–1,000 words.
- **Evidence anchors:** Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology" section (Nekrasov 1902 argument paraphrase, Markov "abuse of mathematics" quote, secular-Petersburg-vs-ecclesiastical-Moscow framing, 1902 Gorky protest, 1908 Tolstoy excommunication request); Seneta 2006 p.3 (Nekrasov 1853–1924 dates, "what Markov interpreted as a claim in 1902"); Link 2006 *Traces* p.321 (Markov 1856–1922 dates).

## Scene 2: The 1906 Counterexample

- **Setting:** St. Petersburg, 1905–1906. Markov has retired from St. Petersburg University as Emeritus Professor but still teaches the probability course he inherited from Chebyshev in 1883.
- **Beat:** In a paper for the Kazan *Izvestiia*, Markov constructs the counterexample to Nekrasov: a two-state chain with all four transition probabilities strictly between zero and one, for which the Weak Law of Large Numbers holds despite the dependence between successive trials. The proof is symbolic, on paper, and runs without empirical illustration. Nine more chain-dependence papers follow over the next six years — generalizations to multiple states, to "complex" chains, to chains with unobserved events. They appear in the Kazan *Izvestiia*, the *Bulletin de l'Académie Impériale des Sciences de St.-Pétersbourg*, and (in 1910) *Acta Mathematica* — the last giving the work its first French-language readership outside Russia. Across all of them, Markov refuses on principle to look for empirical applications. His letter to Chuprov: "I am concerned only with questions of pure analysis…. I refer to the question of the applicability of probability theory with indifference."
- **Pedagogical use:** Explain the chain-dependence claim concretely using Markov's 2-state framing — "if the current state is A, the next state is A with probability p1 and B with probability 1−p1; the law of large numbers still applies." Avoid the temptation to overflow into matrix mathematics or contractivity coefficients (Seneta 2006 §7 covers them; not load-bearing for an AI-history book).
- **Narrative use:** Establish the six-year purely-theoretical period during which Markov could (or would) not connect his chain proof to anything concrete. This is not a detour; it is the chapter's central tension. The 1913 lecture only makes sense as the moment that posture broke.
- **Anti-padding rule:** Do not list every paper from 1906–1912; gesture at "a series" and name only the 1906 paper specifically. Do not invent a "Markov struggling to find an application" scene; the primary evidence (Link 2006 *Chains to the West* p.563 + the Chuprov letter) supports indifference, not struggle.
- **Word target:** 500–800 words.
- **Evidence anchors:** Seneta 2006 pp.1–3 (1905 retirement, Chebyshev course inheritance, 1906 motivation as counterexample to Nekrasov, "fiery post-cards" 1910); Hayes 2013 "Counting Vowels and Consonants" section (2-state framing of 1906 paper, Chuprov "indifference" letter); Link 2006 *Chains to the West* p.563 + bibliography pp.587–589 (1906–1912 series, "could not think of a practical illustration… or was not interested in finding one").

## Scene 3: The Bernoulli Bicentenary Lecture (23 January 1913)

- **Setting:** The physical-mathematical faculty of the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg, January 23, 1913 (Old Style). Russia at large is preparing to celebrate three hundred years of Romanov rule, which begins in May. Markov has chosen a different anniversary: the bicentenary of Jakob Bernoulli's *Ars Conjectandi*, posthumously published in 1713 by his nephew Niklaus, the book that first formulated the law of large numbers.
- **Beat:** Markov stands in front of his colleagues with a hand-counted table of 20,000 letters from Pushkin's *Eugene Onegin* — the entire first chapter and sixteen stanzas of the second, with hard signs ъ, soft signs ь, punctuation, and spaces stripped out. He has organized them into 200 small tables of ten rows by ten columns, counted the vowels in each column, paired columns 1+6 / 2+7 / 3+8 / 4+9 / 5+10, and summed horizontally and vertically across each table. The tables fill an entire page of the published paper.
  - The arithmetic mean of vowels per 100 letters is 43.19. The unconditional probability that a random letter is a vowel is 0.432.
  - Returning to the unbroken stream of letters, Markov counts pair patterns: 1,104 vowel-vowel pairs; 8,638 vowels total; so the probability of a vowel given the previous letter was a vowel — Markov's p1 — is 1104/8638 = 0.128. By subtraction he gets 7,534 consonants following a vowel and 3,827 consonant-consonant pairs; the probability of a vowel following a consonant — p0 — is 7534/11361 = 0.663.
  - The empirical dispersion coefficient, computed via the method of least squares (Legendre 1805 / Gauss 1809), is 0.208. The theoretical dispersion coefficient under the assumption that the chain has "simple" (first-order) dependence is 0.300. The fit is rough but unmistakably better than the independence-assumption value of 1.0.
  - Markov does not stop there. He counts triple patterns: 115 vowel-vowel-vowel sequences and 505 consonant-consonant-consonant sequences. After two vowels in a row, the probability of a third vowel collapses to 0.104; after two consonants, the probability of a vowel rises to (1 − 0.132) = 0.868. Plugging these into a second-order chain gives a theoretical dispersion of 0.195, much closer to the empirical 0.208. The dependence is real, and it extends at least one letter back.
- **Pedagogical use:** Walk the reader through the 1-step and 2-step chain calculations using Markov's actual numbers. Do not invent a toy alphabet. The 66.3% (vowel after consonant) and 87.2% (consonant after vowel) figures that show up in popular accounts are p0 and q1 from p.596 of the published lecture; cite them at the page level and use them as the pedagogical core.
- **Narrative use:** This is the chapter's longest scene because it is also the chapter's only fully primary-anchored scene. Every sentence is tied to a page in Markov 1913 (Link translation), Link 2006 *Traces*, or Hayes 2013. Use this scene to slow down and let the math breathe — the reader should leave understanding what a Markov chain *is* by way of doing the calculation Markov did.
- **Anti-padding rule:** Do not invent atmospheric details (audience reaction, weather, room layout). The published primary record is silent on these. Do not stage a Pushkin literary-criticism digression — Link 2006 *Traces* offers thirty pages of philological interpretation, but they are out of scope for an AI-history book. Stay with the counting.
- **Word target:** 1,000–1,400 words.
- **Evidence anchors:** Markov 1913 (Link translation) p.591 (lecture date, venue, 20,000 letters, definitions of p, p1, p0, p1,1, p1,0, p0,1, p0,0); pp.591–592 (200×100 paired-column table method); p.595 (arithmetic mean 43.19, p = 0.432, sum of squared deviations 1022.8 / 200 = 5.114); p.596 (1,104 vowel-vowel pairs, 8,638 vowels, p1 = 0.128; 3,827 consonant-consonant pairs, p0 = 0.663; δ = −0.535); p.597 (115 v-v-v and 505 c-c-c triples, p1,1 = 0.104, q0,0 = 0.132); pp.597–598 (dispersion coefficients 0.195 vs. 0.208); Link 2006 *Traces* p.324 (lecture occasion as Bernoulli bicentenary, political dimension); pp.326–327 (counting method, Figure 1 reproduction); p.329 (arithmetic mean derived via modal method; method of least squares anchoring); Hayes 2013 "Counting Vowels and Consonants" section (8,638 vowels / 11,362 consonants / 1,104 vowel-vowel / 3,827 consonant-consonant counts, hand-counting time estimate); Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology" section (Markov organized *Ars Conjectandi* counter-celebration against Romanov tercentenary).

## Scene 4: The Forty-Year Silence and Shannon's 1948 Adoption

- **Setting:** The decades 1913–1948. Markov dies in Petrograd in 1922 having left two empirical applications of his chains: the *Onegin* paper of 1913, and a second 100,000-letter analysis of S. T. Aksakov's *Childhood Years of Bagrov's Grandson* (1858), published posthumously in 1924 in the appendix to the 4th edition of his *Calculus of Probability*. Both appear only in Russian. Nekrasov dies in 1924, two years after Markov, his work falling into post-revolutionary obscurity. The Markov chain itself does not.
- **Beat:** The chains travel west not through Markov's papers but through the term-of-art "Markoff process." Liebmann's 1912 German translation of Markov's textbook seeds the German mathematical community. Maurice Fréchet writes *Méthode des fonctions arbitraires. Théorie des événements en chaîne dans le cas d'un nombre fini d'états possibles* in Paris in 1938, generalizing the Markoff-process formalism. Andrei Kolmogorov's 1933 *Grundbegriffe* recasts probability — including Markov chains — in measure-theoretic language. By 1948, "Markoff process" is a settled mathematical term with an established literature, even though the *Onegin* paper itself remains unread outside Russia.
  - Then Claude Shannon publishes "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" in *Bell System Technical Journal* vol. 27, July and October 1948, pp. 379–423 and 623–656. Section 3, "The Series of Approximations to English," runs first-order, second-order (digram), third-order (trigram), and word-level n-gram approximations to English text. The output is famous: "OCRO HLI RGWR NMIELWIS EU LL NBNESEBYA TH EEI ALHENHTTPA OOBTTVA NAH BRL" for the second-order; "IN NO IST LAT WHEY CRATICT FROURE BIRS GROCID PONDENOME OF DEMONSTURES OF THE REPTAGIN IS REGOACTIONA OF CRE" for the third. Section 4, "Graphical Representation of a Markoff Process," generalizes the construction. Footnote 6 cites M. Fréchet, *Méthode des fonctions arbitraires*, Paris, Gauthier-Villars, 1938 — "for a detailed treatment." Markov is not cited.
  - Shannon's worked text approximations are themselves a hand-counting exercise. Section 3 is explicit: the second-order example "was constructed by the use of a book of random numbers in conjunction with… a table of letter frequencies." The third- through sixth-order examples were generated by opening a book at random, finding the previous n−1 letters, and copying the next one. Shannon notes that "the labor involved becomes enormous at the next stage." The infrastructure of 1913 is, for this purpose, still the infrastructure of 1948.
  - In 1955 Morris Halle, an MIT linguist, makes the first English translation of Markov 1913 at the request of colleagues exploring statistical approaches to language. The translation is never published; it survives only in mimeograph in a few libraries until 2006, when David Link and colleagues publish the first widely available English version in *Science in Context*. By then, the Markov chains it describes have been digital infrastructure for fifty years.
- **Pedagogical use:** Explain that Shannon's adoption is concept-via-genus-name, not citation-via-paper. The intellectual route is real (Markov → Liebmann/Fréchet/Kolmogorov → Shannon); the bibliographic route is indirect.
- **Narrative use:** Make visible how long mathematical ideas can travel without their author. Markov died unread in English; Shannon ran his successor experiments unaware of the 1913 lecture; the Halle translation sat in mimeograph for half a century.
- **Anti-padding rule:** Do not invent a Shannon-encounters-Markov scene; there is no evidence Shannon read the *Onegin* paper. Do not turn this scene into a Shannon-information-theory primer; that is Ch9's job. Stay with the n-gram approximations and the footnote.
- **Word target:** 600–900 words.
- **Evidence anchors:** Hayes 2013 "The Bootless Academician" section (Halle 1955 mimeograph, Link 2006 first published English, Markov died 1922, Nekrasov 1924); Link 2006 *Traces* p.325 (Aksakov 100,000-letter second study); Link 2006 *Chains to the West* pp.562–563 (1906–1912 series, German Liebmann translation 1912 as transmission to Western Europe); Shannon 1948 §3 (worked English approximations, OCRO HLI / IN NO IST verbatim, "labor becomes enormous"); Shannon 1948 §4 footnote 6 (Fréchet 1938 reference).

## Scene 5: The Honest Close

- **Setting:** A short narrative pause before the chapter hands off to Ch5 (*The Neural Abstraction*) and forward-references Ch9 (Shannon-era information theory) and Ch30 (*The Statistical Underground*).
- **Beat:** Say plainly what Markov did and did not do. He proved a theorem in 1906 against a theological misuse of mathematics. He demonstrated the theorem in 1913 on a literary text he chose for its accessibility, not for any belief that text was the future of probability theory. He continued to insist, to Chuprov, that he was indifferent to applications. He did not foresee text generation, language models, or computational linguistics. The chain of transmission to twenty-first-century language modeling is real but indirect; it ran through Liebmann, Fréchet, Kolmogorov, and a fifty-year delay before published English access to the *Onegin* paper. The Halle 1955 mimeograph and the Link 2006 publication are bookends on a long silence.
- **Pedagogical use:** Reframe the chapter's title. The "statistical roots" are real but they are not roots in the sense of a continuous line of cultivation. They are roots in the sense of a buried theorem that took decades to be wanted.
- **Narrative use:** Close honestly. Mention that the practical questions modern Markov-chain research cares about — convergence speed, error bounds for prematurely terminated simulations, MCMC sampling — are questions only a computer can answer, and Markov never had one.
- **Anti-padding rule:** Do not draft a "and now LLMs use Markov chains" passage. Modern LLMs are not Markov chains in any technically meaningful sense; the autoregressive training objective is statistical-prediction-of-next-token, but the architecture is non-Markovian (full attention over the entire context window is a violation of the Markov property). Forward-reference Ch30 sparingly; do not anticipate its argument.
- **Word target:** 300–500 words.
- **Evidence anchors:** Hayes 2013 "Counting Vowels and Consonants" section (Markov-Chuprov letter on indifference); Hayes 2013 "The Bootless Academician" section (1922 death, Halle 1955 / Link 2006 transmission); Shannon 1948 §4 footnote 6 (Fréchet routing, not Markov citation); Link 2006 *Chains to the West* p.563 (1906–1912 purely theoretical period).
