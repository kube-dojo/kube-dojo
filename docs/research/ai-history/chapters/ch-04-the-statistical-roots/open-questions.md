## Q1 — Nekrasov's 1902 paper at primary access

**Question.** What did Nekrasov's 1902 paper actually argue, in his own words?

**Why it matters.** Scene 1 frames Nekrasov's free-will / law-of-large-numbers argument via Markov's interpretation as paraphrased by Hayes 2013 ("Mathematical Theology") and Seneta 2006 (p.3). Hayes gives the popular-science form ("voluntary acts are independent → LLN applies → social statistics conform → therefore acts are voluntary"); Seneta gives the technical form (Markov interpreted Nekrasov as claiming that pairwise independence was both necessary and sufficient for the WLLN). These two readings are compatible but not identical. Without primary access, the chapter must hedge: "Markov read Nekrasov as claiming X."

**What would help.** Russian-language access to Nekrasov 1902 (the specific paper Markov targeted with his 1906 counterexample). Sheynin 2004 *Probability and Statistics: Russian Papers, Selected and Translated* (NG Verlag, Berlin) may include relevant Nekrasov material; not personally accessed.

**Tractability.** Yellow. A native Russian-speaking collaborator could resolve this in a single archive visit. Not a hard blocker for prose; the existing Hayes + Seneta anchors support a hedged Scene 1 already.

**Status.** Open. Anchored at Yellow; do not promote without primary access.

## Q4 — Pushkin transcription details

**Question.** Which edition of Pushkin's *Eugene Onegin* did Markov use, and what counts as the "first 20,000 letters" — i.e., where exactly does his cut-off fall in the canonical text?

**Why it matters.** Markov 1913 (Link translation) p.591 says "the entire first chapter and sixteen stanzas of the second." Eugene Onegin is in iambic tetrameter sonnets ("Onegin stanzas"); each sonnet is exactly 14 lines. The first chapter has 60 stanzas; sixteen stanzas of the second adds 16 × 14 = 224 lines plus the first chapter's 60 × 14 = 840 lines, for 1,064 lines total. Whether this comes to exactly 20,000 letters of Russian-Cyrillic text excluding ъ, ь, punctuation, and spaces depends on the edition and on Markov's letter-counting conventions.

**Why it might not matter.** The chapter does not need to reproduce the exact letter count; "first 20,000 letters" is sufficient for prose. Resolving this is interesting but not load-bearing.

**Tractability.** Green-ish, but low priority. A Russian-speaking collaborator with a Pushkin edition could verify; the Link 2006 *Traces* paper p.327 reproduces Markov's table layout, which would let a reader verify the count empirically.

**Status.** Open. Not a blocker. Note in prose only as "the first 20,000 letters... approximately the entire first chapter and sixteen stanzas of the second."

## Q5 — Was Markov's 1913 lecture publicly attended?

**Question.** Who attended Markov's 23 January 1913 lecture? Was it a public Academy session or a faculty-internal seminar?

**Why it matters.** The boundary contract forbids invented audience reactions; this question is asked only to ensure the chapter does not over-stage the lecture. Markov 1913 (Link translation) p.591 footnote 1 says only "Lecture at the physical-mathematical faculty, Royal Academy of Sciences, St. Petersburg, 23 January 1913." Hayes 2013 calls it an "address to the Imperial Academy of Sciences." Link 2006 *Traces* p.324 says "Markov gave a lecture to the physical-mathematical faculty."

**What would help.** Access to *Bulletin de l'Académie Impériale des Sciences de St.-Pétersbourg* meeting records for January 1913 — the Academy's *Proceedings* and announcements would record who was in the room. Not personally accessed.

**Tractability.** Yellow. Russian-language and archival access required; tractable but not load-bearing.

**Status.** Open. Frame the lecture in prose as "a lecture to the physical-mathematical faculty of the Imperial Academy of Sciences" (per the primary anchor); do not embellish the audience.

## Q6 — The bicentenary-vs-tercentenary framing

**Question.** Did Markov explicitly position his *Ars Conjectandi* bicentenary commemoration as opposition to the Romanov tercentenary, or is that Hayes 2013's interpretation?

**Why it matters.** Hayes 2013 ("Mathematical Theology") frames Markov's choice of anniversary as "a different anniversary: the publication of *Ars Conjectandi* 200 years before" — explicitly contrastive with the Romanov tercentenary. Link 2006 *Traces* p.324 is more cautious: "Obviously, this gave the physical-mathematical faculty's event a political dimension." Hayes asserts the contrast; Link only notes the political dimension.

**What would help.** Markov's correspondence around 1912–1913, particularly any letters or Academy meeting records announcing the *Ars Conjectandi* commemoration. Probably in the Markov Papers held at the Russian Academy of Sciences archive (St. Petersburg branch).

**Tractability.** Yellow. Russian-language and archival; not load-bearing.

**Status.** Open. Use Link's hedge ("political dimension") in prose; flag Hayes's stronger reading as one historian's interpretation if needed.

## Q7 — Shannon's actual reading list in 1948

**Question.** Did Shannon read any Markov primary or secondary source before drafting "A Mathematical Theory of Communication"? Or was his Markoff-process formalism inherited entirely through Fréchet 1938 / Kolmogorov 1933 / contemporaries' textbook treatments?

**Why it matters.** Scene 4 frames Shannon's adoption as concept-via-genus-name, not citation-via-paper, on the strength of Shannon 1948 footnote 6's exclusive reference to Fréchet. But absence of citation is not the same as absence of reading. Shannon could have read translations of Markov's textbook (Liebmann 1912 German edition); he could have read Khinchin or Kolmogorov; he could have encountered Markov via a colleague at Bell Labs.

**What would help.** Shannon's working notes for the 1948 paper, preserved at the MIT Museum / Bell Labs archives. Possibly also his 1940 PhD thesis at MIT for any earlier engagement with stochastic processes.

**Tractability.** Yellow. Bell Labs / MIT archive access required. The Soni and Goodman 2017 biography *A Mind at Play* (Simon & Schuster) reportedly covers his pre-1948 reading; not personally accessed.

**Status.** Open. The chapter's framing is correct as stated (Shannon does not cite Markov); resolving this question would let Scene 4 say "Shannon does not cite Markov, but evidence X suggests he encountered the chain formalism through pathway Y." Useful but not blocking.

**Tractability.** Yellow. Single archive visit.

**Status.** Open. Not load-bearing; the chapter mentions Halle 1955 only to establish the 33-year delay and the further 51-year wait until print.
