# People: Chapter 4 — The Statistical Roots

## Verified Protagonists

### Andrei Andreevich Markov (1856–1922)

- **Born:** June 14, 1856 (June 2 o.s.) in Ryazan, Imperial Russia. **Died:** July 20, 1922 in Petrograd / St. Petersburg. (Seneta 2006 p.1.)
- **Father:** A government employee in the forestry service who later became the manager of an aristocrat's estate. (Hayes 2013 "Peterburgian Math".)
- **Education:** St. Petersburg University, where his teachers included Pafnuty Chebyshev. (Hayes 2013; Seneta 2006 pp.1–2.)
- **Career:**
  - 1883: Took over Chebyshev's probability theory course at St. Petersburg University on Chebyshev's retirement; continued teaching it for the rest of his life. (Seneta 2006 pp.1–2.)
  - 1886: Appointed Extraordinary (Associate) Professor; elected Adjunct of the Imperial Academy of Sciences. (Seneta 2006 pp.3–4.)
  - 1890 (o.s.): Elected Extraordinary Academician. (Seneta 2006 p.4.)
  - 1893: Promoted to Ordinary (full) Professor. (Seneta 2006 p.4; Hayes 2013.)
  - 1896: Elected Ordinary Academician. (Seneta 2006 p.4.)
  - 1905: Retired from St. Petersburg University as Emeritus Professor; remained Academician at the Imperial Academy. (Seneta 2006 p.2.)
- **Family:**
  - Married Maria Valvatieva (1883), daughter of the owner of the estate his father once managed. (Hayes 2013.)
  - One son, Andrei Andreevich Markov Jr. (1903–1979), who became a distinguished Soviet mathematician and head of the Department of Mathematical Logic at Moscow State University. The shared three-part name has caused biographical confusion. (Seneta 2006 p.4; Hayes 2013.)
- **Mathematical contributions in scope for this chapter:**
  - 1906: 2-state proof of the Weak Law of Large Numbers for chain-dependent variables. *Bulletin de la société physico-mathématique de Kasan* (2) 15(4):135–156. (Link 2006 *Chains to the West* p.587; Seneta 2006 p.3.)
  - 1907–1912: Series of follow-up papers generalizing the chain-dependence proof, all "purely theoretical" without empirical illustration. (Link 2006 *Chains to the West* pp.563, 587–588.)
  - January 23, 1913 (o.s.): Lecture at the Imperial Academy of Sciences applying the chain proof to the first 20,000 letters of Pushkin's *Eugene Onegin*. Published in *Bulletin de l'Académie Impériale des Sciences de St.-Pétersbourg* (6) 7(3):153–162. (Markov 1913 (Link translation) p.591; Link 2006 *Chains to the West* p.588.)
  - 1924 (posthumous, 4th edition of *Calculus of Probability*): Parallel statistical analysis of the first 100,000 letters of S. T. Aksakov's *Childhood Years of Bagrov's Grandson* (1858). (Link 2006 *Traces* p.325; Link 2006 *Chains to the West* p.588.)
- **Character and politics:**
  - "By most accounts, Markov was a nettlesome character, abrasive even with friends, fiercely combative with rivals." (Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology".)
  - 1902: After Tsar Nicholas II vetoed Maxim Gorky's election to the Imperial Academy, Markov announced he would refuse all future tsarist honors (without resigning his own membership, unlike Anton Chekhov). (Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology".)
  - 1908: When the Russian Orthodox Church excommunicated Leo Tolstoy, Markov asked the Church to expel him also. The request was granted. (Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology".)
  - 1913: Organized the *Ars Conjectandi* bicentenary commemoration in deliberate counterpoint to the Romanov 300-year tercentenary. (Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology"; Link 2006 *Traces* p.324, more cautiously.)
  - 1921: Complained to the Academy that he could not attend meetings for lack of suitable footwear; Maxim Gorky chaired the committee that found him a pair, which Markov dismissed as "stupidly stitched." (Hayes 2013 "The Bootless Academician".)
  - On Nekrasov's work: "an abuse of mathematics." (Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology"; primary source is Markov-Chuprov correspondence in Ondar 1981, not personally accessed.)
  - On the applicability of probability theory: "I am concerned only with questions of pure analysis…. I refer to the question of the applicability of probability theory with indifference." (Markov to Chuprov, quoted in Hayes 2013 "Counting Vowels and Consonants"; primary in Ondar 1981, not personally accessed.)

### Pavel Alekseevich Nekrasov (1853–1924)

- **Affiliation:** Faculty of Moscow University, "then a stronghold of the Russian Orthodox Church." (Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology".)
- **Education:** Began schooling at a theological seminary before turning to mathematics; "apparently he believed the two vocations could support each other." (Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology".)
- **Mathematical-theological argument (1902 paper, not personally accessed):** As Markov interpreted it, Nekrasov claimed that pairwise independence of summands was both necessary and sufficient for the Weak Law of Large Numbers to hold (Seneta 2006 p.3). Hayes paraphrases the popular form: voluntary acts are independent like coin flips; the law of large numbers applies only to such independent events; data gathered by social scientists (e.g., crime statistics) conform to the law of large numbers; therefore the underlying acts of individuals must be independent and voluntary — i.e., free will is mathematically demonstrable. (Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology".)
- **Reception:** Markov treated Nekrasov as the immediate target of his 1906 counterexample. The Markov-Chuprov correspondence preserves "fiery post-cards" from late 1910 after Chuprov mentioned Nekrasov in a positive light. (Seneta 2006 p.3.)
- **Death:** 1924. "Not surprisingly, the royalist Nekrasov had a hard time hanging onto his position after the 1917 Bolshevik revolution. He died in 1924, and his work fell into obscurity." (Hayes 2013 "The Bootless Academician".)

## Supporting Figures

### Pafnuty Lvovich Chebyshev (1821–1894)

- Markov's mentor at St. Petersburg University. Made foundational contributions to number theory, methods of approximation (Chebyshev polynomials), and probability theory; published a broader proof of the law of large numbers than Bernoulli's. Retired 1883; died 1894. (Hayes 2013 "Peterburgian Math"; Seneta 2006 pp.1–2.)
- Used in this chapter only as a one-paragraph framing of Markov's mathematical inheritance.

### Aleksandr Aleksandrovich Chuprov (1874–1926)

- St. Petersburg statistician with whom Markov maintained a long correspondence (preserved in Ondar 1981). The "fiery post-cards" of late 1910 about Nekrasov, and the "indifferent to applicability" letter, both come from this correspondence. (Seneta 2006 p.3; Hayes 2013 "Mathematical Theology" / "Counting Vowels and Consonants".)
- Used as the second-hand carrier of two anchored Markov sentences. Not a chapter protagonist; appears in passing.

### Aleksandr Sergeyevich Pushkin (1799–1837)

- Russian national writer. Author of *Eugene Onegin*, the verse novel whose first 20,000 letters Markov used as his test set in 1913. Banished from St. Petersburg in 1820 because of political poetry; began *Onegin* in exile (Odessa, then his mother's country seat at Mikhaylovskoe). (Link 2006 *Traces* p.325.)
- Used only as the source of the test set; not a chapter protagonist. *Eugene Onegin*'s first chapter and sixteen stanzas of the second supply the 20,000 letters.

### Sergei Timofeevich Aksakov (1791–1859)

- Russian author of *Childhood Years of Bagrov's Grandson* (1858), the autobiographical memoir whose first 100,000 letters Markov analyzed in the appendix to the 4th edition of his *Calculus of Probability* (1924, posthumous). Aksakov dictated the memoir to his daughter because he had been blind for ten years. (Link 2006 *Traces* p.325.)
- Used only as a one-line "Markov did this twice" anchor for Scene 4.

### Maurice Fréchet (1878–1973)

- French mathematician whose 1938 *Méthode des fonctions arbitraires. Théorie des événements en chaîne dans le cas d'un nombre fini d'états possibles* (Paris, Gauthier-Villars) is the work Shannon 1948 footnote 6 cites for "detailed treatment" of Markoff processes. The bibliographic bridge between Markov and Shannon. (Shannon 1948 §5 footnote 6.)
- Used only as the citation route by which Shannon reached Markov's mathematics without citing Markov's papers directly.

### Claude Elwood Shannon (1916–2001)

- Bell Labs mathematician and engineer. Author of "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" (*Bell System Technical Journal* 27, July & October 1948, pp. 379–423, 623–656). §3 of that paper applies Markoff-process modeling to English text in a sequence of n-gram approximations; §4 generalizes the Markoff-process framework. Shannon does not cite Markov directly; his only reference for "detailed treatment" of Markoff processes is Fréchet 1938. (Shannon 1948 §3, §4, §4 footnote 6.)
- Used only as Scene 4's recipient of the Markoff-process concept. Shannon's full information theory is out of scope here.

### Morris Halle (1923–2018)

- MIT linguist who in 1955 produced the first English translation of Markov 1913, at the request of colleagues interested in statistical approaches to language. The translation was never published; it survived only in mimeograph form in a few libraries until the David Link et al. 2006 published translation. (Hayes 2013 "The Bootless Academician".)
- Used only to anchor the 33-year delay between the *Onegin* paper's existence in Russian and its accessibility to Western readers (and the further 51 years before formal publication).

### David Link

- Cologne-based scholar at the Centre for Art and Media Technology, Karlsruhe; later Academy of Arts and the Media, Cologne. Translator (with A. Y. Nitussov, L. Voropai, and G. Custance) of the 2006 first widely available English edition of Markov 1913 in *Science in Context* 19(4):591–600. Author of two companion 2006 essays on Markov's mathematization of writing and the transmission of his ideas to Western Europe. (Markov 1913 (Link translation) cover page; Link 2006 *Traces*; Link 2006 *Chains to the West*.)
- Used as the secondary translator-historian whose page-anchored work makes Markov 1913 accessible at all.

## Out of Scope (Mentioned in Sources But Not Drafted)

- **Andrei Andreevich Markov Jr. (1903–1979):** Markov's son. Distinguished Soviet mathematician of mathematical logic at Moscow State University; wrote a biography of his father in the Soviet political idiom that "paints P. A. Nekrasov in a very negative way" (Seneta 2006 p.4 and on the biography). Mentioned only to disambiguate authorship; no chapter scene.
- **Maxim Gorky (1868–1936):** Russian writer whose 1902 election to the Academy was vetoed by the Tsar (provoking Markov's protest) and who in 1921 chaired the committee that found Markov a pair of boots. Chapter aside, not protagonist.
- **Leo Tolstoy (1828–1910):** Excommunicated by the Russian Orthodox Church in 1908; Markov asked to be excommunicated also. Chapter aside, not protagonist.
- **Eugene Seneta, Brian Hayes, Oscar Sheynin, Daniel Crevier-equivalent secondary figures:** Cited in `sources.md` as authors of secondaries, not as chapter actors.
- **Jakob Bernoulli (1654–1705), Pierre-Simon Laplace, Adolphe Quetelet, Nikolai Lobachevsky:** Mentioned only in the one-paragraph mathematical-lineage framing in Scene 1; no scene action.

## Naming and Transliteration

The Russian-name landscape in the secondary literature varies by transliteration system. This chapter follows:

- "Andrei Andreevich Markov" (BGN/PCGN, used by *Science in Context*) — preferred.
- "Andrey Andreyevich Markov" (alternative; used by Link 2006 *Traces*).
- "A. A. Markoff" (older French/German transliteration; appears on the 1910 *Acta Mathematica* paper) — historical only.
- "Pavel Alekseevich Nekrasov" — preferred.
- "P. A. Nekrasov" — abbreviated form, used by Seneta 2006.

When prose introduces a Russian name for the first time, use the full BGN/PCGN form; thereafter use the surname.
