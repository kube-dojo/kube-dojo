# Brief: Chapter 4 — The Statistical Roots

## Thesis

The statistical lineage that runs from Markov's 1906 chain-dependence proof, through his 1913 *Eugene Onegin* analysis, to Claude Shannon's 1948 information theory is best understood not as a discovery of "predictive text" but as the slow institutional acceptance that the law of large numbers can apply to dependent events. Markov's motivation was not linguistic; it was a mathematical correction to Pavel Nekrasov's 1902 claim that statistical regularity in social data implied independent — and therefore voluntary — individual acts. The 1913 lecture put 20,000 vowels and consonants from Pushkin under the law of large numbers to *demonstrate* a theorem he had already proved on purely formal grounds in 1906. Forty-two years later, Shannon adopted "Markoff processes" as the mathematical model of an information source — by then the genus name, not a citation of Markov's papers — and ran second-, third-, and word-level n-gram approximations to English. The statistical roots of language modeling were genuine, but the path from Markov's pencil-and-paper Pushkin tables to anything that could be called text generation took a Cold War institutional infrastructure that did not yet exist when Markov died in 1922.

## Scope

- IN SCOPE: Pavel Nekrasov's 1902 free-will-and-the-law-of-large-numbers argument as Markov interpreted it; Markov's 1906 Kazan Bulletin paper extending the WLLN to a 2-state chain as a deliberate counterexample; the 1906–1912 series of follow-up papers that generalized the proof "purely theoretically" (Link 2006); the 23 January 1913 (o.s.) lecture at the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg, framed by Markov as the bicentenary of Bernoulli's *Ars Conjectandi*; Markov's exact methodology on the first 20,000 letters of *Eugene Onegin* — the 200×100 partition, the paired-column counting, the arithmetic mean of 43.19 vowels per 100 letters, the first-order transition probabilities (p1 = 0.128, p0 = 0.663), and the second-order chain (p1,1 = 0.104, q0,0 = 0.132); Markov's later analysis of 100,000 letters from Aksakov (1924); Shannon 1948's adoption of "Markoff processes" and his worked second- and third-order English approximations; Shannon's footnote 6 routing readers to Fréchet 1938 rather than to Markov directly; the Halle 1955 mimeographed and Link 2006 published English translations as the *Onegin* paper's belated arrival in Western mathematics.
- OUT OF SCOPE: Shannon's full information theory (entropy, channel capacity, coding) — touched only as the receiving frame for the Markoff-process model; PageRank, hidden Markov models, MCMC, modern n-gram language models — explicitly forward-references only; any claim that Markov "foresaw" modern language models or LLMs; the pre-Markov history of probability theory (Bernoulli, Laplace, Quetelet, Chebyshev) — used only as a one-paragraph lineage; the Russian Revolution and its effects on Markov's later years beyond what Hayes 2013 carries.

## Boundary Contract

This chapter must not present Markov as having anticipated text generation, language models, or modern AI. He did not. His own letter to Chuprov is explicit: "I am concerned only with questions of pure analysis…. I refer to the question of the applicability of probability theory with indifference" (Hayes 2013, "Counting Vowels and Consonants"). Pushkin was a *test set* for the WLLN-on-dependent-events theorem, not a target application. The chapter must also not present Shannon 1948 as a citation-level reception of Markov 1913. Shannon footnote 6 sends readers to Fréchet 1938 for the "detailed treatment" of Markoff processes; he uses "Markoff process" as the established term for a stochastic process, the same way an engineer in 1948 would use "Fourier transform." The conceptual lineage is real; the bibliographic lineage is indirect.

The chapter must not invent dialogue or interior states for any participant. Markov's letters to Chuprov are quoted in Hayes from Ondar 1981; treat any sentence in quotation marks as a single Hayes-anchored anchor, not a primary access. Nekrasov's 1902 paper has not been read directly; everything attributed to Nekrasov is via Markov's interpretation as paraphrased by Seneta 2006 and Hayes 2013. The scene must say so when the Nekrasov argument enters.

The chapter is a statistical-roots chapter, not a panoramic one. It runs 1902–1948, with most of the weight on 1906 and 1913. It is short by design.

## Scenes Outline

1. **The Theological Provocation (1902–1905).** Pavel Nekrasov, a mathematician at Moscow University with a theological-seminary background, publishes an argument: voluntary acts are independent like coin flips; the law of large numbers applies only to independent events; crime statistics conform to the law of large numbers; therefore individual human acts must be voluntary — free will is mathematically demonstrable. From St. Petersburg, the 49-year-old Andrei Markov reads the argument as a misuse of probability theory and calls Nekrasov's work "an abuse of mathematics."
2. **The 1906 Counterexample.** Markov, retired as Emeritus Professor of St. Petersburg University but still teaching the probability course he inherited from Chebyshev, writes a paper for the Kazan *Izvestiia* extending the Weak Law of Large Numbers to a two-state chain in which all four transition probabilities lie strictly between zero and one. The paper is a counterexample to Nekrasov, and the start of what would become a six-year, eight-paper purely theoretical series. Markov refuses on principle to look for empirical applications — Link 2006 *Chains to the West* is explicit that "in those six years, Markov could not think of a practical illustration… or was not interested in finding one."
3. **The Bernoulli Bicentenary Lecture (23 January 1913).** While Russia at large prepares to celebrate three hundred years of Romanov rule, Markov organizes a counter-anniversary at the Imperial Academy of Sciences in St. Petersburg: the bicentenary of Bernoulli's *Ars Conjectandi* (1713). His lecture finally provides an empirical illustration of the theorem he proved in 1906. The first 20,000 letters of Pushkin's *Eugene Onegin* — the entire first chapter and sixteen stanzas of the second — become a test set. He writes them out in 200 tables of ten rows by ten columns, hand-counts every vowel, and computes the unconditional probability of a vowel as 0.432. He then computes the first-order transition probabilities (a vowel follows a consonant 66.3% of the time; a consonant follows a vowel 87.2% of the time) and the second-order ones (after two vowels in a row, the probability of another vowel collapses to 10.4%). The empirical dispersion coefficient (0.208) matches the second-order theoretical model (0.195) better than the first-order one — the chain of dependence is real.
4. **The Forty-Year Silence (1913–1948).** Markov's *Onegin* paper is published in the *Bulletin of the Imperial Academy of Sciences of St. Petersburg* in Russian only. Markov dies in 1922; Nekrasov in 1924. Nekrasov's work falls into post-revolutionary obscurity. Markov's chains do not. They travel west through Fréchet's *Méthode des fonctions arbitraires* (Paris, 1938), through Kolmogorov's foundations of probability, and through the term-of-art "Markoff process" itself. In 1948 Claude Shannon publishes "A Mathematical Theory of Communication" with §3 "The Series of Approximations to English" — running second-, third-, and word-level n-gram approximations to English text — and §4 "Graphical Representation of a Markoff Process." Footnote 6 sends readers to Fréchet, not to Markov. Shannon does not appear to have read the *Onegin* paper; Morris Halle will translate it into English in 1955, but the translation goes unpublished and survives only in mimeograph in a handful of libraries until 2006.
5. **The Honest Close.** What lay between Markov's pencil-and-paper Pushkin tables and any practical use of n-gram models was four decades and an institutional infrastructure that did not yet exist: published English translations of Russian probability work, a Cold War theory of communication that needed a stochastic source, and digital storage capable of carrying transition matrices for any vocabulary larger than two states. Markov did not foresee text generation. He proved a theorem against a theological misuse of mathematics, gave it a literary demonstration, and walked away. The chain of transmission to 21st-century language models is real but indirect — and that indirection is itself the chapter's point.

## Conflict Notes

- **Markov's retirement year:** 1905 (Seneta 2006 p.2) is the correct year for his retirement from St. Petersburg University as Emeritus Professor; he remained Academician at the Imperial Academy of Sciences. Hayes 2013's "had already retired by 1906" is consistent with this. Use 1905 in prose.
- **The Onegin pair counts:** Markov 1913 p.596 uses 11,361 (one fewer than 11,362) for the digram denominator because the first letter has no predecessor. Hayes 2013 uses 11,362 as the unconditional consonant count. Both are correct in their own contexts. In prose, use 11,362 for the unconditional count and only invoke 11,361 if explaining the digram method in detail.
- **Nekrasov's 1902 argument:** The chapter relies on Hayes's full paraphrase plus Seneta's caution that this was "what Markov interpreted as a claim." Frame the scene as Markov's reading; do not write a Nekrasov-perspective scene without primary access.
- **Shannon's debt to Markov:** Direct citation is absent. Shannon's footnote 6 routes readers to Fréchet 1938, not Markov 1906/1913. The conceptual debt is real (Fréchet builds on Markov–Kolmogorov machinery) but the reception is via the term-of-art. Scene 4 must say so.
- **The Romanov-vs-Bernoulli framing of the 1913 lecture:** Hayes 2013 ("Mathematical Theology") frames Markov's choice of *Ars Conjectandi* bicentenary as deliberate counterpoint to the Romanov tercentenary; Link 2006 *Traces* p.324 is more cautious, noting only "this gave the physical-mathematical faculty's event a political dimension." Use Link's hedge in prose; flag Hayes's stronger reading as one historian's interpretation.
