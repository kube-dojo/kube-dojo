# Timeline: Chapter 22 - The Lisp Machine Bubble

## Anchored Chronology

- **Mid-1970s:** Large interactive Lisp systems at MIT and elsewhere press
  against PDP-10 time-sharing, address-space, response-time, and swapping
  limits. Gabriel/Steele later describe hardware options for Lisp as bleak by
  the end of the decade.
- **1974:** Symbolics' 1983 history says the MIT AI Lab initiated the Lisp
  Machine project to build a personal networked computer optimized for Lisp and
  symbolic software.
- **1975:** Symbolics' history says software development for the Lisp Machine
  system had been ongoing since 1975.
- **1976:** Symbolics' history says CONS, the first implementation of the Lisp
  Machine architecture, was running.
- **Early 1977:** AIM-444 says the prototype gained memory, disk, and terminal
  support; the software system had editor/compiler/disk paging/keyboard/mouse
  and PDP-10 file I/O working.
- **August 1977:** MIT AI Memo 444 publishes the Lisp Machine progress report,
  documenting the time-sharing problem, personal-computer answer, CONS status,
  LUNAR conversion, and plan for seven additional machines.
- **1978:** Symbolics' history says CADR replaced CONS as the improved
  Lisp-machine implementation.
- **May 1979:** MIT AI Memo 528 documents CADR as the processor at the center of
  the Lisp Machine project, with writable microcode, stack support, and virtual
  memory.
- **1980:** Symbolics formed. Gabriel/Steele describe LMI and Symbolics emerging
  from MIT commercialization disagreements, both initially making CADR clones.
- **1981:** Symbolics introduced LM-2, described in its 1983 summary as a CADR
  repackaged for reliability and serviceability.
- **April 1981:** ARPA called the Lisp Community Meeting, where diverging Lisp
  communities helped trigger the Common Lisp standardization effort.
- **July 1981:** The fourth edition of the Lisp Machine Manual documents
  Zetalisp and the operating-system-like parts of the Lisp Machine environment.
- **1981-1982:** LMI and Symbolics joined the Common Lisp effort while still
  focused commercially on Lisp machines.
- **1979-1982:** Symbolics says research and engineering on the 3600 occurred
  during this period.
- **February 1983:** Symbolics publishes the 3600 Technical Summary, pitching a
  36-bit single-user symbolic workstation with integrated Lisp environment,
  graphics, networking, and development tools.
- **Late 1980s:** Gabriel/Steele later describe general-purpose hardware and
  stock-hardware Lisp implementations as competing effectively with Lisp
  machines.
