# Open Questions: Chapter 22 - The Lisp Machine Bubble

## Must Resolve Before Prose

- Should the Symbolics 3600 Technical Summary remain Green for product
  specifications while being explicitly barred from market-success claims? The
  current contract says yes, but reviewers should confirm the vendor-source
  boundary.
- Does the chapter need a verified independent business source for Symbolics/LMI
  finances, layoffs, bankruptcy, or sales trajectory? Without one, the chapter
  should explain the bubble through technical/economic pressure and avoid exact
  market claims.
- Should Dan Weinreb's archived "What happened to Symbolics?" be added as a
  Yellow participant-memory source for failure causes, or would that over-bias
  the chapter toward one postmortem?
- Should Stallman's GNU/Lisp account be used at all? If yes, it must be framed
  as partisan memory about software-sharing conflict, not as neutral history.
- Are the Common Lisp sections proportioned correctly? The current plan gives
  it 450-600 words as portability pressure, not as a full language-history
  chapter.

## Stretch Unlocks

- Verified business source for Symbolics revenue, headcount, customer count,
  IPO/financing, or bankruptcy chronology.
- Independent source on TI Explorer, Xerox Lisp machines, or other competitors
  if the chapter needs a wider market map. Current core does not require it.
- Parsed Dan Weinreb account with exact anchors and explicit memory-source
  caveats.
- Parsed Stallman account with exact anchors and balanced framing.

## Guardrails

- Do not fabricate market numbers.
- Do not treat vendor application lists as adoption data.
- Do not call Lisp machines "failed toys"; they were highly capable systems
  whose economics became vulnerable.
- Do not let the LMI/Symbolics split consume the chapter without verified
  sources.
- Do not use Wikipedia as a prose citation, only discovery and sanity checking.
