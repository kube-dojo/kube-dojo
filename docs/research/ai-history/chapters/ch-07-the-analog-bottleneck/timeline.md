# Timeline: Chapter 7 — The Analog Bottleneck

All dates here trace to a verified anchor in `sources.md`. Yellow / Red entries are flagged inline.

## 1910

- **19 February 1910.** William Grey Walter born in Kansas City, Missouri. — Wikipedia / SpringerLink biographical entry, cross-confirmed via WebFetch 2026-04-28. Background detail; not a load-bearing chapter date.

## 1939

- **Walter joins the Burden Neurological Institute (BNI) in Bristol.** He holds a position at the BNI from 1939 until 1970. — Holland 2003, p. 2086 (BNI affiliation summarised); Wikipedia biographical entry, cross-confirmed 2026-04-28. **Green** for the 1939 anchor and the BNI institutional detail.

## 1948 – 1949

- **Walter designs and builds the first two tortoises, Elmer and Elsie, and works out the behavioural-interpretation framework.** Holland 2003 p. 2087 is unambiguous: "the design of the tortoises, and the interpretation of their behaviour | was carried out in 1948 and 1949." **Green.**
- **December 1949.** *Daily Express* article by Chapman Pincher: "Elmer and Elsie | they see, feel, move and even feed." — Cited in Holland 2003 reference list, p. 2120 as Pincher 1949. **Green** for the bibliographic anchor; **Yellow** for any direct quotation, pending direct *Daily Express* archive access.

## 1950

- **March 1950.** *Picture Post* article "New Light on the Brain" by D. W. Morley, pp. 18–23. — Cited in Holland 2003 reference list, p. 2120. **Green** for the bibliographic anchor.
- **May 1950.** Walter, "An Imitation of Life," *Scientific American* vol. 182 no. 5, pp. 42–45. The first scientific publication on the tortoises. — Walter 1950 p. 42 (article title page); Holland 2003 p. 2089. **Green.**
- **1950 (date provisional within the year).** BBC television newsreel film "Bristol's robot tortoises have minds of their own." — Holland 2003 p. 2090 reproduces narration: "dress them up to look like men | he calls them tortoises..." **Green** for the existence and approximate dating of the newsreel; **Yellow** for any specific month within 1950, pending BBC archive access.

## 1951

- **Early 1951.** Six improved tortoises engineered for Walter by W. J. "Bunny" Warren of the BNI engineering team. Elmer and Elsie appear to have been scrapped at about this time. — Holland 2003 p. 2092. **Green** for the engineering team and the scrapping; **Yellow** for the precise month.
- **January 1951 (approximate).** CORA / *Machina docilis* operational; demonstrated at the Paris Cybernetics Convention. — cyberneticzoo.com summary, cross-confirmed 2026-04-28. **Yellow** for the convention demonstration date pending verification against the convention proceedings.
- **August 1951.** Walter, "A Machine That Learns," *Scientific American* vol. 185 no. 2, pp. 60–63. Introduces CORA and the conditioned-reflex-analogue learning circuit. — Holland 2003 p. 2120 reference list; verbatim quotation chain at Holland 2003 pp. 2112–2113 with explicit "Walter (1951, p. 62)" cite. **Green** for the publication; **Yellow** for direct quotation pending open-access PDF.
- **1951.** Festival of Britain. Tortoises demanded for demonstration. — Holland 2003 p. 2092. **Green** for the Festival demand; **Yellow** for the specific demonstration dates within the Festival.

## 1952

- **4–15 January 1952.** John von Neumann delivers five lectures titled *Probabilistic Logics and the Synthesis of Reliable Organisms from Unreliable Components* at the California Institute of Technology. Notes taken by R. S. Pierce. — Von Neumann 1952 IAS PDF cover page and §1 p. 1. **Green.**

## 1953

- **1953.** Walter, *The Living Brain* (London: Duckworth). The major book-length treatment of the tortoise project (one chapter), of CORA, and of Walter's design philosophy in the Appendix. — Holland 2003 p. 2092 (the 1953 publication); Holland 2003 p. 2095 (chained quotation from p. 280 of the book). **Green** for the publication; **Yellow** for direct page anchors beyond those Holland 2003 chains.

## 1956

- **1956.** Von Neumann's Caltech lectures published in C. E. Shannon and J. McCarthy (eds.), *Automata Studies*, Annals of Mathematics Studies No. 34 (Princeton University Press), pp. 43–98. — SciRP reference index, cross-confirmed 2026-04-28. **Green** for the bibliographic citation; the chapter's anchored page numbers are against the IAS PDF reprint of the Caltech typescript, not against the *Automata Studies* publication itself.
- **1956.** P. de Latil, *La pensée artificielle* (Paris: Gallimard); English translation (1956) as *Thinking by Machine* (Sidgwick and Jackson). Contains the only extended interview with Walter on the tortoises that survives outside the BNI archives. — Holland 2003 reference list, p. 2120; quotations on Holland 2003 pp. 2092–2093. **Green** for the publication; **Yellow** for any direct de Latil quotation that the chapter wants to use beyond what Holland 2003 has already verified.

## 1968

- **1961, 1963, 1968.** *The Living Brain* reissued in paperback (1961) and reprinted (1963, 1968). — Holland 2003 p. 2092. Useful only as evidence that the tortoise framing remained in popular circulation through the 1960s.

## 1970

- **1970.** Walter is gravely injured in a motor-scooter accident with a runaway horse; he eventually regains consciousness but is no longer able to work effectively. — Holland 2003 p. 2089. **Green.**

## 1977

- **6 May 1977.** W. Grey Walter dies in Clifton, Bristol. — Wikipedia biographical entry, cross-confirmed 2026-04-28. Background detail; not a load-bearing chapter date.

## 2000 – 2001

- **2000.** One of the surviving 1951 tortoises goes on display at the London Science Museum. — Holland 2003 p. 2092. **Green.**
- **2001.** A second surviving 1951 tortoise goes on display at the MIT Museum. — Holland 2003 p. 2092. **Green.**

## Dates the chapter must NOT assert without further anchoring

- The exact date(s) of the BBC newsreel within 1950.
- The specific Festival of Britain demonstration date(s) in 1951.
- Any claim about von Neumann's awareness of Walter's tortoises (or Walter's awareness of von Neumann's lectures) at the time of writing — there is no anchored evidence either way.
- The exact disposition date of Elmer and Elsie themselves (Holland 2003 says only that they "appear to have been scrapped at about this time," meaning around early 1951).
