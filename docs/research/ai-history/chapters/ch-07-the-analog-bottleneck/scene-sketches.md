# Scene Sketches: Chapter 7 — The Analog Bottleneck

Five scenes, mapped 1:1 to the Prose Capacity Plan layers in `brief.md`. Each scene names its anchored sources by page.

## Scene 1: An Imitation of Life — what readers actually saw (May 1950)

Maps to Prose Capacity Plan layer "700–1,000 words: 'An Imitation of Life' — what the May 1950 paper actually said."

- **Setting.** The pages of *Scientific American* vol. 182 no. 5, May 1950, pp. 42–45. Eight stylised sketches by the artist Bernarda Bryson Shahn (Holland 2003, p. 2089) thread through the article, showing the tortoise in box, with light in box, with two lights in box, with kennel, with low obstacle, with mirror, and with a second tortoise.
- **Action.** Walter opens with a Lewis Carroll epigraph from the Mock Turtle scene. He distinguishes the cybernetic "scientific imitation of life" from sorcerer's-image magic by noting that the latter "is content to copy external appearance" while the former "is concerned more with performance and behavior" (Walter 1950, p. 42, verbatim). He names the *Machina speculatrix* genus, names Elmer and Elsie ("ELectro MEchanical Robots, Light-Sensitive, with Internal and External stability"), and gives the hardware list in one short paragraph (Walter 1950, p. 42).
- **The behaviours, in order, as the article presents them.** Cycloidal exploration in the dark (Walter 1950, p. 43); attraction to a moderately bright light source through photocell amplification (p. 43); the "abrupt shear away" from a strong light, "a flashlight about six inches away" (p. 43); the Buridan's-ass solution between two light sources, "a complex path of advance and withdrawal" (p. 43); the kennel with its 20-watt lamp, the auto-disconnect during charging, the post-charge repulsion from the same kennel light (p. 43); the obstacle-avoidance multivibrator (p. 44); the indicator light and the mirror-flicker (p. 45); the two-tortoise interaction "leading finally to a stately retreat" (p. 45); the "dog-in-the-manger" three-body case where a casual interferer prevents a battery-low tortoise from reaching its charger, "leading to the more needy one expiring from exhaustion within sight of succor" (p. 45).
- **What this scene must NOT do.** Inflate Walter's hedged "were it an animal a biologist would be justified in attributing to it a capacity for self-recognition" (p. 45) into a claim about consciousness. Walter's prose is restrained throughout. The chapter's prose must match.
- **Anchored sources.** Walter 1950 pp. 42–45 (every claim above). Holland 2003 p. 2089 (the Bernarda Bryson Shahn attribution and the article framing). **Green.**

## Scene 2: Inside the shell — the actual hardware

Maps to Prose Capacity Plan layer "600–900 words: Inside the shell — the actual hardware."

- **Setting.** Bristol, the Burden Neurological Institute, 1948 or 1949. Walter's home study turned into a workshop (Holland 2003 p. 2094 references "the home study was turned into a workshop"). Holland 2003 reproduces a labelled BNI archival diagram of "Elsie with her shell removed" (Figure 3) and the original 1951-batch circuit diagram (Figure 4).
- **Action.** A precise circuit walkthrough that is *not* what Walter 1950 gave the popular audience. The two miniature thermionic valves (radio tubes) are arranged as a two-stage amplifier. The photoelectric cell, fitted with a shroud that limits its angular field, sits on the same vertical spindle as the front drive wheel, so that the eye is always looking in the direction the tortoise is moving. Voltage from the photocell controls the first-stage current; that current controls the second-stage current; the second-stage current drives two relay coils RL1 and RL2 (Holland 2003 pp. 2099–2100).
- **The four behaviour patterns.** E (exploration) — drive motor at half speed, steering motor at full speed, cycloidal trajectory, photocell scanning. P (positive tropism) — moderate light, drive at full, steering off, oriented motion toward the light. N (negative tropism) — strong light, "abruptly sheers away" (Walter 1950 p. 43). O (obstacle-avoidance) — shell deflected, ring contact closes, two-stage amplifier becomes a multivibrator, relays oscillate, "all stimuli are ignored and its gait is transformed into a succession of butts, withdrawals and sidesteps" (Walter 1950 p. 44, verbatim). The E / P / N / O typology is Walter's own letters from his unpublished BNI typescript (Walter 1960 *Notes on operation*) preserved by Holland (2003 pp. 2100–2102) and not in the original *Sci. Am.* article.
- **The point this scene carries.** The tortoises are not pure analog continuity. They are a relay-mediated finite-state machine driven by a continuous-amplifier sensor. The "analog versus digital" framings the chapter must hedge against are too coarse to describe what is actually inside the shell.
- **Anchored sources.** Walter 1950 p. 42 (hardware list), p. 43 (scanning, P), p. 44 (multivibrator obstacle-avoidance). Holland 2003 pp. 2098–2102 (full circuit walkthrough; behaviour-pattern letters). **Green.**

## Scene 3: Hardware is the program

Maps to Prose Capacity Plan layer "700–1,000 words: Hardware is the program."

- **Setting.** Walter's *The Living Brain* (London: Duckworth, 1953), Appendix C; reachable in this contract via Holland 2003's chained quotations.
- **Action.** Walter's three-conditions argument for what counts as a legitimate model: "1. Several features of the mystery must be known. 2. The model must contain the absolute minimum of working parts to reproduce the known features. 3. The model must reproduce other features, either as predictions, or as unexpected combinations." (Walter 1953 p. 280 verbatim, via Holland 2003 p. 2095.) The further passage on the electric model of nerve: "The model is simply the analogue of one set of familiar mathematical expressions relating to passive networks linked by a nonlinear operator in the form of a discharge tube. It could quite well be formed of chemical or mechanical parts and does not in theory contain more information than do the algebraic equations." (Walter 1953 pp. 284–286 verbatim, via Holland 2003 pp. 2095–2096.)
- **The hinge.** Walter's defence of minimalism — six elements, "a new pattern every tenth of a second for 280 years — four times the human lifetime of 70 years" (Walter 1950 p. 43 verbatim) — is *also* the description of the wall the approach runs into when behaviour must be revisable. The combinatorial richness is fixed by the wiring; revising the wiring is rebuilding the machine. The chapter's bottleneck claim is right here, at the hinge between Walter's defence of small models and the architectural cost of behaviour-bound-to-wiring.
- **The fragility evidence.** Elmer and Elsie were "cobbled together from wartime surplus components and scrap materials | for example, their gearwheels were salvaged from old gas meters and alarm clocks" and "in consequence chronically unreliable" (Holland 2003 p. 2092 verbatim). The 1951 demand for demonstrations including the Festival of Britain forced W. J. "Bunny" Warren and the BNI engineering team to build six improved tortoises (Holland 2003 p. 2092). The point: the prototype build was already failing as engineering before the conceptual ceiling was articulated.
- **What this scene must NOT do.** Frame the fragility as a moral fault of the design. Walter himself never apologised for the wartime-surplus build — it was a research instrument, not a product. The bottleneck the chapter is naming is conceptual ("hardware is the program"), not engineering-quality.
- **Anchored sources.** Walter 1953 p. 280 (three conditions, via Holland 2003 p. 2095 chained quotation). Walter 1953 pp. 284–286 (the "model is simply the analogue" passage, via Holland 2003 pp. 2095–2096). Walter 1950 p. 43 (280-years argument). Holland 2003 p. 2092 (Elmer / Elsie fragility; 1951 batch by Warren). **Green via chained anchor for Walter 1953; direct Green for Walter 1950 and Holland 2003.**

## Scene 4: Von Neumann's Caltech calculus (January 1952)

Maps to Prose Capacity Plan layer "800–1,100 words: Von Neumann's Caltech calculus."

- **Setting.** Five lectures at the California Institute of Technology, January 4 to January 15, 1952. R. S. Pierce takes notes; von Neumann revises the notes for circulation. The lectures' eventual published form is in *Automata Studies* (Princeton, 1956), pp. 43–98 — but the chapter's anchored quotations are against the IAS PDF reprint of the original Caltech typescript, which uses different page numbers.
- **The framing argument (IAS PDF p. 1, §1).** "The subject-matter is the role of error in logics, or in the physical implementation: of logics — in automata-synthesis. Error is viewed, therefore, not as an extraneous and misdirected or misdirecting accident, but as an essential part of the process under consideration — its importance in the synthesis of automata being fully comparable to that one of the factor which is normally considered, the intended and correct logical structure." (Verbatim.)
- **The technical setup (IAS PDF pp. 22–37, §§7–10 introduction).** Von Neumann assumes basic components — Sheffer-stroke organs (NAND gates) and majority organs — that fail with some probability ε per operation. He shows that with ε ≥ 1/6 even a basic organ in the most favourable configuration cannot be improved by any composition (IAS PDF p. 30, §8 cap). He then introduces multiplexing in §9: each line in the network is replaced by N parallel lines, and the state is interpreted by majority vote.
- **The worked example (IAS PDF pp. 45–46, §10.5.3.1).** "Consider first a computing machine with 2500 vacuum tubes each of which is actuated on the average once every 5 microseconds. Assume that a mean free path of 8 hours between errors is desired. In this period of time there will have been 1.4 × 10¹³ actuations, hence the above specification calls for δ ≈ 1/(1.4 × 10¹³) = 7 × 10⁻¹⁴. According to the above table this calls for an N between 10,000 and 20,000 — interpolating linearly on −10 log δ gives N = 14,000. I.e. the system should be multiplexed 14,000 times." (Verbatim.)
- **The verdict (IAS PDF p. 47, §11.1).** "The multiplexing technique is impractical on the level of present technology, but quite practical for a perfectly conceivable more advanced technology and for the natural relay organs (neurons). I.e. it merely calls for a, not at all unnatural, micro-componentry." (Verbatim.)
- **What this scene must NOT do.** Read von Neumann's verdict as "vacuum tubes are doomed" or "we must abandon analog for digital." Von Neumann is explicit: the verdict is about *the multiplexing fix on 1952 component technology*. The "more advanced technology" he names — micro-componentry on the scale of neurons — was the conceptual horizon he was already gesturing toward. The chapter's "scaling wall" thesis must be built from this exact text, not from a paraphrase that claims more.
- **Anchored sources.** Von Neumann 1952, IAS PDF p. 1 (framing), p. 37 (§10), pp. 45–46 (§10.5.3.1), p. 47 (§11.1). **Green.**

## Scene 5: The bridge, not the funeral (honest close)

Maps to Prose Capacity Plan layer "300–500 words: Honest close — the bridge, not the funeral."

- **Setting.** The penultimate paragraph of Walter 1950 itself, on p. 45: "More complex models that we are now constructing have memory circuits in which associations are stored as electric oscillations, so the creatures can learn simple tricks, forget them slowly and relearn more quickly. This compact, plastic and easily accessible form of short-term memory may be very similar to the way in which the brain establishes the simpler and more evanescent conditioned reflexes." (Verbatim.) Walter has *already*, in May 1950, named his next project — the work that became CORA / *Machina docilis* in the August 1951 *Scientific American* paper.
- **The Pavlovian conditioning protocol Walter 1951 describes** (via Holland 2003 pp. 2112–2113 chained verbatim quotation at "Walter 1951, p. 62"): "the specific stimulus is a moderate light and the neutral one is the sound of a whistle. The whistle is blown just before the light is seen; after this has been repeated 10 or 20 times the model has 'learned' that the sound means light and will come to the whistle as though it were a light... In another arrangement the specific stimulus is touch, that is, an encounter with an obstacle. In that case the whistle is blown just as the model comes into contact with the obstacle, so that after a while the warning whistle triggers a withdrawal and avoidance reaction. This process may of course be accelerated by formal education: instead of waiting for the creature to hit a natural obstacle the experimenter can blow the whistle and kick the model. After a dozen kicks the model will know that a whistle means trouble, and it can thus be guided away from danger by its master."
- **The 1951 batch and the museum dispositions.** Six improved tortoises by W. J. "Bunny" Warren in early 1951; one in the London Science Museum (since 2000), one at the MIT Museum (since 2001) (Holland 2003 p. 2092).
- **The hand-off to Ch8.** One sentence, one forward-reference, no anticipation of Ch8's argument. "The architectural escape route — separating *what* a machine does from *how* its parts are wired together — was being worked out in parallel at the IAS, and is the subject of the next chapter." That is all the chapter is allowed to say about the stored-program transition. The case for stored-program architecture belongs to Ch8.
- **What this scene must NOT do.** Stage the tortoise project as defeated. Walter himself never staged it that way (Holland 2003 p. 2088 documents his 174-paper bibliography and his continued clinical EEG work through 1970). The tortoise project was one chapter of one book and a handful of papers; calling it "the end of the analog dream" is the legacy chapter's framing, and it is not anchored in any source the chapter is using.
- **Anchored sources.** Walter 1950 p. 45 (forward-look at memory circuits). Walter 1951 p. 62 (CORA whistle-and-touch protocol, via Holland 2003 pp. 2112–2113 chained quotation). Holland 2003 pp. 2092 (museum dispositions), 2110–2114 (CORA / IRMA section). **Green for Walter 1950 p. 45 and Holland 2003 anchors; Green via chained anchor for Walter 1951 p. 62.**
