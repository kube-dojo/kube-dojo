## Verified Protagonists

### W. Grey Walter (1910 – 1977)

- **Born** 19 February 1910 in Kansas City, Missouri. **Died** 6 May 1977 in Clifton, Bristol, United Kingdom. (Wikipedia biographical entry, cross-confirmed via WebFetch 2026-04-28.)
- **Position.** Neurophysiologist at the Burden Neurological Institute (BNI) in Bristol from 1939 until 1970, when he was injured in a motor-scooter accident. Holland 2003 p. 2086 (BNI affiliation) and Holland 2003 p. 2089 (1970 accident).
- **Affiliation as printed in Walter 1950.** "W. Grey Walter is director of the physiological department at the Burden Neurological Institute in Bristol, England." (Walter 1950, p. 45, verbatim.) **Green.**
- **Primary scientific reputation.** Electroencephalography. The tortoise project was a small fraction of his lifetime output: "a single chapter in *The Living Brain*, and a handful of papers amongst the 174 recorded under his authorship in the BNI bibliography" (Holland 2003, p. 2088). **Green.**
- **Public reputation.** "Maverick" / "swashbuckling" — Hayward 2001b p. 616 (verbatim, via Holland 2003 p. 2088 chained quotation). **Green via chained anchor**, since Hayward 2001 itself is paywalled.
- **Authorship of his books.** Holland 2003 p. 2088, citing N. Walter 1990, notes that both of Walter's books were "actually written by his father from his notes and conversations." This is a footnoted observation, not a load-bearing claim; the chapter may mention it as background colour, not as a structural detail. **Green** for the existence of the observation; **Yellow** for any chapter prose that turns this into a thesis about authorship credit.
- **Tortoise design and behavioural-interpretation work** carried out 1948–1949. Holland 2003 p. 2087. **Green.**
- **Wartime and political activities.** Walter was a member of the Peace Pledge Union in the 1930s; an officer in the Bristol Home Guard during the war; a leading member of the Bristol branch of the Association of Scientific Workers; a frequent guest on the BBC Brains Trust during the 1950s and 1960s. — Holland 2003 p. 2088 (citing N. Walter 1990 verbatim). **Green** for the BBC framing; **Yellow** for any prose that uses these biographical details as load-bearing context for the tortoise project — they are colour, not causal.

### John von Neumann (1903 – 1957)

- **Position at the time of the Caltech lectures.** Professor at the Institute for Advanced Study, Princeton. — Von Neumann 1952 IAS PDF cover page (verbatim: "PROFESSOR J. von NEUMANN / The Institute for Advanced Study / Princeton, N. J."). **Green.**
- **Lectures delivered.** Five lectures, *Probabilistic Logics and the Synthesis of Reliable Organisms from Unreliable Components*, California Institute of Technology, January 4–15, 1952. — Von Neumann 1952 IAS PDF cover page. **Green.**
- **Acknowledgements in the published lectures.** Von Neumann thanked K. A. Brückner and M. Gell-Mann (then at the University of Illinois) for "important stimuli on this subject" in 1951; R. S. Pierce for the lecture notes; and Caltech for the invitation. — Von Neumann 1952 IAS PDF p. 1 (§1 verbatim). **Green.**
- **Out of scope for this chapter.** Von Neumann's 1945 *First Draft of a Report on the EDVAC*, his work at the IAS computer project, and his stored-program advocacy belong to Ch8. The chapter must reference von Neumann only through the 1952 Caltech lectures; any biographical or institutional detail beyond what is in those lectures must be hedged accordingly.

## Verified Supporting Cast

### R. S. Pierce

- **Role.** Note-taker for von Neumann's January 1952 Caltech lectures. The published text is "based on notes taken by R. S. Pierce on five lectures given by the author at the California Institute of Technology in January 1952" (von Neumann 1952 IAS PDF p. 1, verbatim). The lectures were "revised by the author, but they reflect, apart from stylistic changes, the lectures as they were delivered" (same passage). **Green.**
- The chapter should name Pierce explicitly as the note-taker when describing the provenance of the published lectures; collapsing him into "von Neumann's own write-up" is an overclaim.

### W. J. "Bunny" Warren

- **Role.** BNI engineer, recruited by Walter after the war. Built the 1951 batch of six improved tortoises. — Holland 2003 p. 2092 (verbatim). **Green.**
- The chapter's "scaling fragility" prose can name Warren and his BNI engineering team as the people who actually solved the chronic unreliability of Elmer and Elsie's wartime-surplus build. This is a useful anti-romance corrective to the "lone inventor" framing.

### Vivian Dovey Walter

- **Role.** Grey Walter's wife at the time of the tortoise project. Appears in the de Latil 1956 photograph captioned: "In their country home near Bristol, these parents have two children: one is electronic. Vivian Dovey and Grey Walter have two offspring: Timothy, a human baby and Elsie, the tortoise, of coils and electronic valves." — de Latil 1956, via Holland 2003 p. 2093 chained quotation. **Green for the photograph caption**; **Yellow for any biographical detail beyond name and presence in the household.** The chapter should not develop Vivian Dovey Walter as a character — she is colour, not a load-bearing actor.

## Mentioned in Sources but Out of Scope for This Chapter

- **Norbert Wiener (1894–1964).** Walter's view of Wiener was lukewarm: in a 1947 letter to Professor Adrian, Walter wrote that Wiener "represents quite a large group in the States, including McCulloch and Rosenblueth. These people are thinking on very much the same lines as Kenneth Craik did, but with much less sparkle and humour." (Walter 1947 letter, BNI Papers, via Holland 2003 p. 2094 chained quotation.) The chapter may mention this in one sentence as context for the British cybernetic tradition's independence from Wiener's school, but Wiener's broader work belongs to Ch6.
- **Kenneth Craik.** Cambridge psychologist. Walter credits Craik as the originator of the "synthetic method" of using physical models to test biological hypotheses, and as the source of the "first notion of constructing a free goal-seeking mechanism." (Walter 1953 *The Living Brain* p. 125, via Holland 2003 p. 2094 chained quotation.) The chapter may carry one sentence on Craik as background for Walter's design philosophy. **Green via chained anchor.**
- **Ross Ashby.** Walter 1950 p. 42 names "the 'homeostat' made by W. R. Ashby of Gloucester, England" as "the prototype of these" stationary cybernetic devices, in the paragraph just before Walter introduces *Machina speculatrix*. The chapter may use this as the immediate preceding context within Walter's own framing, not as a structural anchor. **Green.**
- **Walter B. Cannon.** Harvard physiologist credited by Walter 1950 p. 42 with coining "homeostasis." Background only.
- **Pamela McCorduck**, **John McCarthy**, **Pavlov**, **The Ratio Club** members (Bates, Barlow, Mackay, Woodward, Turing, Good, Uttley): mentioned in Holland 2003 but out of scope for this chapter; cross-references belong to Ch5, Ch6, Ch10, Ch11 as appropriate.
- **R. Cooper, J. Bird** — authors of the BNI institutional history (Cooper & Bird 1989) cited by Holland 2003. Useful only as a tertiary reference.
- **Bernarda Bryson Shahn.** Artist who drew the eight stylised sketches that illustrate Walter 1950. Holland 2003 p. 2089 attributes the sketches to her by name. The chapter may name her if it discusses the iconography of the original *Sci. Am.* article. **Green** for the attribution.
