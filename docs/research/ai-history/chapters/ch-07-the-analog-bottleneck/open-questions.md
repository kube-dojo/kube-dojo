# Open Questions: Chapter 7 — The Analog Bottleneck

Each entry names a Yellow or Red status in `sources.md`, what archival access would close the gap, and whether closing it is required for prose drafting to begin.

## Q1. Direct page anchors against Walter 1953, *The Living Brain*

- **Status.** Yellow. The chapter currently chains its `Walter 1953 p. 280` and `Walter 1953 pp. 284–286` anchors through Holland 2003's verbatim quotations on Holland 2003 pp. 2095–2096. The Internet Archive scan at `archive.org/details/livingbrain00walt` is borrow-only (LCP-Encrypted PDF; DjVu text gated behind authenticated borrowing).
- **What would close it.** Either an Internet Archive borrower account that exports the page-image scan; an institutional library copy of the 1953 Duckworth or 1961 Penguin edition; or a Codex archival-helper run that fetches the page via Google Books snippets cross-checked against Holland 2003's quotations.
- **Required for prose drafting?** No. Holland 2003's chained anchors are publication-grade; the chapter can draft from them. Promotion to direct anchors would tighten the citation chain but is not load-bearing.

## Q2. Direct page anchors against the 1956 *Automata Studies* publication of von Neumann's lectures

- **Status.** Yellow. The chapter's anchored quotations against §1, §10, §10.5.3.1, and §11.1 are against the IAS PDF reprint of the original Caltech typescript, which uses *different page numbers* from the 1956 *Automata Studies* publication (where the paper occupies pp. 43–98).
- **What would close it.** Either a copy of *Automata Studies* (Princeton, 1956) — available in many university libraries — or a careful collation between the IAS PDF reprint's section structure and the 1956 publication's pagination. The latter is doable algorithmically given that the IAS reprint preserves von Neumann's section numbering.
- **Required for prose drafting?** No. The chapter must cite the IAS PDF page numbers when quoting verbatim. It may *also* mention the *Automata Studies* publication (and the pp. 43–98 range) in the citation, because that range is bibliographically verified, but it must not anchor to specific pages within that range until they are independently checked.

## Q3. Walter 1951 *A Machine That Learns* in open access

- **Status.** Yellow. The original *Scientific American* 185(2) PDF was not located in open-access form during this anchor-hunt. The verbatim quotation chain via Holland 2003 pp. 2112–2113, with Holland's explicit "Walter (1951, p. 62)" cite, is the strongest available anchor.
- **What would close it.** A scan of *Scientific American* 185(2) at the August 1951 issue — likely available at the *Scientific American* archive (paywalled), the BNI archive, or a research library with the bound *Sci. Am.* run.
- **Required for prose drafting?** No. CORA properly belongs to the chapter's honest close (Scene 5), which is a 300–500-word layer; the chained Holland 2003 anchor is sufficient for that load.

## Q4. Hayward 2001 *Sci. Context* 14(4) p. 616 verification beyond the chained anchor

- **Status.** Yellow. PubMed bibliographic record verified. Article body paywalled at Cambridge Core. Holland 2003 p. 2088 quotes Hayward 2001b at p. 616 verbatim, so the chained anchor is reachable for the "swashbuckling / maverick" framing.
- **What would close it.** Cambridge Core institutional access; a research-library copy of *Science in Context* vol. 14; or a Codex archival-helper run via JSTOR or ProQuest.
- **Required for prose drafting?** No. The Walter-as-maverick framing is biographical colour, not a load-bearing claim; the chained anchor is sufficient.

## Q5. The BNI archives and Walter's unpublished typescripts

- **Status.** Holland 2003 makes heavy use of the BNI archive (now held in part at the Wellcome Collection in London and curated by Cooper & Bird 1989). Specifically, Holland 2003 quotes from:
  - Walter 1947 letter to Professor Adrian (Holland 2003 p. 2094).
  - Walter 1948 typescript "The enchanted loom" (BBC Third Programme broadcast, 23 April 1948).
  - Walter 1953b typescript "Accomplishments of an artefact" (provisional date and attribution).
  - Walter 1957 lecture summary at the Graduate Center for Psychotherapy, New York.
  - Walter 1960 typescript "Notes on operation: Machina speculatrix" (Holland 2003 pp. 2100–2102 quotes large portions verbatim; Holland 1996 published a slightly shortened version, Holland 2003 the full text).
  - Walter 1967 typescript "William Grey Walter, his Trumpet Voluntary."
  - Walter 1968 typescript "The future of Machina speculatrix."
- **What would close any specific archival quotation.** Direct consultation of the BNI Papers at the Science Museum / Wellcome Collection, or a Codex archival-helper handoff for specific items.
- **Required for prose drafting?** No, for any anchor Holland 2003 already quotes verbatim (those become chained Greens). Yes, if the chapter wants to use Walter 1948 *Enchanted loom*, Walter 1968 *Future of Machina speculatrix*, or any BNI typescript not already in Holland 2003's published quotations.

## Q6. The Newell-Simon precedent question (cross-chapter)

- **Status.** Out of scope for Ch7. Listed here only so that the question is not invented later: did Walter's 1948–1949 tortoise work influence the later 1950s symbolic-AI program (Newell-Simon 1956 Logic Theorist; the 1956 Dartmouth conference)? Holland 2003's bibliography contains no reference to the Dartmouth conference, the Logic Theorist, or McCarthy. The chapter must not claim a causal arrow from Walter to Dartmouth without external sourcing — and the answer is almost certainly that the influence ran *through* Wiener and the cybernetics movement (Ch6) rather than through Walter directly.
- **Required for prose drafting?** No. The chapter must hold the boundary contract: Walter's tortoises are a Bristol-and-BNI story, not a Dartmouth or RAND story.

## Q7. Did Walter and von Neumann correspond or meet?

- **Status.** Red. No anchored evidence either way. Walter 1947 letter to Adrian (via Holland 2003 p. 2094) describes a Wiener visit but does not mention von Neumann. Holland 2003 reference list contains no von-Neumann-related items.
- **What would close it.** The IAS archives (von Neumann's correspondence) and the BNI archives (Walter's correspondence). Both are out of reach for this anchor-hunt.
- **Required for prose drafting?** No, and arguably should *not* be drafted: the chapter's structure pairs Walter 1948–1950 with von Neumann 1952 as parallel diagnoses of the same component-reliability problem, not as collaborators. Asserting collaboration without anchored evidence would invent a relationship.

## Q8. Cost / power figures for Elmer and Elsie

- **Status.** Red. No anchored figure for the cost of the wartime-surplus build, the power consumption of the two-stage amplifier in operation, the lifetime of the storage battery between charges, or any other infrastructure-cost number. Holland 2003 documents the *fragility* of the build (p. 2092) but not the cost.
- **What would close it.** BNI procurement records (BNI archives); the *Daily Express* / *Picture Post* / Pincher 1949 contemporary press coverage might have ballpark figures.
- **Required for prose drafting?** No. The chapter must not invent power-grid analogies (cf. the legacy chapter's "skyscraper, a city's power grid" framing — not anchored). The chapter can use von Neumann's worked example for the *quantitative* scaling argument and Walter / Holland for the *qualitative* fragility argument.

## Q9. The "transition to digital" thesis as a defensible framing

- **Status.** Yellow. The legacy chapter's framing — that vacuum-tube unreliability "drove the field toward digital architectures where logic was separated from wiring" — is not directly anchored in Walter 1950 or in von Neumann 1952. The chapter's honest close in `brief.md` reframes this as "hardware-as-program ran into a quantitative reliability ceiling, and the architectural escape route was stored-program programmability (Ch8), not digital per se."
- **What would close it.** A direct Boden 2006 *Mind as Machine* page anchor on the analog-versus-digital framing; a direct Edwards 1996 *The Closed World* page anchor on the institutional context; or a tertiary citation against Russell & Norvig 4th ed. §1.3 with an anchored page.
- **Required for prose drafting?** No. The chapter's reframed honest-close thesis is defensible from the primary sources alone. Reviewers will likely flag this; the boundary contract in `brief.md` is designed to absorb that flag.

## Q10. The "what counts as 'the field' moving on" question

- **Status.** Yellow / interpretive. Walter himself did not move on — he kept building cybernetic models through 1968 (Holland 2003 p. 2114). What changed was institutional: ARPA funding (Ch16), the Dartmouth naming (Ch11), and the symbolic-AI program (Ch12, Ch13) all routed around the cybernetic tradition rather than building on it.
- **What would close it.** Cross-chapter coherence; this is properly settled at the Part-2 / Part-3 boundary, not within Ch7.
- **Required for prose drafting?** No. The chapter must keep its forward-references sparse and not reach for the institutional-shift argument.
