# Brief: Chapter 7 - The Analog Bottleneck

## Thesis
Grey Walter’s electronic tortoises proved that complex, life-like behavior could emerge from simple analog circuits. However, the physical reality of vacuum tubes posed a severe scaling constraint that drove the field toward digital architectures where logic was separated from wiring.

## Scope
- IN SCOPE: W. Grey Walter, *Machina speculatrix* (tortoises Elmer and Elsie), his 1950 and 1951 papers, analog circuit behavior, physical constraints of vacuum tubes, von Neumann's 1952 analysis of unreliable components.
- OUT OF SCOPE: Digital architectures.

## Scenes Outline
1. **An Imitation of Life:** Walter builds the tortoises to demonstrate emergent biological behavior, as detailed in his 1950 paper.
2. **The Illusion of Intent:** The tortoises navigate and interact using only a light sensor, bump sensor, and two vacuum tubes (anchored to circuit diagrams).
3. **The Scaling Wall:** Von Neumann's 1952 quantitative analysis of the failure rates of vacuum tubes.
4. **The End of the Analog Dream:** The practical limitations of soldering hardware vs. programming software, paving the way for digital logic.
