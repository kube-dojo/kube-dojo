## Conflict Notes

- **First-Macy-meeting date** — Galison gives "8 and 9 March 1946" (p. 254); Pias gives "first conference, March 1946" (p. 11) without day-level precision. Both can be true; use Galison's day-level anchor in prose.
- **AA-predictor performance** — Galison shows Wiener's statistical predictor barely matched and sometimes lost to Bode's simpler geometrical methods (p. 244). Some secondary popular accounts (e.g., textbook AI histories) frame the predictor as a triumph because of the Stibitz "uncanny" reaction; the chapter must hold both points and not let the dramatic Stibitz quotation eclipse the December 1942 failure.
- **Cybernetics' "displacement" by AI** — the secondary literature is split. Kline 2015 frames it as institutional / funding-driven; Pickering 2010 frames it as ideological / philosophical-style-driven. Wiener 1961 Preface treats it as natural maturation. The chapter's stance: cybernetics' analog substrate, narrow programmability, and Wiener's own pacifist withdrawal from military funding all contributed; do not pin the displacement to a single cause. This is a Yellow-supported synthesis.
- **Whether the Macy Conferences "founded" anything** — the conferences produced no shared definition of intelligence, no single program, and no joint mathematical theory. They produced a vocabulary (feedback, information, control) and a set of cross-disciplinary friendships. The chapter must avoid the founding-myth framing in either direction (cybernetics-founded-AI or cybernetics-died-with-the-Macy-meetings).
