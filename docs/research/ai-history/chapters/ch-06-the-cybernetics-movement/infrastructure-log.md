# Infrastructure Log: Chapter 6 — The Cybernetics Movement

Technical and institutional metrics relevant to the chapter's infrastructure-history thesis: cybernetics was an *analog-substrate* research program with task-specific machines, modest funding, and an institutional infrastructure (Macy meetings) shaped by McCulloch's interdisciplinary parity policy and Fremont-Smith's standard-format running. Each row is an operational fact that the chapter's prose can land on.

| Construction (Part A) | Four interconnected units, each carrying a pivoted magnet on top whose angular deviation from center forms one of four main variables. Each unit also has ancillary coils, potentiometers, water trough with electrodes at each end providing a potential gradient. | **Green** — Ashby 1960 *Design for a Brain* §8/2 pp. 100-101; Ashby 1956 §5/14 pp. 83-84. |
| Construction (Part B) | A relay (energized or not) and four 25-position stepping-switches. Each switch position carries a resistor of some value. Total Part B states: 2 × 25⁴ = 781,250. | **Green** — Ashby 1956 §5/14 p. 83. |
| Coupling rule | The relay is non-energized when and only when A is stable at the central positions. With the relay energized, none of B's states is equilibrial (switches keep moving); with the relay not energized, all are equilibrial (switches stay where they are). | **Green** — Ashby 1956 §5/14 p. 83. |
| Operation | When perturbed, B's stepping switches scan random configurations until A finds central equilibrium under that B configuration; B then stops and "remembers" by holding the switch positions. | **Green** — Ashby 1956 §5/14 p. 83-84. |
| Ashby's claim | The Homeostat demonstrates that an electromechanical system can find equilibrium configurations by searching part-function-rich state spaces — a model for adaptive behavior in organism-environment couplings. | **Green** — Ashby 1956 §5/14 + Ashby Macy 1952 talk in Pias 2003 p. 593-598. |
| Ashby's careful disclaimer | The adjective "homeostatic" derives from Cannon's prior coinage "homeostasis," not from Ashby's machine. | **Green** — Ashby 1960 *Design for a Brain* §8/2 footnote, p. 100. |

## Operational Limits Worth Naming in Prose

These are the infrastructure constraints the chapter should foreground when explaining why cybernetics, despite its institutional infrastructure and intellectual ambition, did not converge on a programmable thinking-machine project:

- **The AA predictor was task-specific.** The light-spot-tracking apparatus was wired for the specific problem of curve extrapolation under aerial-evasion conditions. Reprogramming it to play chess or solve calculus would have required not new code (there was no code) but a different set of analog circuits. The physical structure *was* the program.
- **The Homeostat was task-specific.** Part B's stepping switches with 781,250 possible configurations searched only in the space of internal-coupling parameters — they could not be reprogrammed to control a different external variable space. Ashby explicitly notes that the machine demonstrates "ultrastability," not flexible computation.
- **The Macy meetings produced no shared programming-language analogue.** The conferences predate LISP (McCarthy 1958-1960) and FORTRAN (1957). When attendees discussed feedback systems they discussed circuit diagrams, block-flow descriptions, and transcript-quoted reasoning — not code. There was no medium for collaborative programming, and there was no expectation that there should be.
- **Closed-group format limited diffusion.** McCulloch's strict disciplinary parity meant that the Macy meetings were closed to most outside requests for participation (Pias p. 13). The published transcripts only began with the sixth conference (1949); the foundational early conferences (1946-1948) left only correspondence and McCulloch's organizational notes. The chapter's account of conferences 1-5 is necessarily reconstructed from secondary materials.
- **Wiener's pacifist withdrawal removed cybernetics' weapons-funding ladder.** Wiener's October 1945 letters to de Santillana and Compton signaled his refusal to do further classified weapons work; his 1947 *Atlantic* article made it public. This was a real institutional cost: the post-1945 ARPA-style funding pipeline that Ch11 documents (for symbolic AI) ran around Wiener, not through him. Cybernetics' funding-of-record stayed Macy + NSF + private foundations + UK research-council support (Ashby).

## Notes

- Several "infrastructure facts" frequently repeated in tertiary AI-history sources (e.g., "cybernetics was killed by the digital computer") collapse into a more nuanced multi-cause picture once the primary record is examined: analog-substrate task-specificity, Wiener's voluntary military withdrawal, McCulloch's interdisciplinary closed-group format, and the eventual symbolic-AI capture of the "thinking machine" question via the 1956 Dartmouth Proposal (Ch11) all played roles. The chapter must not collapse them into a single cause.
- The infrastructure-log claims should stay narrowly *operational*: who funded what, when, where, with what hardware, with what people, what got produced. Interpretive synthesis (cybernetics' analog limits as a *philosophical* commitment vs. as a *technological* contingency) belongs in `brief.md` *Conflict Notes*, not here.
