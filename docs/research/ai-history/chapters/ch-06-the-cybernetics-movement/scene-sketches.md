## Scene 1 — The Anti-Aircraft Predictor (1940-1943)

Anchored layer: *700-1,100 words: The AA-predictor as institutional and engineering artifact* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Norbert Wiener writes to Vannevar Bush on 20 September 1940 offering to find some "corner of activity" in which he could be of use during the emergency. Britain is under aerial attack; "The Day of the Eagle" was 13 August 1940. | Galison 1994 p. 228 (Wiener letter); p. 229 (Battle of Britain context) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Wiener proposes anti-aircraft fire control: extrapolate where the plane *will be*, not where it *is*. Bush's differential analyzer at MIT becomes the simulation testbed. November 1940: Wiener tests four trajectory types (straight-line, doubled slope, parabolic, semicircle-integral). | Galison 1994 p. 234 (citing Caldwell NDRC proposal 1940); Wiener Cybernetics 1961 Introduction p. 6 (general framing) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Wiener hires Julian Bigelow, MIT-trained electrical engineer, in January 1941. The first-year budget is $2,325. The scale is infinitesimal compared to the Radiation Laboratory or Los Alamos. | Galison 1994 p. 234 (Bigelow hire); p. 235 (budget) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Summer 1941: Wiener-Bigelow build the simulation hardware. A light-spot projector throws an irregular bouncing white spot on a laboratory wall; an operator with a deliberately sluggish control stick — Bigelow, an active pilot, designed the stick "to have the 'feel' of an actual [airplane] control" — guides a second colored spot in pursuit. The data is the input to the predictor circuits. | Galison 1994 pp. 235-243 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| 1 July 1942: George Stibitz of Bell Labs visits the lab and writes in his diary that Wiener-Bigelow's predictor "accomplishes miracles" for ~2-second leads (equivalent to ~10 seconds in real-time, given the time-compression scheme). Warren Weaver "threatens to bring along a hack saw on the next visit and cut through the legs of the table to see if they do not have some hidden wires somewhere." | Galison 1994 pp. 243-244 (quoting Stibitz "D" papers) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| December 1942: Wiener compiles for Weaver a hits-comparison table on tracking data tracks 303 and 304 from the Anti-Aircraft Board at Camp Davis NC. Hendrik Bode at Bell Laboratories had developed two simpler geometric predictors. Track 303: Bode-simple 6 hits, Bode-10-second 22, Wiener-statistical 23. Track 304: Bode-simple 35, Bode-10-second 55, Wiener-statistical 49. Wiener's method is at most marginally better and on track 304 is *worse* than Bode's geometric method. | Galison 1994 p. 244 (citing Wiener letter to Weaver 15 Jan 1943, OSRD Report 1863, NA-LC) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. The chapter's pivot beat: as a weapon, the project failed. |
| Early 1943: Wiener writes to Weaver: "I still wish that I had been able to produce something to kill a few of the enemy instead merely of showing how not to try to kill them." | Galison 1994 p. 245 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |

**Scene-level note:** This scene must not lapse into hagiography. Wiener's predictor was a near-miss whose engineering yield was beaten by a simpler geometric extrapolator. The chapter's job is to show *why* a project that failed as a weapon was nonetheless productive intellectually — the conceptual yield (the 1943 paper, the 1948 book, the Macy synthesis) came from re-casting the predictor's behavioristic substrate as a general theory of purpose. The Stibitz "uncanny" beat is real and quotable, but must not eclipse the December 1942 hits-comparison verdict.

## Scene 2 — Behavior, Purpose, Teleology (1943)

Anchored layer: *600-900 words: The 1943 paper as the cybernetic redefinition of purpose* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Wiener and Bigelow approach Rosenblueth with a specific clinical question: is there any pathological condition in which a patient, trying to perform a voluntary act like picking up a glass of water, overshoots the mark and goes into uncontrollable oscillation? Rosenblueth answers immediately: yes, this is "purpose tremor," associated with cerebellar injury. The cerebellar patient *behaves* like a feedback machine with insufficient damping. | Wiener Cybernetics 1961 Introduction p. 7-8 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The 1943 paper "Behavior, Purpose and Teleology" appears in *Philosophy of Science* 10(1), pp. 18-24. Authors: Rosenblueth, Wiener, Bigelow. The journal venue is itself notable — this is a philosophy-of-science journal, not an engineering or physiology journal. | Direct paper extraction; Wiener Cybernetics 1961 p. 8 footnote | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The paper builds a behaviorist taxonomy: behavior is active or passive (energy source); active behavior is purposeful or random; purposeful behavior is feedback or non-feedback; feedback behavior is predictive or non-predictive; predictive behavior has orders (1st-order = cat chasing mouse; 2nd-order = stone at a moving target; higher-order = sling, bow). | Rosenblueth, Wiener & Bigelow 1943, pp. 18-21 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The paper's key move: redefine teleology so it stops being a metaphysical embarrassment. "Teleological behavior thus becomes synonymous with behavior controlled by negative feedback." A torpedo with a target-seeking mechanism is intrinsically purposeful; the engineering term "servo-mechanism" is precisely the right name. | Rosenblueth, Wiener & Bigelow 1943, pp. 19, 23 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The cerebellar-disease analogy in the paper's text: "If [the cerebellar patient] is asked to carry a glass of water from a table to his mouth, however, the hand carrying the glass will execute a series of oscillatory motions of increasing amplitude as the glass approaches his mouth, so that the water will spill and the purpose will not be fulfilled." The authors "venture to suggest that the main function of the cerebellum is the control of the feed-back nervous mechanisms involved in purposeful motor activity." | Rosenblueth, Wiener & Bigelow 1943, pp. 21-22 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Galison's interpretive frame: the 1943 paper is a re-cast of the AA-predictor's behaviorist substrate. The pilot under flak does not have free will in any operational sense; he behaves "like a servo-mechanism, attempting to overcome the intrinsic lag due to the dynamics of his plane." The same vocabulary that the AA project used to characterize the pilot becomes, in 1943, the vocabulary that characterizes purposeful behavior generally. | Galison 1994 pp. 245-246 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |

**Scene-level note:** The chapter must not present this paper as the founding of cybernetics; the term "cybernetics" itself does not appear. The paper is the *behaviorist* statement that cybernetics would later organize itself around. Frame as: a dense 7-page paper that re-defined purpose in terms of negative feedback, written by a mathematician, an engineer, and a physiologist who needed each other's vocabulary to make the argument work.

## Scene 3 — The Macy Conferences (1946-1953)

Anchored layer: *900-1,400 words: The Macy Conferences as institutional infrastructure* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| 1942: At a New York meeting on central inhibition under the Josiah Macy Foundation, Rosenblueth disseminates the Wiener-Bigelow ideas. Warren McCulloch attends and is interested. This is the institutional bridge between the AA project's classified channels and the open interdisciplinary world. | Wiener Cybernetics 1961 Introduction p. 12 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Late winter 1943-1944: Wiener and von Neumann convene a joint meeting at Princeton. Engineers, physiologists, mathematicians. McCulloch and Lorente de Nó (physiologists), Goldstine (computing-machine designer), Pitts and Wiener and von Neumann (mathematicians). At the end of the meeting, "it had become clear to all that there was a substantial common basis of ideas... that some attempt should be made to achieve a common vocabulary." | Wiener Cybernetics 1961 Introduction p. 15 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Spring 1946: McCulloch arranges with the Macy Foundation for the first cybernetics-themed conference. Frank Fremont-Smith — "Mr. Interdisciplinary Conference," already running standard-format closed two-day Macy meetings on other medical topics — applies his standard format to the new subject. | Wiener Cybernetics 1961 Introduction p. 17; Pias 2003 p. 11 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| 8-9 March 1946: First Macy conference on circular causality, at the Beekman Hotel on Park Avenue, New York. Sponsored by the Josiah Macy, Jr. Foundation, organized by Frank Fremont-Smith, moderated by Warren S. McCulloch. The original conference series title is "Circular Causal and Feedback Mechanisms in Biological and Social Systems." | Galison 1994 p. 254 (date); Pias 2003 pp. 11-13 (sponsor, organizer, moderator, venue, title) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| 20 September 1946: Second Macy meeting on Teleological Mechanisms in Society. Bateson is a co-organizer. A third meeting, "Feedback Mechanisms and Circular Causal Systems in Biology and the Social Sciences," follows; invitees include Lazarsfeld, Mead, Northrop. | Galison 1994 p. 254 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Macy meeting format: closed group, ~20 attendees, two days, all-day informal papers + discussions + meals together. McCulloch insists on disciplinary parity ("three mathematicians plus three physiologists plus three psychiatrists plus three sociologists plus three psychologists"); recommendations rejected if they would break the parity. The conferences remain closed to most outside requests. | Wiener Cybernetics 1961 Introduction p. 18 (size and format); Pias 2003 p. 13 (parity policy) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Russell, Einstein, and Turing decline their invitations. Russell on grounds of post-trip fatigue from a 1953 American "jaunt." Einstein with the dismissive remark that cybernetics is just "applied mathematics" — a useful tool for "specialists" but his own knowledge of it is too "superficial" to contribute. Turing on grounds of being "a stay-at-home type," a new academic semester, and likely confidentiality concerns. | Pias 2003 pp. 13-14 (citing letters in McCulloch Papers, APS) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The cybernetic synthesis Pias identifies has three pillars, all formulated in the early 1940s: (i) Pitts and McCulloch's 1943 logical calculus of nervous activity; (ii) Shannon's 1948 information theory; (iii) the Wiener-Bigelow-Rosenblueth 1943 behavioral theory of feedback. A universal theory of digital machines, a stochastic theory of the symbolic, and a non-deterministic yet teleological theory of feedback combined to claim validity for living organisms, machines, economic processes, sociological phenomena, and aesthetic phenomena alike. | Pias 2003 p. 15 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| 1948: Wiener publishes *Cybernetics: Or Control and Communication in the Animal and the Machine*. The term — "from the Greek κυβερνήτης or steersman" — was coined by Wiener in summer 1947. The book gives the field a name in print; the Macy series renames itself accordingly as of 1949. | Wiener Cybernetics 1961 Introduction p. 11-12; Pias 2003 p. 11 (renaming) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| 24-25 March 1949: Sixth Macy conference, with Heinz von Foerster as Secretary — the first stenographically recorded conference. The published transactions begin from this meeting. | Pias 2003 p. 15 footnote 24 (sketch of seating at sixth conference, 24-25 March 1949) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Bateson's later valedictory: "I think that cybernetics is the biggest bite out of the fruit of the Tree of Knowledge that mankind has taken in the last 2000 years." | Pias 2003 p. 12 (quoting Bateson "From Versailles to Cybernetics" in *Steps to an Ecology of Mind*, 2nd ed. 1987, p. 481) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |

**Scene-level note:** Avoid over-romanticizing the Macy meetings. They produced no shared definition of intelligence and no joint program. What they produced was a vocabulary, a rolling cohort of cross-disciplinary friendships, and a published record of conferences 6-10 (1949-1953) that — together with McCulloch's organizational notes for conferences 1-5 — is the chapter's primary archival surface. The chapter must distinguish *meetings 1-5 (1946-1948), unrecorded except for agendas and correspondence* from *meetings 6-10 (1949-1953), stenographic*; do not silently treat them as a single uniform body.

## Scene 4 — The Homeostat (1948-1952)

Anchored layer: *600-900 words: The Homeostat as cybernetic machine* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| W. Ross Ashby — Director of Research at Barnwood House, Gloucester — pre-figures the Homeostat in his 1947 *Mind* paper "The Nervous System as Physical Machine," arguing that adaptive behavior is the property of a physical system that finds new equilibria under disturbance. | Yellow — Ashby Digital Archive bibliography (web.archive.org snapshot 2026-04-28); article PDF not pulled. | Yellow |
| December 1948: Ashby publishes "Design for a Brain" in *Electronic Engineering* 20:379-383 — the first published description of the Homeostat. | Ashby Digital Archive bibliography (citation verified 2026-04-28); article PDF not pulled. | Yellow |
| 1952: Ashby publishes the book-length *Design for a Brain* (Chapman & Hall, 1952; second edition with rewritten latter two-thirds, 1960). Chapter 8 of the second edition describes the Homeostat in detail. | Ashby 1960 *Design for a Brain* 2nd ed., Ch. 8, pp. 99-125+. The 1960 edition is fully extractable. | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Construction (Part A): "The Homeostat (Figure 8/2/1) consists of four units, each of which carries on top a pivoted magnet (Figure 8/2/2, M in Figure 8/2/3). The angular deviations of the four magnets from the central positions provide the four main variables." Each unit has a trough of water in front of the magnet with electrodes at each end providing a potential gradient; the magnet carries a wire that picks up a position-dependent potential and sends it to the grid of a triode. Anode-output proportional to magnet deviation. | Ashby 1960 *Design for a Brain* 2nd ed. §8/2 pp. 100-101 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Construction (Part B): A relay (energized or not) plus four 25-position stepping switches, each switch carrying a resistor of some value at each position. Total: 2 × 25⁴ = 781,250 possible Part B states. | Ashby 1956 *An Introduction to Cybernetics* §5/14 p. 83 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Coupling rule: "B has been coupled to A so that the relay is non-energised when and only when A is stable at the central positions." With the relay energized, none of B's states is equilibrial (switches keep moving); with the relay not energized, all are equilibrial (switches stay put). | Ashby 1956 *An Introduction to Cybernetics* §5/14 p. 83 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Operation: when the system is perturbed (e.g., the experimenter manually flips a switch), B's stepping switches scan random configurations until A finds central equilibrium under that B configuration; B then stops and "remembers" by holding the switch positions. The Homeostat finds its way back to ultrastability through search. | Ashby 1956 §5/14 pp. 83-84 (operating principle); Ashby 1960 §8/2-§8/4 pp. 100-110 (worked examples) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Ashby's careful priority claim: "It was given the name of Homeostat for convenience of reference, and the noun seems to be acceptable. The derivatives 'homeostatic' and 'homeostatically', however, are unfortunate, for they suggest reference to the machine, whereas priority demands that they be used only as derivatives of Cannon's 'homeostasis'." | Ashby 1960 *Design for a Brain* 2nd ed. §8/2 footnote, p. 100 | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. The chapter's tightest exhibit of cybernetics' modesty about what its machine actually demonstrated. |
| 1952: Ashby presents "Homeostasis" at the ninth Macy Conference, New York. The discussion includes Wiesner, McCulloch, Bigelow, Pitts, Wiener, and Bavelas. Wiener prompts: "What sort of a randomly connected net is inside the box?" Ashby — wanting to keep the conversation principle-level rather than engineering-level — defers to a discussion of part-functions, full-functions, step-functions, and null-functions in random networks. | Pias 2003 p. 593+ (Ashby talk); pp. 596-619 (transcribed Q&A). | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |

**Scene-level note:** This scene's narrative job is to show that cybernetics had a *real machine* — not a theoretical one. The Homeostat existed, its construction is documented in Ashby's own books, and it was demonstrated to the Macy room in 1952. But its construction is also instructive about cybernetics' analog limits: 781,250 internal Part B states is a lot, but they are all configurations of the same four-magnet equilibrium-finding apparatus. The Homeostat does not "play chess" not because nobody had thought of it, but because there is no way to wire the same machine to do a different task.

## Scene 5 — The Aftermath (1945-1961)

Anchored layer: *400-700 words: The dispersal and the 1961 retrospective* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| 16 October 1945: Two months after Hiroshima and Nagasaki, Wiener writes to philosopher Giorgio de Santillana: "Ever since the atomic bomb fell I have been recovering from an acute attack of conscience as one of the scientists who has been doing war work and who has seen his war work as part of a larger body which is being used in a way of which I do not approve... I have seriously considered the possibility of giving up my scientific productive effort because I know no way to publish without letting my inventions go to the wrong hands." | Galison 1994 p. 253 (citing Wiener letter to de Santillana, NWP box 2 folder 69) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| 18 October 1945: Wiener drafts a letter to MIT President Karl T. Compton announcing his intent to "leave scientific work completely and finally" and to "find some way of living on my farm in the country." He did not in fact leave MIT, but the letter marks his pacifist break. | Galison 1994 p. 254 (citing Wiener letter to Compton, NWP) | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| 1947: Wiener publishes "A Scientist Rebels" in *The Atlantic Monthly*, announcing his refusal to publish in classified military journals or do further classified weapons work. | Galison 1994 p. 254 paraphrases the position. | Yellow — *Atlantic* PDF not pulled in this anchor pass. The substantive claim is supported by Galison's narrative. |
| 1961: Wiener writes the Preface to the second edition of *Cybernetics*. By then, "the role of feedback both in engineering design and in biology has come to be well established. The role of information and the technique of measuring and transmitting information constitute a whole discipline for the engineer, for the physiologist, for the psychologist, and for the sociologist." Wiener notes that the simple-linear-feedback program of the 1948 first edition has been outgrown — "the simple linear feedbacks... now are seen to be far less simple and far less linear than they appeared at first view." | Wiener Cybernetics 1961 Preface to Second Edition pp. vii-viii | **Green** — verified Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The cybernetic synthesis dispersed into its constituent disciplines: information theory absorbed the symbolic side (and through it, eventually communications engineering and machine learning); control engineering absorbed the analog feedback side (servomechanisms, robotics, process control); neurophysiology kept the McCulloch-Pitts substrate (which became, much later, the deep-learning lineage); psychiatry and anthropology kept the social-feedback side (Bateson's later "ecology of mind" work). The "thinking machine" question, which Wiener had framed as the cybernetic synthesis problem, was picked up under a different name in 1956 (Ch11). | Pias 2003 introduction (synthesis framing); Galison 1994 (concluding sections); cross-link to Ch11. | Yellow — synthesis claim. Each component is independently anchored; the umbrella generalization is the chapter's interpretive close. |

**Scene-level note:** This is a transition scene, not a takedown. The chapter must not say cybernetics "lost." It must say cybernetics dispersed into its constituent disciplines, each of which kept its piece, while the "thinking machine" question — which cybernetics had originally framed — moved to the symbolic-computational tradition under the new name "Artificial Intelligence" (Ch11's territory). Tone: matter-of-fact historical correction, not narrative arc-closure.

## Cross-scene notes

- No invented dialogue. Every quoted sentence must come from a primary source: the 1943 paper, Wiener's 1948/1961 *Cybernetics* Introduction, the Pias 2003 transcripts, Galison's quotations from the Wiener Papers and OSRD files, or Ashby's own books.
- Where secondary sources conflict (e.g., on whether cybernetics was displaced by AI for institutional, philosophical, or technological reasons), the chapter should name the disagreement rather than picking a side.
- The "before AI was AI it was cybernetics" framing is incorrect and must not appear. Cybernetics was a contemporary, parallel research program with its own machines, vocabulary, and institutional infrastructure. It was not a precursor.
- The chapter should NOT include a stand-alone explainer of feedback, information theory, or homeostasis as concepts. The 1943 paper text gives the chapter all the conceptual scaffolding it needs; do not lecture on top of it.
