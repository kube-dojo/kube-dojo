# Chapter 3: The Perceptron and the First Hype Cycle

## Thesis
The early neural network movement was inextricably linked to analog, bespoke hardware. Frank Rosenblatt’s Mark I Perceptron was not just an algorithm; it was a 5-ton machine of motors and potentiometers. The "AI Winter" that followed Minsky and Papert's critique was as much an infrastructural failure (the inability to easily scale or simulate multi-layer networks on the digital computers of the era) as it was a mathematical one.

## Scope
- IN SCOPE: Frank Rosenblatt's invention of the Perceptron, the physical construction of the Mark I Perceptron (custom hardware), the media hype cycle (NYT "thinking machine"), and Minsky and Papert's 1969 book *Perceptrons* that devastated neural network funding.
- OUT OF SCOPE: Backpropagation and the 1980s connectionist revival (belongs to Chapter 5/7); early Dartmouth digital logic (belongs to Chapter 2).

## Scenes Outline
1. **The 5-Ton Brain:** Rosenblatt builds the Mark I Perceptron at Cornell Aeronautical Laboratory (1958). It is a custom-built analog machine with a 400-photocell eye and motorized potentiometers for weights. The physical reality of early connectionism: learning required physically adjusting dials.
2. **The First Hype Cycle:** The Navy reveals the Perceptron. The New York Times quotes Rosenblatt saying it will soon "walk, talk, see, write, reproduce itself and be conscious of its existence." The mismatch between analog capability and public expectation.
3. **The XOR Deathblow:** Minsky and Papert publish *Perceptrons* (1969). They mathematically prove single-layer perceptrons cannot solve the XOR problem. More importantly, computing multi-layer networks on 1960s digital mainframes is computationally intractable, making the connectionist paradigm seem like a dead end compared to symbolic logic. The funding dries up.
