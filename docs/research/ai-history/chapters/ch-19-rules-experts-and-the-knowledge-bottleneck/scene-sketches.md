# Scene Sketches: Chapter 19 - Rules, Experts, and the Knowledge Bottleneck

## Scene 1: After General Methods

Open by contrasting Ch18's skepticism about general AI with Stanford's practical
answer: stop asking one method to solve everything and encode the knowledge of
one difficult domain. Use Feigenbaum's knowledge-engineering frame and the
EMYCIN chapter's contrast between general problem solvers and knowledge-based
systems.

## Scene 2: The Consultant in the Terminal

Tell MYCIN's origin as a move from antibiotic-prescription monitoring to
interactive consultation. The medical experts needed therapy advice, not just
diagnosis; the computer system needed enough antimicrobial expertise to be worth
consulting. Use the 1973 grant excerpt for the three goals: consultation,
explanation, and acquisition of judgmental knowledge.

## Scene 3: Rules as Frozen Expertise

Show a clinical judgment becoming an IF/THEN rule without quoting long rule
blocks. Explain why the rule mattered: it was understandable to clinicians,
editable by builders, and usable by a backward-chaining inference engine. This
is the moment where expertise becomes maintainable software infrastructure.

## Scene 4: Uncertainty Without Full Probability

Use certainty factors as a pragmatic compromise. The point is not that MYCIN
invented a better probability theory; the point is that doctors often lacked
complete statistical data and still had to reason with partial, judgmental
evidence.

## Scene 5: The Bottleneck Appears

Stage knowledge acquisition as work: the expert explains, the knowledge
engineer formalizes, the system runs a case, the expert sees a wrong or
surprising consequence, then the rule set is repaired. TEIRESIAS and explanation
features belong here because they turned the system's reasoning into something
the expert could inspect and correct.

## Scene 6: A Strong Result, A Narrow Door

Use the blinded meningitis evaluation as evidence of real accomplishment, then
immediately narrow it: 10 cases, one therapy-selection task, outside evaluators,
small-case limitation. Close with the retrospective's harder lesson: ward
testing never happened, the infectious-disease knowledge base was retired, and
the reusable idea became shells like EMYCIN.

## Scene 7: Handoff to the Boom

End by pointing toward Ch20/Ch21. Expert systems now look economically plausible
because their architecture can be packaged: shell plus domain rules plus expert
interviews. The next chapter(s) should show the machines and corporate setting
that made this architecture scalable enough to sell.
