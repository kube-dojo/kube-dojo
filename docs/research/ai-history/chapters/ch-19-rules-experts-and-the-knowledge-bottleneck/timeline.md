# Timeline: Chapter 19 - Rules, Experts, and the Knowledge Bottleneck

- 1965: Stanford Heuristic Programming Project work begins around DENDRAL,
  according to Feigenbaum's 1977 retrospective.
- Early 1970s: MYCIN grows from Stanford discussions about antimicrobial
  therapy monitoring into an interactive infectious-disease consultation system.
- 1973: MYCIN grant goals describe consultation, interactive explanation, and
  computer acquisition of judgmental therapeutic rules.
- 1975: Shortliffe and Buchanan publish the certainty-factor model for inexact
  medical reasoning in *Mathematical Biosciences*.
- 1976: Shortliffe completes *Computer-Based Medical Consultations: MYCIN*.
- 1977: Feigenbaum's IJCAI invited paper names knowledge engineering as an
  emerging practice and uses PUFF/MYCIN-style systems as case studies.
- 1977-1979: Explanation and TEIRESIAS work develop around WHY/HOW questions
  and interactive transfer of expertise.
- 1978: The MYCIN infectious-disease knowledge base is laid to rest, according
  to the 1984 retrospective.
- 1979: The JAMA evaluation compares MYCIN's antimicrobial advice with human
  prescribers in a blinded meningitis study.
- 1981: EMYCIN is described as a shell/tool for building rule-based
  consultation systems from MYCIN's domain-independent core.
- 1984: Buchanan and Shortliffe publish the full *Rule-Based Expert Systems*
  retrospective.
