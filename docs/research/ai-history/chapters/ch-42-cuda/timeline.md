# Timeline: Chapter 42 - CUDA

- **1999:** NVIDIA introduces GeForce; later secondary reporting says NVIDIA's marketing framed it as a "graphics-processing unit." Anchor: New Yorker lines 95-96.
- **2000:** Ian Buck, then a Stanford graduate student, connects 32 GeForce cards for an 8K Quake display, according to later New Yorker reporting and Buck interview material. Anchor: New Yorker line 98.
- **2003:** Buck and Hanrahan's Stanford tech-report abstract argues that researchers are using graphics hardware for CPU-style work and that a correct abstraction is important; it names Brook as an implementation of a stream processor abstraction. Anchor: Stanford abstract lines 9-13.
- **August 2004:** Buck et al. publish "Brook for GPUs: Stream Computing on Graphics Hardware" in SIGGRAPH/TOG, pp. 777-786. Anchor: HGPU lines 48-75; DOI 10.1145/1186562.1015800.
- **2004/2005:** Buck moves from Stanford/Brook into NVIDIA's CUDA work. NVIDIA's current bio says he joined in 2004; Buck told Tom's Hardware he joined NVIDIA to start CUDA in 2005. Anchor: NVIDIA bio lines 5-8; Tom's Hardware lines 487-488.
- **Summer 2002 to 2006:** NVIDIA's GeForce 8800/G80 architecture is designed over a multi-year period, with goals including high-end floating-point computation and DirectX 10 features. Anchor: NVIDIA technical brief p.26, lines 496-514.
- **November 8, 2006:** Game Developer reports NVIDIA's CUDA architecture announcement alongside the new GeForce 8800. Anchor: Game Developer lines 150-166.
- **November 13-16, 2006:** Other trade outlets carry the CUDA announcement, describing a GPU C-compiler development environment, GeForce 8800 availability, and SDK access for registered developers. Anchors: Military & Aerospace lines 67-87; CGW lines 38-48.
- **2007:** Nickolls et al. later describe CUDA as released by NVIDIA in 2007 and cite CUDA Programming Guide 1.1. Anchor: ACM Queue lines 16 and 120-123.
- **April 2008:** NVIDIA researchers publish "Scalable Parallel Programming with CUDA" in ACM Queue, documenting the programming model, early application examples, and developer uptake. Anchor: ACM Queue lines 6-19, 71-74, 115-119.
- **2008:** The Tesla architecture is described in IEEE Micro and in the ACM Queue sidebar as a unified graphics and computing architecture spanning GeForce 8800, Quadro, Tesla, and mainstream GeForce products. Anchor: ACM Queue sidebar lines 141-165.
- **September 2009:** Buck gives Tom's Hardware a retrospective interview on Stanford GPGPU, Brook, and the start of CUDA. Anchor: Tom's Hardware lines 467-493.
- **2012 and after:** AlexNet and deep learning use CUDA-enabled GPUs in the next major narrative turn. This is a forward reference only; see Ch43.
