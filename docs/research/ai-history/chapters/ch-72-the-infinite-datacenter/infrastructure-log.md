# Infrastructure Log: Chapter 72 - The Infinite Datacenter

## Physical Stack

- **Land:** Stargate's nationwide site search, Richland Parish's 2,250 acres,
  and El Paso's 1GW-scalable site show that AI capacity is sited before it is
  trained.
- **Buildings:** Richland's 4 million square feet and Hyperion's JV-backed
  buildings make the datacenter a civil-construction object.
- **Power Connection:** Ch72 should mention substations, transmission lines,
  and utility planning only as site prerequisites. Ch70 owns deeper generation
  and grid-policy analysis.
- **Cooling:** PUE, outside-air cooling, liquid cooling, closed-loop systems,
  WUE, Cold UTES, and water restoration are in scope as design constraints.
- **Compute Hardware:** GB200 racks, Trainium2, UltraServers, NeuronLinks, and
  EFA networking belong only insofar as they change facility density,
  networking, power, and cooling.
- **Network/Fiber:** Treat connectivity as a long-lived infrastructure input.
  Do not over-speculate about unpublished fiber routes.
- **Municipal Systems:** Roads, water, wastewater, workforce training, local
  contractors, bill assistance, and grants show how communities enter the
  infrastructure stack.
- **Finance:** Capex, leases, finance-lease principal payments, JVs, residual
  guarantees, private debt, and depreciation are infrastructure mechanisms, not
  spreadsheet trivia.

## Boundary With Neighbor Chapters

- **Ch70:** energy demand, electricity generation, PPAs, nuclear, FERC, and
  grid collision. Ch72 uses energy only as a siting/buildout dependency.
- **Ch71:** chips, export controls, HBM, ASML/TSMC, and geopolitical choke
  points. Ch72 assumes chips arrive and asks where they can be installed.
- **Ch63:** inference economics and per-token cost. Ch72 may mention inference
  workloads only as a reason for capacity demand.
- **Ch67:** NVIDIA market power and CUDA/software moat. Ch72 can mention
  NVIDIA racks only as datacenter equipment.
- **Ch68:** labor/copyright/data supply chain. Ch72 owns construction labor
  and local workforce, not annotation/copyright labor.

- Make "gigawatt" legible by tying it to project commitments and site design,
  not by adding unsupported household-equivalent comparisons.
- Use company claims with attribution. "Meta says" and "OpenAI announced" are
  better than omniscient narration for future plans.
- The final chapter should feel like a culmination, not an appendix. Keep the
  last pages tied to the book arc.
