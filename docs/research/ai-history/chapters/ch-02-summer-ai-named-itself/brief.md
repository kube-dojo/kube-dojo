# Chapter 2: The Summer AI Named Itself (Dartmouth + Cold War)

## Thesis
The 1956 Dartmouth workshop officially established "Artificial Intelligence" as a field of symbolic logic, but its immediate viability was entirely dependent on Cold War military-academic infrastructure. Early AI was essentially a byproduct of the massive investments in command-and-control computing (like the SAGE system) and defense funding that sought to automate strategic reasoning.

## Scope
- IN SCOPE: The actual events of the 1956 Dartmouth Summer Research Project, Newell and Simon's Logic Theorist (and its reliance on the RAND JOHNNIAC computer), and the influx of military funding (ONR, early ARPA) that provided the necessary compute infrastructure.
- OUT OF SCOPE: The planning of the workshop (belongs to Chapter 1); the Perceptron and neural network debates (belongs to Chapter 3).

## Scenes Outline
1. **The Logic Theorist on the JOHNNIAC:** Before Dartmouth even begins, Allen Newell and Herbert Simon successfully run the Logic Theorist. Crucially, they achieve this not at a university, but at the RAND Corporation using the JOHNNIAC (a von Neumann architecture machine built for the Air Force). This proves symbolic AI requires military-grade infrastructure.
2. **The Dartmouth Anti-Climax (Summer 1956):** The workshop itself. The realization that McCarthy's vision of a collaborative breakthrough on the IBM 704 is hindered by the reality that programming these early mainframes is incredibly tedious. The contrast between the grand vision and the infrastructural friction.
3. **The Cold War Blank Check:** The aftermath of Dartmouth. The field realizes that to get the massive mainframe time required for symbolic AI (like list processing in LISP), they need to align with military goals. The establishment of AI labs at MIT and Stanford funded by the Office of Naval Research (ONR) and later ARPA, tying AI's infrastructure to the Cold War.
