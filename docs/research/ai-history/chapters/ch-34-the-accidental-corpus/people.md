# People: Chapter 34 — The Accidental Corpus

Each entry lists only what is verified by an anchored primary or anchored-secondary source. Speculation, biographical color from non-anchored sources, and "later became famous for X" claims are deferred to `open-questions.md`.

## The corpus baseline

- **W. Nelson Francis** (Brown University). Co-compiler of the Brown Corpus. Listed alongside Henry Kučera as the principal compiler in the Brown Corpus Manual front matter (revised July 1979). The original 1967 work *Computational Analysis of Present-Day American English* (Brown University Press) is co-authored with Kučera. (Brown Corpus Manual front matter; Halevy/Norvig/Pereira 2009 ref [3].)

- **Henry Kučera** (Brown University). Co-compiler of the Brown Corpus, co-author of the 1967 Brown University Press analytic volume, and co-reviser of the Manual. Listed as "H. Kucera" or "H. Kučera" depending on source. (Brown Corpus Manual front matter; Halevy/Norvig/Pereira 2009 ref [3].)

- **John B. Carroll**. Listed as the third author in Halevy 2009's reference [3] for the 1967 Brown University Press volume. The Brown Corpus Manual lists Carroll among "those who have worked on the production of alternate versions of the Corpus" but does not list him as a Corpus compiler; the precise nature of his contribution to the 1967 volume vs. later tagged versions is a Yellow biographical detail. (Brown Corpus Manual front matter; Halevy/Norvig/Pereira 2009 ref [3].)

## The accidental cause

- **Tim Berners-Lee** (CERN, March 1989). Author of "Information Management: A Proposal." At the time of writing, employed by CERN. Had previously written a personal hypertext system called Enquire in 1980 in the PS (Proton Synchrotron) control system. The 1989 proposal is signed simply "Tim Berners-Lee, CERN." The "Mesh"→"World Wide Web" naming decision is documented in the prefatory header of the HTMLized version. (Berners-Lee 1989 prefatory header; §"Personal Experience with Hypertext.")

  The chapter must not extrapolate from the 1989 document to Berners-Lee's later career arcs (W3C founding, knighthood, etc.) without separate primary anchors; those facts are real but out of this chapter's scope.

## Mining the wild

- **Philip Resnik** (Department of Linguistics / Institute for Advanced Computer Studies, University of Maryland, College Park). Sole author of Resnik 1999 "Mining the Web for Bilingual Text." Email at the time: `resnik@umiacs.umd.edu`. The work was supported by Department of Defense contract MDA90496C1250, DARPA/ITO Contract N66001-97-C-8540, and a Sun Microsystems Laboratories research grant — i.e., it was not Microsoft- or Google-funded; it was DoD-funded academic work. (Resnik 1999 byline and footnote 1, p.527.)

- **Dan Melamed** and **Doug Oard**. Acknowledged as helpful interlocutors for STRAND (Resnik 1999 footnote 1, p.527). Neither is a co-author; mention only if needed for context.

- **Jeff Allen**. Acknowledged in Resnik 1999 footnote 1 for assistance in the French-English experimental evaluation. (Resnik 1999 footnote 1, p.527.)

- **Michael Littman**. Source for the personal-communication note that the Babel survey missed five additional languages on the Web. (Resnik 1999 §1 p.527 parenthetical.)

## The log-linear surprise

- **Michele Banko** (Microsoft Research, Redmond, WA, 2001). Co-author with Eric Brill of "Scaling to Very Very Large Corpora for Natural Language Disambiguation" (ACL 2001). Email at the time: `mbanko@microsoft.com`. (Banko & Brill 2001 byline, PDF p.1.)

- **Eric Brill** (Microsoft Research, Redmond, WA, 2001). Co-author. Email at the time: `brill@microsoft.com`. (Banko & Brill 2001 byline, PDF p.1.) Brill was already known in the field for transformation-based learning (cited as Mangu and Brill 1997; Brill and Wu 1998 in Banko & Brill 2001 references).

- **Dan Roth**. Acknowledged in Banko & Brill 2001 footnote 1 for making both Winnow and Perceptron implementations available. (Banko & Brill 2001 footnote 1, PDF p.1.)

## The doctrine articulated

- **Thorsten Brants** (Google Inc., Mountain View, 2006-2007). First-named author on both the Web 1T 5-Gram corpus release (LDC2006T13, with Alex Franz, 2006) and the EMNLP-CoNLL 2007 paper "Large Language Models in Machine Translation" (with Popat, Xu, Och, and Dean). Email at the time: `brants@google.com`. (LDC LDC2006T13 catalog page; Brants et al. 2007 byline, p.858.)

- **Alex Franz** (Google Inc., 2006). Co-author of the Web 1T 5-Gram corpus release. (LDC LDC2006T13 catalog page.)

- **Ashok C. Popat**, **Peng Xu**, **Franz J. Och**, **Jeffrey Dean** (all Google Inc., 2007). Co-authors of Brants et al. 2007 EMNLP-CoNLL. (Brants et al. 2007 byline, p.858.) Och and Dean are notable in their own right (Och for SMT, Dean for distributed-systems infrastructure), but the chapter's primary anchor is the joint paper.

- **Alon Halevy** (Google research scientist, 2009). Co-author of "The Unreasonable Effectiveness of Data." (Halevy/Norvig/Pereira 2009 byline and author bio, p.12.)

- **Peter Norvig** (Google research director, 2009). Co-author. The undergraduate-at-Brown anecdote in the article ("One of us, as an undergraduate at Brown University, remembers the excitement of having access to the Brown Corpus") refers to Norvig. (Halevy/Norvig/Pereira 2009 p.8 and author bio, p.12.)

- **Fernando Pereira** (Google research director, 2009). Co-author. (Halevy/Norvig/Pereira 2009 byline and author bio, p.12.)

- **James Hays** and **Alexei A. Efros**. Authors of the scene-completion experiment cited in Halevy 2009 p.9 (Hays & Efros, "Scene Completion Using Millions of Photographs," *Communications of the ACM* vol. 51, no. 10, 2008, pp.87-94). The paper is the supporting anchor for the photos-needed-to-be-millions caveat. (Halevy/Norvig/Pereira 2009 ref [7], p.12.)

## The contemporary historiography

- **Kenneth Church** (Johns Hopkins University, HLTCOE, at time of writing 2011). Author of "A Pendulum Swung Too Far" (LiLT vol. 6 issue 5, October 2011). Church had been a central figure in the empirical revival itself (cited in Hall et al. 2008 ACL Anthology analysis as one of the inflection-point authors with Brown et al. 1988 and Church 1988). The Pendulum paper is partly an autobiographical reflection on a movement Church helped start. (Church 2011 byline, p.1; opening section "Motivation for Pragmatism.")

- **Karen Spärck Jones** (Cambridge, 2005 ACL Lifetime Achievement). Author of "Some Points in a Time" (*Computational Linguistics* vol. 31 no. 1). Useful as a senior witness to the same statistical-revival arc. (Spärck Jones 2005 J05-1001, byline and abstract.)

## The earlier waves Church names but the chapter does not draft

Church 2011 names a "PCM" coalition of rationalists the empirical revival reacted against:
- **John R. Pierce** — chaired the 1966 ALPAC report (referenced Church 2011 §3 PCM); later wrote the "Whither Speech Recognition?" letter to JASA. Background only; load-bearing for Ch32 (DARPA SUR), not Ch34.
- **Noam Chomsky** — Church 2011 §3 references Chomsky's 1957 *Syntactic Structures* critique of n-grams. Background only; load-bearing for Ch28 (the second AI winter framing) and earlier chapters.
- **Marvin Minsky** — Church 2011 §3 references Minsky and Papert 1969 critique of neural networks. Background only; load-bearing for Ch17 (perceptron's fall).

These are listed for the chapter's awareness; do not draft scenes around them. Church names them collectively to say that empiricism-by-2011 risked forgetting their critiques.
