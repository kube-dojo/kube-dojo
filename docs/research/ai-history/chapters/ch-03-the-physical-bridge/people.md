# People: Chapter 3 — The Physical Bridge

Verified protagonist bios. Bios are kept short — material that does not directly bear on the 1935-1938 switching-theory window stays out. Verification colors track how confident the chapter can be in load-bearing biographical claims when drafting.

## Principal Protagonists

### Claude Elwood Shannon (1916-2001)

- **Position in 1936-1937:** Research assistant in the MIT Department of Electrical Engineering, working under Vannevar Bush on differential-analyzer-related problems. B.S. from the University of Michigan, 1936 (per Shannon 1937 thesis cover page).
- **Role in the chapter:** The chapter's primary protagonist. Author of "A Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switching Circuits" (master's thesis, MIT, signed August 10, 1937; published as *Trans. AIEE* 57, no. 12, pp. 713-723, in December 1938).
- **Thesis supervisor:** Professor F. L. Hitchcock (per the thesis's acknowledgement page). The 1938 paper byline additionally thanks "Doctor F. L. Hitchcock, Doctor Vannevar Bush, and Doctor S. H. Caldwell" for "helpful encouragement and criticism." The chapter must distinguish Hitchcock (formal academic advisor) from Bush (research-program supervisor and MIT employer).
- **Why he matters here:** Shannon's distinctive contribution is the *axiomatic completeness* of the switching-circuit calculus — the eight postulates, the perfect-induction proofs, the explicit identification with George Boole's calculus of propositions, and the worked simplification methodology. Later achievements (1948 information-theory paper; 1949 BSTJ switching-circuits paper) are out of scope for Ch3 except where the 1949 paper anchors the post-Dartmouth Nakashima citation.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the position-and-role claim via Shannon 1938 byline footnote (Wiley reissue p.471) and Shannon 1937 thesis cover page; **Green** for the supervisor identification via thesis acknowledgement page and 1938 byline footnote; Yellow for personal-biographical claims about pre-MIT background (Soni & Goodman 2017 page anchors pending).

### Vannevar Bush (1890-1974)

- **Position in 1936-1937:** Vice-president and Dean of Engineering at MIT (since 1932); architect of the 1931 Differential Analyzer; principal investigator on the March 1936 Rockefeller Foundation grant of $85,000 for three years to build the Rockefeller Differential Analyzer (the relay-controlled successor).
- **Role in the chapter:** The chapter's institutional protagonist. Bush created the research program that motivated Shannon's thesis. The 1931 mechanical analyzer was the working machine; the 1942 Rockefeller successor was the relay-controlled vision that needed an automatic-control theory — i.e., needed Shannon's thesis.
- **Why he matters here:** Without Bush's analyzer program, the setup-problem context that gave Shannon's thesis its practical motivation would not have existed. Bush's later career (OSRD, "Memex," *Science: The Endless Frontier*) is out of scope for Ch3.
- **Verification:** **Green** for institutional-position claims via Owens 1986 narrative throughout; **Green** for the March 1936 grant via Owens 1986 p.79 (citing Bush to Weaver letter, March 17, 1936); Yellow for any claim about Bush's day-to-day mentorship of Shannon (no Owens-anchored Shannon mentions; Soni & Goodman or Hagley/MIT correspondence required).

### Akira Nakashima (1908-1970)

- **Position in 1934-1938:** Engineer at Nippon Electric Company (NEC) (Nippon Denki Kabushiki Gaisha), Tokyo. Graduated from Tokyo University. In 1936 transferred to transmission engineering but continued switching-theory research after office hours under guidance of Yasujiro Niwa (NEC Chief Engineer at the time) and Yasujiro Shimazu.
- **Role in the chapter:** The chapter's parallel-discovery protagonist. Author of the 1934-1935 *Nichiden Geppo* serial "The theory of relay circuit engineering" and the September 1935 *Journal of the Institute of Telegraph and Telephone Engineers of Japan* paper "Synthesis theory of relay networks" — both of which present a switching algebra for relay networks two years before Shannon's thesis.
- **Why he matters here:** Demonstrates that the switching-circuit-as-algebra insight was not unique to Shannon. Nakashima developed the framework independently, recognized the identity with Boolean algebra only in August 1938, and first cited Boole and Schröder explicitly in 1940.
- **Verification:** **Green** for institutional-position claims via TICSP-40 pp.13-17 (Stanković & Astola 2008's biographical-narrative section verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28); **Green** for publication priority via TICSP-40 pp.18-20 publication list; Yellow for life dates (1908-1970 figure not yet primary-anchored — typically reported in Japanese-language sources).
- **Spelling note (per TICSP-40 p.10):** Three English spellings appear across his papers — Nakashima, Nakasima, Nakajima — and two kanji renderings exist in his Japanese publications. The chapter should standardize on "Akira Nakashima" (the most common modern English form) and footnote the variants on first mention.

### Masao Hanzawa (dates not yet anchored)

- **Position in 1936-1941:** Engineer at Nippon Electric Company (NEC), exchange-engineering team. Co-author with Nakashima on multiple key switching-theory papers from 1936 onward.
- **Role in the chapter:** Nakashima's co-author on the 1938 *Nippon Electrical Communication Engineering* "Theory of equivalent transformation of simple partial paths" and the 1940-1941 "Expansion theorem and design of two-terminal relay networks" papers — the latter being the first place the Nakashima-Hanzawa work explicitly cites Boole and Schröder.
- **Why he matters here:** A second name that should be acknowledged in the parallel-discovery framing. Hanzawa later authored (1989) the chapter "Origin and Development of Switching Circuit Theory" in NEC's *Switching Circuit Theory*, which TICSP-40 reproduces partially as Reference 6.
- **Verification:** **Green** for co-authorship claims via TICSP-40 pp.18-20 publication list; Yellow for biographical depth (life dates not yet anchored).

## Supporting Figures

### Frank Lauren Hitchcock (1875-1957)

- **Position in 1937:** Professor of mathematics at MIT (and former president of MIT's Mathematical Association of America chapter); known earlier for the 1941 Hitchcock transportation problem in optimization theory.
- **Role in the chapter:** Shannon's formal master's-thesis supervisor at MIT, named in the thesis's acknowledgement page and again in the 1938 paper byline footnote.
- **Why he matters here:** A small but important point — Bush was *not* Shannon's formal academic advisor; Hitchcock was. Bush was Shannon's research-program supervisor and MIT employer. The chapter must preserve this distinction.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the supervisor-of-thesis claim via Shannon 1937 thesis acknowledgement page and Shannon 1938 paper byline footnote (verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28). Yellow for Hitchcock's broader biographical context (life dates per standard reference; not anchored to a specific source).

### Samuel H. Caldwell (1904-1960)

- **Position in 1937:** Bush's MIT colleague on the Differential Analyzer project; Bush's deputy on the Rockefeller Differential Analyzer project; later director of MIT's Center of Analysis (from 1939).
- **Role in the chapter:** Named in the 1938 paper byline footnote alongside Hitchcock and Bush as a thanked acknowledgee. Visible in Owens 1986 Figure 4 (the 1931 Differential Analyzer photograph) — "Sam Caldwell can be seen at the left." Per Owens 1986 p.79, Caldwell took on day-to-day responsibility for the Rockefeller analyzer project after Bush's MIT vice-presidential and dean duties accumulated.
- **Why he matters here:** A minor figure, named primarily because Shannon's 1938 byline thanked him. Caldwell's work setting up the Rockefeller machine was the operational counterpart to Shannon's theoretical thesis on the same setup problem.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the Owens 1986 anchor (p.72 Figure 4 caption + p.79 leadership-on-Rockefeller-analyzer claim); **Green** for the Shannon 1938 acknowledgement via byline footnote (Wiley reissue p.471).

### Warren Weaver (1894-1978)

- **Position in 1936:** Director of the Natural Sciences Division of the Rockefeller Foundation. The funder's representative on the March 1936 grant.
- **Role in the chapter:** A minor named figure — Weaver is the recipient of Bush's March 17, 1936 letter that documents the $85,000 grant. Weaver later returned in AI-history through Shannon's 1948 information-theory paper and the 1950s machine-translation initiatives, but those are out of scope for Ch3.
- **Why he matters here:** A pointer to the funder relationship. Bush approached Weaver explicitly for the relay-controlled-analyzer project. Weaver is the Rockefeller-side counterpart to Bush; the chapter should mention his name in the funding scene without expanding into his cybernetics-era role.
- **Verification:** **Green** for the position via Owens 1986 narrative pp.79-80; **Green** for the funder-relationship via the March 17, 1936 letter citation in Owens 1986 p.79 footnote.

## Notes on bios

- All bios are kept narrowly to 1934-1938. Later careers (Shannon's 1948 information theory; Bush's "Memex" and OSRD; Nakashima's wartime radar work) are deliberately out of scope.
- Bios mention each person's *position* in the relevant year because institutional location was load-bearing for who got into print first and on what terms.
- No invented quotes. Where a bio implies motive (e.g., "Bush wanted automatic relay control"), the implication must be traceable to Owens 1986 or another Green-anchored source.
- The chapter's protagonist count is deliberately small (Shannon, Bush, Nakashima as principals; Hitchcock, Caldwell, Hanzawa, Weaver as supporting). Adding more figures would risk diffusing the narrative into a "history of digital logic" survey rather than the focused 1937-naming-of-the-switching-algebra story.
