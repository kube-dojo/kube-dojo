**Scene-level note:** Avoid the trap of either (a) making Nakashima a heroic underdog who deserves Shannon's credit, or (b) treating Shannon's later citation in 1949 as an act of generous attribution. The historical reality is messier: two engineers with similar institutional pressures (Bell-Labs-adjacent in Shannon's case, NEC-adjacent in Nakashima's) arrived at the same insight roughly contemporaneously. Shannon was *more complete* (axiomatization, perfect-induction proofs, Boole identification at first publication); Nakashima was *earlier* (1934-1935 vs. 1937). Both points are true. The chapter should preserve both without artificially ranking them.

## Scene 3 — The Thesis (August 1937)

Anchored layer: *800-1,100 words: Shannon's thesis as axiomatic system* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Shannon signs his master's thesis "A Symbolic Analysis of Relay and Switching Circuits" in the MIT Department of Electrical Engineering on August 10, 1937. Supervisor: Professor F. L. Hitchcock. | Shannon 1937 thesis cover/signature page; Shannon 1937 thesis acknowledgement page. | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The thesis opens with the two-problem framing: "analysis" (determine the operating characteristics of a given circuit) and "synthesis" (find a circuit incorporating given characteristics, ideally requiring the least number of switch blades and relay contacts). | Shannon 1937 thesis p.1. | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Shannon names the practical motivating applications: "automatic telephone exchanges, industrial motor control equipment and in almost any circuits designed to perform complex operations automatically." | Shannon 1937 thesis p.1. | **Green** — direct quote, verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Shannon introduces "hindrance" notation: "The symbol 0 (zero) will be used to represent the hinderance of a closed circuit, and the symbol 1 (unity) to represent the hinderance of an open circuit." | Shannon 1937 thesis p.4. | **Green** — direct quote, verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Series connection is denoted +, parallel connection is denoted · (multiplication). | Shannon 1937 thesis pp.4-5. | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The eight postulates (1a, 1b, 2a, 2b, 3a, 3b, 4) are arranged in pairs to emphasize a duality between addition/multiplication and 0/1; "if in any of the *a* postulates the zero's are replaced by one's and the multiplications by additions and vice versa, the corresponding *b* postulate will result." | Shannon 1937 thesis pp.5-6. | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Theorems are proved by "perfect induction" — verification of the theorem for all possible cases (which is finite because each variable takes only the values 0 and 1, per Postulate 4). | Shannon 1937 thesis p.7; Shannon 1938 §II Theorems (Wiley reissue p.473). | **Green** — direct quote, verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Negation X' is defined as the make-vs-break-contact relationship of a relay: "If X is the hindrance of the make contacts of a relay, then X' is the hindrance of the break contacts of the same relay." This is the "normally-closed contact as NOT" anchor. | Shannon 1937 thesis pp.7-8; Shannon 1938 §II Negation (Wiley reissue p.473-474). | **Green** — direct quote, verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The negation theorems X + X' = 1, X·X' = 0, 0' = 1, 1' = 0, (X')' = X follow from the definition. | Shannon 1937 thesis p.8; Shannon 1938 equations (6a)–(8) (Wiley reissue p.474). | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Shannon explicitly identifies his calculus with George Boole's: "The algebra of logic ... originated by George Boole, is a symbolic method of investigating logical relationships." Shannon does *not* claim novelty for the algebra itself — only for its interpretation as a calculus of switching circuits. | Shannon 1937 thesis p.8; Shannon 1938 §II "Analogue with the Calculus of Propositions" (Wiley reissue p.474). | **Green** — direct quote, verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Shannon's Table I (in the published 1938 version, Wiley reissue p.475) maps the symbols across two interpretations: X+Y as series connection ↔ proposition "X or Y is true"; XY as parallel connection ↔ proposition "X and Y are both true"; X' as the contradictory proposition. | Shannon 1938 §II Table I (Wiley reissue p.475). | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |

**Scene-level note:** This is the scene where the chapter must be most careful with the reader's mental model. Shannon's "hindrance" framing is *opposite* to the modern convention: in modern Boolean (1=true=closed=current flows, 0=false=open=current blocked), series circuits implement AND and parallel circuits implement OR. In Shannon's hindrance framing (X=1 for open, X=0 for closed), the operations "+" and "·" act on *open-ness*, and series + means "open if either is open" (an OR-of-open-ness, which is the same as AND-of-closed-ness). The chapter should explain this convention explicitly once and stick with it; either Shannon's hindrance framing or the modern framing is internally consistent, but mixing them silently will confuse readers who try to verify against a textbook.

## Scene 4 — The Simplification (Math as Hardware Optimizer)

Anchored layer: *700-1,000 words: The simplification methodology* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Shannon writes: "any expression formed with the operations of addition, multiplication, and negation represents explicitly a circuit containing only series and parallel connections. Such a circuit will be called a series-parallel circuit. Each letter in an expression of this sort represents a make or break relay contact, or a switch blade and contact." | Shannon 1937 thesis pp.14-15; Shannon 1938 §II "Simplification of Expressions" (Wiley reissue p.477). | **Green** — direct quote, verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| To find the circuit requiring the least number of contacts, Shannon notes, "it is therefore necessary to manipulate the expression into the form in which the least number of letters appear." | Shannon 1937 thesis p.15; Shannon 1938 §II (Wiley reissue p.477). | **Green** — direct quote, verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The worked example (Figure 5): the original hindrance function is Xab = W + W'(X+Y) + (X+Z)(S+W'+Z)(Z'+Y+S'V) — a circuit with thirteen element-occurrences across six distinct relays (W, X, Y, Z, S, V). | Shannon 1937 thesis pp.13-14 (Fig.5); Shannon 1938 §II (Wiley reissue p.477). | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Through repeated application of theorem 17b (a rewriting rule for nested expressions) and the distributive law, the expression reduces to Xab = W + X + Y + ZS'V (Figure 6) — five element-occurrences across the same six relays. | Shannon 1937 thesis pp.14-15 (Fig.6); Shannon 1938 §II (Wiley reissue p.477-478). | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. NB: this is a 13-element-to-5-element reduction, **not** a "5-relay-to-3-relay" reduction. The chapter must use accurate numbers. |
| Shannon's commentary on the reduction: "Note the large reduction in the number of elements." | Shannon 1937 thesis p.15; Shannon 1938 §II (Wiley reissue p.478). | **Green** — direct quote, verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The Section V *Selective Circuit* example (relay A operates when any one, any three, or all four of relays w, x, y, z are operated) demonstrates further reductions: from 20 elements (initial series-parallel form, A = wxyz + w'x'y'z' + w'xyz' + ... [series-parallel sum of 7 terms]) to 15 elements (via the symmetric-function method, A = S₄(1, 3, 4)) to 14 elements (via the dual-network construction on A' = S₄(0, 2)) — the 14-element form being "probably the most economical circuit of any sort." | Shannon 1937 thesis pp.51-58. | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The pedagogical move is the chapter's centerpiece: before Shannon, designing a complex relay circuit meant "a 'cut and try' method, first satisfying one requirement and then making additions until all are satisfied" — and the resulting design "will seldom be the simplest" and often contained "hidden 'sneak circuits.'" Shannon's algebra replaces trial-and-error with deductive simplification. | Shannon 1937 thesis p.1 (the "cut and try" framing). | **Green** — direct quote, verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |

**Scene-level note:** This scene must NOT use the legacy chapter prose's "5 relays to 3" framing. The actual numbers are clear from the primary source: thirteen element-occurrences become five via algebraic simplification. The pedagogical point — that mathematics can optimize hardware before the hardware is built — is preserved without distortion. If the chapter wants a clean numeric sound bite, "20 down to 14" from the *Selective Circuit* example is well-anchored and does not invent.

| Honest close: Shannon's 1937 thesis was not the moment the digital computer was invented (that conjunction came later — Atanasoff-Berry, ENIAC, Turing's wartime Colossus, von Neumann's stored-program); it was not the moment that Boolean algebra entered engineering practice everywhere (Nakashima had begun that move in Japan in 1934-1935; Piesch and Plechl-Duschek would do it in German-speaking Europe in 1939 and 1946); and it was not the moment that automation became calculation (the disc integrator was already a calculation device by other means). What it *was* was the moment one careful master's thesis fixed the axioms, the proofs, the postulates, the negation, and the simplification methodology in a single deductive system — and tied it to George Boole by name. The chapter ends here, on the note that *axiomatization*, not *invention*, is the contribution worth preserving. | Chapter's own framing, drawn from the boundary contract. | Yellow — interpretive synthesis, not a primary-anchored claim. |

**Scene-level note:** This is where the chapter's thesis lands. The narrative move is "Shannon's thesis was the axiomatization moment, not the invention moment." Don't overstate — the chapter must not claim that nothing else mattered or that the digital era was inevitable from 1937 forward. The pattern is that Shannon's framework gave engineers a *deductive language* for circuit design that scaled where intuition could not; the practical consequences (reduced relay counts in Bell System routing, the 1942 Rockefeller analyzer's automatic-control system, post-war digital logic education) compounded over the next two decades.

## Cross-scene notes

- No invented dialogue. Every quoted sentence must come from a primary source — the thesis, the 1938 paper, Owens 1986, or TICSP-40. Where a secondary source (Soni & Goodman 2017, Mindell 2002, Gleick 2011) is cited, it should be tagged as such.
- The "1931 mechanical analyzer vs. 1942 Rockefeller relay analyzer" distinction is the chapter's most error-prone fact. Drafting must keep them clearly separate at every mention.
- The "5 relays to 3" folk claim is wrong; the actual Figure 5/6 reduction is 13 element-occurrences to 5. Drafting must not reproduce the folk version.
- The Nakashima parallel-discovery framing should not be either heroic-underdog or dismissive. The two researchers worked independently with overlapping institutional pressures and arrived at similar insights with different stylistic emphases.
- The chapter should NOT include a deep technical explainer of Boolean algebra (that's Ch1) or of relays-as-physical-devices (that's general engineering background). Ch3's narrow focus is the *axiomatization event* and its institutional consequences.
- Forward references are sparse pointers ("see Ch10 for Shannon's 1948 information theory"; "see Ch4 for the statistical roots that ran in parallel"). Don't anticipate later chapters' arguments.
