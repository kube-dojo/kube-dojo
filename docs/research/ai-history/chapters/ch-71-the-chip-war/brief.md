# Brief: Chapter 71 - The Chip War

## Thesis

After Ch70 turns AI scaling into an electricity problem, Ch71 turns it into a
statecraft problem. Frontier AI did not merely need "chips"; it needed a stack of
scarce, globally distributed choke points: NVIDIA-class accelerators,
high-bandwidth memory, advanced packaging, TSMC foundry capacity, ASML
lithography, EDA/software, and the legal permission to move those pieces across
borders. From 2022 onward, export controls made AI capability a geopolitical
object. The United States tried to slow China's access to the most advanced
compute and manufacturing paths; allies and companies had to operationalize the
rules; China answered with domestic substitution and materials leverage. The
chapter should avoid techno-nationalist triumphalism. The more durable point is
that AI capability became a negotiated property of supply chains, law, and
alliances.

## Scope

- IN SCOPE: 2022 BIS advanced-computing and semiconductor-manufacturing export
  controls; the 2023 updates; the December 2024 package covering HBM,
  semiconductor manufacturing equipment, software tools, and Entity List
  additions; U.S.-Netherlands-Japan coordination around semiconductor
  manufacturing equipment controls; 2025 AI Diffusion rule/rescission as
  evidence of policy instability; NVIDIA H20 and product-threshold adaptation;
  ASML EUV/DUV lithography as a choke point; TSMC advanced-node and
  CoWoS/Arizona capacity as geographic concentration; CHIPS Act industrial
  policy; China's gallium/germanium/antimony and graphite counter-controls.
- OUT OF SCOPE: NVIDIA's market power and software moat as the main story
  (Ch67); datacenter construction, land, cooling, and power megacampuses (Ch72);
  one-company investment history; general Taiwan military scenario speculation;
  detailed tariff policy; claims about secret Chinese chip inventories unless
  publicly anchored; moralizing about "winning" the chip war.

## Required Scenes

1. **The Server Becomes A Strategic Asset:** Open with the moment AI accelerators
   stop looking like ordinary commercial parts. BIS's 2022 rule explicitly links
   advanced computing ICs, supercomputers, AI, WMD design, military
   modernization, and surveillance.
2. **The 2022 Firewall:** Explain the October 2022 control architecture:
   advanced computing items, supercomputer end use, semiconductor manufacturing
   equipment, Entity List/FDP mechanisms, and U.S.-person support controls. Keep
   this readable; the point is legal reach, not regulatory trivia. Include the
   enforcement friction: diversion tactics, shell companies, third-country
   transshipment, and why a legal firewall becomes a compliance chase.
3. **The Moving Threshold:** Show why rules had to keep changing. The 2023 update
   "reinforced" the 2022 controls, and NVIDIA's H20 disclosure shows the
   commercial adaptation loop: firms design around thresholds; governments move
   thresholds again.
4. **The Manufacturing Stack:** Move from chips to machines. ASML is the
   semiconductor manufacturing equipment (SME) choke point for EUV/DUV
   lithography; TSMC is the foundry and advanced-packaging choke point for
   advanced nodes and CoWoS. Advanced AI depends on both front-end nodes and
   back-end packaging/HBM.
5. **Industrial Policy As Re-shoring, Not Instant Independence:** CHIPS funding
   is a response to supply-chain fragility, but a fab is not a laptop factory.
   Use the CHIPS/NIST and TSMC Arizona anchors to show national policy trying to
   rebuild capacity slowly.
6. **China's Counter-Levers:** The chip war is not one-directional. China's
   December 2024 MOFCOM notice restricted gallium, germanium, antimony,
   superhard materials, and graphite checks for the United States. Treat this as
   supply-chain leverage, not proof that China can symmetrically retaliate at
   every point.
7. **Policy Volatility:** The January 2025 AI Diffusion rule and May 2025
   rescission show a second tension: even allies and companies cannot plan if the
   rules swing between global tiering and narrower adversary-focused control.
8. **Handoff To The Infinite Datacenter:** Close by tying export controls back to
   physical buildout. If chips decide who can train, Ch72 asks where those chips
   can actually be installed, cooled, powered, and financed.

## Guardrails

- Do not imply that export controls "stopped" Chinese AI. They changed cost,
  access, performance, and timing; they did not erase capability.
- Do not present BIS statements as neutral truth about Chinese intent. Attribute
  national-security claims to BIS/Commerce where appropriate.
- Do not turn Ch71 into a NVIDIA monopoly chapter. Product thresholds and H20
  matter here only because they show policy/product co-evolution.
- Do not claim ASML alone determines AI capability. Lithography is a choke point
  inside a larger stack that also includes HBM, packaging, foundries, EDA,
  networking, and power.
- Do not overstate CHIPS Act speed. New fabs and packaging capacity take years,
  and Taiwan remains central in the TSMC 2024 report.
- Do not speculate about war over Taiwan. Keep the risk as supply-chain
  concentration and geopolitical exposure unless a primary source supports a
  narrower claim.
- Do not treat China's materials controls as a perfect mirror of U.S. chip
  controls. They are a counter-lever in a different part of the stack.
- Keep datacenter megasites, water, and land for Ch72.
