# Scene Sketches: Chapter 16 - The Cold War Blank Check

## Scene 1 - Sputnik Makes a Patron

**Setting.** Washington and Cambridge, 1957-1962. Sputnik turns technological surprise into a national-security anxiety. Licklider's 1960 paper is not yet an AI funding memo, but it gives the later IPTO program a conceptual grammar: people and computers working in close cooperation, supported by time-sharing, memory, languages, and better input/output.

**Evidence spine.**

| Claim | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Sputnik triggered events leading to ARPA's creation on February 7, 1958. | DARPA Sputnik Surprise section. | Green ([G01]). |
| Licklider's 1960 symbiosis paper framed close human-computer cooperation and named time-sharing/memory/languages/I/O as prerequisites. | Licklider 1960 Summary and Sections 1.1, 5.2. | Green ([G03]). |
| IPTO's origins sat inside command-and-control research concerns. | Kita 2003 pp.62-63; Norberg/O'Neill p.29. | Green ([G04]). |

**Prose use.** Open with the mismatch between the title's "blank check" and the real anxiety behind it. The money is permissive only because the military problem is broad: how to keep command, control, and intelligence from being surprised.

## Scene 2 - Government AI Before the Big Program

**Setting.** RAND, Carnegie/GSIA, and MIT RLE, 1951-1962. Before IPTO makes AI large, symbolic work already depends on defense-adjacent institutions: RAND's Air Force-funded computer, GSIA's ONR/Air Force support, and RLE's military-funded room-and-keypunch support for McCarthy and Minsky.

**Evidence spine.**

| Claim | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Logic Theorist was funded almost entirely by the Air Force through Project RAND and programmed on the Air Force-funded JOHNNIAC. | NRC 1999 pp.201-203. | Green ([G07]). |
| GPS and related GSIA AI work were funded principally by Air Force and ONR money until the early 1960s. | NRC 1999 p.203. | Green ([G08]). |
| MIT's early AI Project was small: room, two programmers, secretary, and keypunch through RLE arrangements. | NRC 1999 p.203. | Green ([G09]). |

**Prose use.** This scene prevents the false "ARPA suddenly created AI" story. The chapter should say ARPA changed the order of magnitude and coherence, not the existence of government support.

## Scene 3 - Licklider's IPTO Bet

**Setting.** IPTO, MIT, Carnegie, Stanford, SDC, 1962-1966. Licklider's office funds centers and shared infrastructure. The "blank check" metaphor belongs here, but the prose must immediately qualify it: Licklider had criteria, program coherence, and command-and-control relevance.

**Evidence spine.**

| Claim | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Licklider used criteria of excellence, DoD-relevant prospects, and coherent sponsored efforts. | Norberg/O'Neill pp.29-30. | Green ([G05]). |
| IPTO shifted AI from small projects to a large, high-profile domain and legitimized centers. | NRC 1999 pp.204-205. | Green ([G11]-[G13]). |
| Project MAC, Carnegie, and Stanford show center-based funding rather than a scatter of small grants. | Norberg/O'Neill pp.98-103; DARPA Project MAC entry; Stanford CS-TR-74-409 pp.1-2. | Green ([G14]-[G18]). |
| PI meetings and program areas made the community itself part of the infrastructure. | Norberg/O'Neill pp.43-47. | Green ([G23], [G24]). |

**Prose use.** Keep the scene specific: a program manager with a small staff and a large budget chooses a few places and makes their work mutually legible. Do not invent internal meeting dialogue.

## Scene 4 - What the Money Bought

**Setting.** Machine rooms and terminal rooms, 1964-1969. The chapter moves from funding language to hardware reality: CTSS, Q-32, PDP-6/PDP-10, ITS, MATHLAB, MACLISP, robot hands and eyes, Stanford's PDP-6, and memory limits in LISP 2 work.

**Evidence spine.**

| Claim | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Project MAC's goal was online computing for individual intellectual work and a large multiple-access system. | Project MAC PR II p.xiii. | Green ([G19]). |
| CTSS was both service facility and man-machine interaction laboratory. | Project MAC PR II p.63. | Green ([G20]). |
| MIT's AI environment included PDP-6/PDP-10/ITS, real-time device control, MATHLAB, and MACLISP. | Project MAC PR VI pp.11-53 to 11-58. | Green ([G21]). |
| Q-32 memory limits affected LISP 2 planning and shifted targets toward IBM 360/67 and PDP-6/PDP-10. | McCarthy "Beyond LISP 1.5." | Green ([G22]). |

**Prose use.** This is the densest infrastructure scene. The point is not nostalgia for machines; it is that AI's theoretical ambition required interactive access, symbolic languages, memory, terminals, and students who could live inside those systems.

## Scene 5 - The Check Gets a Memo Line

**Setting.** 1968-1971. AI becomes more explicit in IPTO categories just as Congress and DoD require clearer military relevance. Mansfield is not the winter; it is the chapter's boundary marker. The permissive Cold War bargain starts demanding more legible justification.

**Evidence spine.**

| Claim | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| Norberg/O'Neill's program tables show changing IPTO labels, including later "Intelligent systems." | Norberg/O'Neill pp.44-45. | Green ([G23]). |
| Edwards says AI was not a separate ARPA budget line until 1968. | Edwards Ch.8 p.273 excerpt. | Green as interpretation ([G28]). |
| Public Law 91-121 Sec.203 required a direct and apparent relationship to a specific military function or operation. | 83 Stat.206. | Green ([G25]). |
| GAO reviewed DoD implementation and equipment-disposition uncertainty. | GAO B-167034 Highlights. | Green ([G26]). |

**Prose use.** End with a disciplined handoff: the old room for ambitious symbolic work did not vanish, but it had to explain itself more explicitly. Ch17 handles perceptron funding consequences; Ch20 handles Project MAC; Ch32 handles DARPA's later benchmark regime.
