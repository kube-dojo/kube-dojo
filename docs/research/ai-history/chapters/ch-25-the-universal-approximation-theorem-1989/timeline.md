# Timeline: Chapter 25 - The Universal Approximation Theorem (1989)

| Date | Event | Evidence | Draft Use |
|---|---|---|---|
| 1957 | Kolmogorov publishes a representation theorem for continuous functions of many variables using superpositions of one-variable functions and addition. | Kolmogorov 1957; Pinkus 1999 context | Mathematical prehistory, not neural-network proof. |
| 1969 | Minsky and Papert's *Perceptrons* sharpens attention on representational limits of simple perceptrons. | Ch17 research; Cybenko references perceptron literature | Sets up why representation proofs mattered two decades later. |
| 1984 | Valiant publishes a formal theory of learnability. | Valiant 1984 | Optional context for separating "can represent" from "can learn." |
| 1986 | Backpropagation revival shows multilayer networks can be trained on small demonstrations. | Ch24 research | Immediate prior chapter; creates a practical reason to care about approximation capacity. |
| 1988 | Cybenko submits/revises work on sigmoidal superpositions. | Springer bibliographic record: received 1988, revised 1989 | Shows theorem development overlaps the late-1980s connectionist revival. |
| 1989 | Funahashi publishes a continuous-mapping approximation result in *Neural Networks*. | Funahashi 1989 | Parallel source cluster. |
| 1989 | Hornik, Stinchcombe, and White publish "Multilayer feedforward networks are universal approximators." | Hornik/Stinchcombe/White 1989 | Parallel source cluster and title-level slogan source. |
| December 1989 | Cybenko's "Approximation by superpositions of a sigmoidal function" appears in *Mathematics of Control, Signals, and Systems*. | Cybenko 1989 | Central theorem scene. |
| 1993 | Leshno, Lin, Pinkus, and Schocken refine activation-function conditions. | Leshno et al. 1993 | Afterlife/precision, not main event. |
