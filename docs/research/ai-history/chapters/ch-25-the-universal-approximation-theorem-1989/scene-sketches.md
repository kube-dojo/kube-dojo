# Scene Sketches: Chapter 25 - The Universal Approximation Theorem (1989)

## Scene 1: The Aftertaste of Perceptrons

The chapter can open with the intellectual scar left by perceptron limitations: neural networks were alluring, but the field had learned to ask what they could represent before asking whether they could learn. This is not a drama scene unless sourced; keep it as a conceptual setup tied to Ch17.

Evidence needed:
- Ch17 Minsky/Papert anchor for single-layer representational limits.
- Cybenko or Pinkus passage that places the theorem in neural-network approximation context.

## Scene 2: Existence, Not Recipe

Explain approximation as a map covered by enough small tiles. A theorem can say a tiled path exists without handing you the tiles in the right order. This section should use a concrete but minimal example, then immediately state the caveats: compact domain, continuity, activation assumptions, enough hidden units, and no guarantee of efficient training.

Evidence needed:
- Cybenko theorem/abstract page.
- Pinkus survey passage on approximation-theoretic scope.

## Scene 3: The 1989 Cluster

Present 1989 as convergence: Cybenko, Hornik/Stinchcombe/White, and Funahashi each helped stabilize the representational claim. This avoids a false lone-genius priority fight and gives readers the sense that the field was mathematically closing a gap.

Evidence needed:
- Bibliographic/page anchors for all three 1989 papers.
- A survey anchor explaining how these results relate.

## Scene 4: The Useful Misunderstanding

The closing scene should treat "neural networks can approximate anything" as a slogan that is true only after the footnotes return. That slogan helped restore confidence, but it also smuggled in assumptions about trainability and practical size. The chapter should end by handing off to LeNet: architecture and data constraints are where the theorem's abstract promise starts becoming engineered.

Evidence needed:
- Pinkus/Leshno guardrails.
- Ch27 cross-link after LeNet research is populated.
