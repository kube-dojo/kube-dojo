# Brief: Chapter 10 - The Imitation Game

## Thesis
As the physical digital infrastructure matured, Alan Turing published a philosophical framework that abandoned biological mimicry entirely. By proposing the "Imitation Game," Turing argued that the internal mechanism of intelligence (analog vs. digital) didn't matter; only the measurable, behavioral output mattered. This set the ultimate empirical goalpost for the field.

## Scope
- IN SCOPE: Alan Turing's 1950 paper "Computing Machinery and Intelligence", the Turing Test, the rejection of theology and biology as prerequisites for thought.
- OUT OF SCOPE: The Dartmouth Conference (next chapter).

## Scenes Outline
1. **The Question:** "Can machines think?" Turing replaces a vague philosophical debate with a pragmatic parlor game.
2. **The Teleprinter Constraint:** Turing explicitly limits the test to a text-based interface, separating intellectual capability from physical embodiment.
3. **The Empirical Baseline:** Turing defends the digital computer against mathematical, theological, and biological objections, setting the stage for the Dartmouth consensus.
