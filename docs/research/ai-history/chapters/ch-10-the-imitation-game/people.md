## Protagonist

### Alan Turing (1912-1954)
- **Position in 1947**: National Physical Laboratory (NPL), Teddington — Senior Principal Scientific Officer working on the Automatic Computing Engine (ACE) design. Delivered the 20 February 1947 lecture to the London Mathematical Society on the ACE.
- **Position in 1948**: NPL (left mid-year for a sabbatical at King's College, Cambridge); composed the *Intelligent Machinery* report at NPL before departing.
- **Position in 1950**: Reader in the Mathematics Department at the University of Manchester (since 1948), with a position concerned with the Manchester computer. Wrote and submitted "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" to *Mind* during this period.
- **Position in 1952**: Manchester. Participates in the BBC Third Programme broadcast in January.
- **Role in the chapter**: The sole protagonist. Every load-bearing claim in the chapter is something Turing wrote or said in one of four primary sources (1947 LMS lecture, 1948 NPL report, 1950 *Mind* paper, 1952 BBC broadcast).
- **What's out of scope**: Bletchley Park / wartime cryptanalysis (background only); the universal Turing machine (Ch2's domain); the morphogenesis work (post-1952); Turing's prosecution and 1954 death (the chapter does not need this material to argue its thesis, and the book's broader Turing arc handles it).
- **Verification**: **Green** for institutional positions (NPL 1947-1948, Manchester 1948-1954) — standard biographical record, cross-confirmable in any reference work. **Green** for what Turing wrote in the four primary sources — verified by `pdftotext`/OCR extraction (Claude 2026-04-28). The chapter does not need to anchor specific Hodges 1983 page numbers for Turing's biography because the argument runs entirely through the primary texts.

## Interlocutors in the 1952 BBC Broadcast

### Max H. A. Newman (1897-1984)
- **Position in 1952**: Fielden Professor of Mathematics at the University of Manchester; head of the department where Turing was Reader. Newman had built the Manchester Royal Society Computing Laboratory after the war.
- **Role in the chapter**: One of four participants in the 1952 BBC broadcast. Generally aligned with Turing's behavioural-test framing in the broadcast (per secondary accounts).
- **Why he matters here**: Newman is the in-broadcast voice closest to Turing's own — a fellow mathematician with first-hand experience of the Manchester machine. His role frames Jefferson and Braithwaite as the dissenting voices.
- **Verification**: **Yellow** — Newman's biographical position is well documented in standard references but not directly verified by Claude 2026-04-28; the broadcast content is Yellow pending Copeland 2004 transcript access.

### Sir Geoffrey Jefferson (1886-1961)
- **Position in 1952**: Professor of Neurosurgery at the University of Manchester. Author of the 1949 Lister Oration "The Mind of Mechanical Man" (*BMJ* 1949 vol. 1 (4616), pp. 1105-1110), which had attacked claims about machine intelligence on biological-neurological grounds.
- **Role in the chapter**: The 1952 BBC broadcast's principal sceptic. Carries forward into the broadcast the biological objection from his 1949 Lister Oration — that thinking requires the kind of nervous-system substrate humans have.
- **Why he matters here**: Jefferson personifies the live opposition to Turing's behavioural-test framing in 1952. The chapter's Scene 5 (Aftermath) uses the Turing-Jefferson exchange to show the framing being defended in real time — not just left on the page of the *Mind* paper.
- **Verification**: **Yellow** — Jefferson's institutional position and the 1949 Lister Oration citation are widely standard; the 1952 broadcast text where Jefferson advances his objections is Yellow pending Copeland 2004 transcript access. The 1949 Lister Oration DOI 10.1136/bmj.1.4616.1105 is Crossref-resolvable but not directly verified by Claude.

### Richard Bevan Braithwaite (1900-1990)
- **Position in 1952**: Knightbridge Professor of Moral Philosophy at the University of Cambridge.
- **Role in the chapter**: One of four participants in the 1952 BBC broadcast. Brings a philosophy-of-mind voice to the conversation.
- **Why he matters here**: Braithwaite's presence frames the broadcast as already a four-way conversation between mathematics (Newman), neuroscience (Jefferson), philosophy (Braithwaite), and the test's own author (Turing) — establishing in 1952 the cross-disciplinary register the Turing Test debate would carry through the rest of the century.
- **Verification**: **Yellow** — biographical position widely standard; broadcast content Yellow pending Copeland 2004 access.

## Cited but Not Present

### Lady Ada Lovelace (1815-1852)
- **Position**: Mathematician; author of the 1842 "Sketch of the Analytical Engine," appended to Menabrea's translation as "Notes by the Translator," in *Scientific Memoirs* vol. 3.
- **Role in the chapter**: Cited by name in *Mind* 1950 §6.6 ("Lady Lovelace's Objection"). Her objection is paraphrased by Turing as: the Analytical Engine "has no pretensions to originate anything." Turing rebuts this objection in the same section.
- **Why she matters here**: §6.6 is one of the rebuttals Turing engages most fully; it is one of Layer 3's load-bearing scene anchors.
- **Verification**: **Green** that Turing cites her by name and paraphrases her objection in §6.6 — verified by `pdftotext` (Claude 2026-04-28). **Yellow** for any verbatim quotation of Lovelace's 1842 *Sketch* — that text is widely available but not directly verified by Claude. The chapter does not need to lean on Lovelace's original wording; Turing's paraphrase is what does work in §6.6.

### Helen Keller (1880-1968)
- **Position**: American author and disability-rights advocate.
- **Role in the chapter**: Cited by Turing in *Mind* 1950 §7 as evidence that education can succeed without conventional sensory channels — "The example of Miss Helen Keller shows that education can take place provided that communication in both directions between teacher and pupil can take place by some means or other." Used to defend the child-machine programme against the objection that machines lack proper sensory embodiment.
- **Verification**: **Green** that Turing cites her in §7 — verified by `pdftotext` (Claude 2026-04-28). Keller's biography is standard reference material; not directly verified by Claude and not load-bearing for the chapter.

### Carl Friedrich Gauss
- **Position**: 19th-century mathematician.
- **Role in the chapter**: Cited by Turing in the 1948 NPL report inner pp. [2]-[3] in the famous schoolboy anecdote about adding 15+18+21+...+54 — used to argue that intelligence sometimes coincides with making a "mistake" by the master's instructions, refuting the claim that intelligence requires never erring.
- **Verification**: **Green** that Turing cites the Gauss anecdote in NPL inner pp. [2]-[3] — verified by `ocrmypdf` + `pdftotext` (Claude 2026-04-28). The anecdote itself is folklore, not a verified historical event; the chapter can present Turing's use of it without authenticating the underlying story.

## Notes on bios

- All bios are kept narrowly to the 1947-1952 window. Later careers (Newman's later Manchester work; Jefferson's later neurosurgical career; Braithwaite's later Cambridge philosophy; Turing's morphogenesis and 1954 death) are deliberately out of scope.
- Bios mention each person's *position* in 1947-1952 because institutional location is load-bearing for the broadcast scene — three Manchester voices (Turing, Newman, Jefferson) plus a Cambridge philosopher (Braithwaite) is a particular configuration of authority.
- No invented quotations. Where a bio implies motive (e.g. "Jefferson personifies the live opposition"), the implication must be cited from a secondary source or marked as conjecture.
- Turing's biography (sexuality, prosecution, death) is out of scope for this chapter. The book's broader Turing arc handles that material; Ch10's argument runs entirely through the four primary texts.
