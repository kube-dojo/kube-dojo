## Scene 1 — The Precursors (1947-1948)

Anchored layer: *700-1000 words: The Precursors (1947-1948)* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| On 20 February 1947 Turing addresses the London Mathematical Society on the ACE design at the National Physical Laboratory; the lecture argues that "fair play must be given to the machine" — intelligence does not require infallibility. | 1947 LMS lecture, seminarTEXT pp. 13-14 (Carpenter & Doran 1986). | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Turing estimates the human-brain memory capacity at "about ten thousand million binary digits" — i.e. 10¹⁰ bits — and argues that "a few million digits" might suffice for limited intelligence demonstrations such as chess. | 1947 LMS lecture, seminarTEXT pp. 12-13. | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Turing argues that "what we want is a machine that can learn from experience" — placing intelligence in the *learning loop* rather than in a fixed programme. | 1947 LMS lecture, seminarTEXT p. 13. | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| In 1948 Turing composes the unpublished NPL report *Intelligent Machinery*. The opening lists five common objections to machine intelligence — (a) human pride, (b) Promethean religious feeling, (c) limitations of recent machinery, (d) Gödel-style theorems, (e) intelligence as a "reflection" of the creator — and refutes each in the section "Refutation of Some Objections." | Turing 1948 NPL inner pp. [1]-[3] (Ince ed. 1992 pp. [107]-[110]). | **Green** — verified by Claude `ocrmypdf`+`pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The 1948 report introduces "unorganized machines" — discrete-state random networks of NAND-like units — as the simplest example of what would later be called connectionist models. The infant cortex is proposed as biologically analogous to such a network. | Turing 1948 NPL inner pp. [5]-[7] (Unorganized Machines section); pp. [9]-[10] (The Cortex as an Unorganized Machine section). | **Green** — verified by Claude `ocrmypdf`+`pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Education of machinery is framed via rewards and punishments; "events which shortly preceded the occurrence of a punishment signal are unlikely to be repeated, whereas a reward signal increased the probability of repetition of the events which led up to it." Turing introduces "paper interference" — the communication of instructions, contrasted with "screwdriver interference" (rewiring). | Turing 1948 NPL inner pp. [7]-[8] (Interference and Education sections). | **Green** — verified by Claude `ocrmypdf`+`pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| The 1948 report ends with a two-room chess proto-imitation experiment: subjects A (poor chess player), B (mathematician operating a paper machine), and C (poor chess player as observer). C plays one game against A and one against the paper machine, communicating moves between rooms. "C may find it quite difficult to tell which he is playing." Turing adds: "This is a rather idealized form of an experiment I have actually done." | Turing 1948 NPL inner p. [13] (Intelligence as an Emotional Concept section, closing paragraph). | **Green** for the experiment text on NPL p. [13]; **Yellow** for any priority claim that this is "the first formulation of the Turing Test." |

**Scene-level note:** The narrative arc of Scene 1 is *the test as Turing's idea before it had its name*. The 1947 lecture lays the philosophical groundwork (no infallibility, learning-from-experience); the 1948 report supplies the operational shape (paper machine, two-room separation, observer C trying to tell which he is playing). Avoid making this a biographical scene about Turing's NPL years — the chapter is not a life-and-times. Stay on the texts.

## Scene 2 — The Parlour Game (October 1950)

Anchored layer: *700-1000 words: The Parlour Game* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| In October 1950 Turing publishes "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" in *Mind* 59 (236), pp. 433-460. The opening sentence — "I propose to consider the question, 'Can machines think?'" — sets up its own subversion: the answer to "Can machines think?" cannot be found by surveying common usage of "machine" and "think." | Mind 1950 §1, p. 433. | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28. |
| Turing rejects the definitional debate as "absurd" — "such a definition I shall replace the question by another, which is closely related to it and is expressed in relatively unambiguous words." The new question is the Imitation Game. | Mind 1950 §1, p. 433. | **Green** — verified. |
| The Imitation Game is played with three people: a man (A), a woman (B), and an interrogator (C) who may be of either sex. C is in a separate room from A and B and knows them only by labels X and Y. C's task is to determine which is the man and which is the woman. A's object is to make C identify wrongly; B's object is to help. | Mind 1950 §1, p. 433. | **Green** — verified. The original game is gender-disambiguation, not human-vs-machine. |
| Communication is by typed messages: "In order that tones of voice may not help the interrogator the answers should be written, or better still, typewritten. **The ideal arrangement is to have a teleprinter communicating between the two rooms.** Alternatively the question and answers can be repeated by an intermediary." | Mind 1950 §1, p. 433. | **Green** — verified. Direct quotation. |
| The pivot: "We now ask the question, 'What will happen when a machine takes the part of A in this game?' Will the interrogator decide wrongly as often when the game is played like this as he does when the game is played between a man and a woman? These questions replace our original, 'Can machines think?'" | Mind 1950 §1, p. 434. | **Green** — verified. Direct quotation. |
| Note the structural-replacement step: the *human baseline* (the gender game) is what the *machine-substituted game* is measured against. The machine does not have to fool C absolutely — only as often as a man would in the analogous gender game. | Mind 1950 §1, p. 434 (interpretation of the verified quotation). | **Green** for the quoted text; **Yellow** for the interpretation that the gender game *is* the baseline against which machine performance is measured. |

**Scene-level note:** The chapter must not blur the gender game into a "tell the human from the machine" framing. Turing's 1950 setup is a substitution of one disambiguation task (man-vs-woman) for another (A-as-machine-vs-B-as-woman). Whether this gender structure is load-bearing or vestigial is a Conflict Note (Proudfoot 2011 vs. abstracted-test readings). Acknowledge the disagreement; do not pick a side mid-scene.

## Scene 3 — The Argumentative Spine (§2-§6)

Anchored layer: *800-1100 words: The Argumentative Spine* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| §2 "Critique of the New Problem" defends the Imitation Game's design. The decoupling argument: "No engineer or chemist claims to be able to produce a material which is indistinguishable from the human skin... we should feel there was little point in trying to make a 'thinking machine' more human by dressing it up in such artificial flesh." The teleprinter constraint *operationalises* this decoupling — C cannot see, touch, or hear A and B. | Mind 1950 §2, p. 434. | **Green** — verified. Direct quotation. |
| §3-§5 develop the digital-computer / discrete-state-machine reframe: the universality argument that any single discrete-state machine of adequate storage and speed can mimic any other; the Manchester machine's storage parameters as a real-world reference (64 magnetic tracks each with a capacity of 2560, and eight electronic tubes with a capacity of 1280). | Mind 1950 §3-§5, pp. 436-442. | **Green** — verified for section structure and the Manchester-machine reference. |
| §6 "Contrary Views on the Main Question" catalogues nine objections: (1) Theological, (2) "Heads in the Sand," (3) Mathematical (Gödel), (4) Argument from Consciousness, (5) Arguments from Various Disabilities, (6) Lady Lovelace, (7) Argument from Continuity in the Nervous System, (8) Argument from Informality of Behaviour, (9) Argument from Extra-Sensory Perception — each rebutted in turn. The chapter need not lecture on all nine; load-bearing rebuttals are Lady Lovelace (§6.6) and the consciousness/disabilities cluster (§6.4-§6.5). | Mind 1950 §6, pp. 442-454. | **Green** for the catalogue; **Yellow** for any specific sub-objection's full rebuttal text outside the §6 opening and §6.6 (Lady Lovelace) extracted passages. |
| §6.6 Lady Lovelace's objection — that the Analytical Engine "has no pretensions to originate anything." Turing's reply opens with the question whether a mind can be "supercritical" in the sense of an atomic pile: "An idea presented to such a mind that may give rise to a whole 'theory' consisting of secondary, tertiary and more remote ideas." | Mind 1950 §6.6 + opening of §7, pp. 450-455. | **Green** for the supercritical-mind passage; **Yellow** for any verbatim quotation of Lovelace's 1842 *Sketch* itself. |
| §6 Argument from Consciousness — Jefferson's 1949 Lister Oration is one of the views Turing engages here, on the question whether thinking requires inner experience. The chapter can mention this as the live opposition that will return embodied in Jefferson himself in the 1952 BBC broadcast (Scene 5). | Mind 1950 §6.4, pp. 445-447 (cited section); 1949 Lister Oration as background source for Jefferson's view. | **Yellow** for the §6.4 sub-page extraction; **Yellow** for the 1949 Lister Oration as a background source. |

**Scene-level note:** The argumentative spine scene is where the chapter risks turning into a §-by-§ summary of the *Mind* paper. Do not. The scene's narrative job is to show *the shape of Turing's defence* — that the test is constructed to deflect the very objections the §6 catalogue lists. The decoupling argument in §2, the universality argument in §5, and the §6 rebuttals are pieces of one structure: a behavioural test deliberately designed to be immune to the objections that "thinking" requires biology, infallibility, originality, or inner consciousness.

## Scene 4 — The Prediction and the Child Machine (§6 opening, §7)

Anchored layer: *800-1100 words: The Prediction and the Child Machine* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| §6 opens with the famous prediction: "I believe that in about fifty years' time it will be possible, to programme computers, with a storage capacity of about 10⁹, to make them play the imitation game so well that an average interrogator will not have more than 70 per cent chance of making the right identification after five minutes of questioning." | Mind 1950 §6 opening, p. 442. | **Green** — doubly verified (Claude `pdftotext` + Saygin 2000 cross-citation `(Turing, 1950, p. 442)` on Saygin pp. 472-473). |
| The companion claim, in the same §6 opening passage: "at the end of the century the use of words and general educated opinion will have altered so much that one will be able to speak of machines thinking without expecting to be contradicted." | Mind 1950 §6 opening, p. 442. | **Green** — verified. |
| §7 "Learning Machines" reorients the engineering problem: rather than programme an adult mind, programme a child mind subjected to "an appropriate course of education." The child-machine and the education process are framed as two interlocking design problems. | Mind 1950 §7, pp. 454-456. | **Green** — verified. Reuses the 1948 NPL framework (Scene 1) explicitly — Turing's notebook child-mind metaphor in §7 ("Presumably the child brain is something like a notebook as one buys it from the stationer's. Rather little mechanism, and lots of blank sheets") is the 1950 restatement of the 1948 cortex-as-unorganized-machine analogy. | 
| §7 names three components of mind: "(a) The initial state of the mind, say at birth, (b) The education to which it has been subjected, (c) Other experience, not to be described as education, to which it has been subjected." Education uses rewards and punishments; "I have done some experiments with one such child machine, and succeeded in teaching it a few things, but the teaching method was too unorthodox for the experiment to be considered really successful." | Mind 1950 §7, p. 456. | **Green** — verified. Note Turing's modesty about his own experimental work. |
| §7 draws an explicit analogy between the design loop (build child machine → educate → measure → revise) and biological evolution: "Structure of the child machine = hereditary material; Changes of the child machine = mutation; Natural selection = judgement of the experimenter." | Mind 1950 §7, p. 456. | **Green** — verified. Direct quotation. |
| §7 ends with the chess-vs-sense-organs bifurcation: "We may hope that machines will eventually compete with men in all purely intellectual fields. But which are the best ones to start with?... Many people think that a very abstract activity, like the playing of chess, would be best. It can also be maintained that it is best to provide the machine with the best sense organs that money can buy, and then teach it to understand and speak English... Again I do not know what the right answer is, but I think both approaches should be tried." | Mind 1950 §7, p. 460. | **Green** — doubly verified (Claude `pdftotext` + Saygin 2000 cross-citation `(Turing, 1950, p. 460)`). |
| The paper closes: "We can only see a short distance ahead, but we can see plenty there that needs to be done." | Mind 1950 §7, p. 460 (final sentence). | **Green** — verified. |

**Scene-level note:** Scene 4 must not turn the prediction into a scoring exercise ("did Turing get the date right?"). The 70%/five-minute/end-of-century target is a *frequency claim about a population of average interrogators*, not a binary milestone. The scene's narrative job is to show that Turing offered the test as an evaluation protocol the future would administer, with the child-machine programme as the *engineering* path that would produce a system the protocol could evaluate — not as a finish-line claim about AGI.

## Scene 5 — Aftermath: BBC and Reframing

Anchored layer: *500-800 words: Aftermath: BBC and Reframing* (brief.md Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| On 14 January 1952 (re-broadcast 23 January) the BBC Third Programme broadcasts "Can Automatic Calculating Machines Be Said To Think?" — a four-way conversation between Turing, Newman, Jefferson, and Braithwaite. The recording was made on 10 January 1952. | 1952 BBC broadcast, transcript in Copeland ed. 2004 *The Essential Turing*, Ch 14, pp. 487-506; Hodges 1983 standard biographical reference. | **Yellow** — broadcast date and participant list widely confirmed; broadcast text not directly verified by Claude 2026-04-28. |
| In the broadcast, Jefferson advances biological objections to the Imitation Game framing — continuing the line of his 1949 Lister Oration "The Mind of Mechanical Man" (BMJ 1949 vol. 1 (4616), pp. 1105-1110, DOI 10.1136/bmj.1.4616.1105) — and Turing defends the empirical-test framing against those objections. | 1952 BBC broadcast, Copeland 2004 Ch 14, pp. 487-506; 1949 Lister Oration as background source for Jefferson's view. | **Yellow** — broadcast text not directly verified. The 1949 Lister Oration background is also Yellow. |
| Saygin et al 2000 surveys the 50-year reception of the Imitation Game in *Minds and Machines* 10 (4), pp. 463-518. §3 covers 1960s-1970s philosophical responses; §4 covers 1980s computational responses; §5 covers 1990s anthropomorphism, gender, and the Genova-Halpern dispute over how the gender axis maps to the human-vs-machine substitution. | Saygin et al 2000 §3-§5 (pp. 473-513). | **Green** for the survey existence and structure (verified by `pdftotext` 2026-04-28); **Yellow** for any specific named-author claim within the survey. |
| Proudfoot 2011 argues that subsequent commentary stripped out the gender-disambiguation structure on which Turing built the test, and that the resulting "abstracted" Turing Test (where C tries to tell human-from-machine) is a different test from what Turing proposed (where C tries to tell man-from-woman, with a machine playing the part of the man). | Proudfoot 2011, *Artificial Intelligence* 175 (5-6), pp. 950-957 (DOI 10.1016/j.artint.2011.01.006). | **Yellow** — Crossref-verified citation; specific page anchors pending. |

**Scene-level note:** Scene 5's narrative job is to show the test's *empirical-test framing being defended in real time* (the 1952 broadcast) and *its later reception drifting* (the rebrand from "Imitation Game" to "Turing Test"; the gender axis being abstracted away in subsequent commentary). The scene must not turn into a survey of every later use of the test — that material belongs in later chapters where AI critiques (Lighthill, the AI winters) and modern test variants (Loebner Prize, ELIZA, large language models) are properly placed. Layer 5's 500-800-word budget reflects this — half the size of Layers 3 and 4 because the verified evidence is honestly thinner here.

## Cross-scene notes

- **No invented dialogue.** Every quoted sentence must come from a primary source. The 1952 broadcast scene must not paraphrase what "Jefferson said" or "Turing replied" until the Copeland 2004 transcript is anchored.
- **No biography drift.** Turing's institutional position (NPL → Manchester) is enough biographical context. Bletchley, prosecution, and death do not enter this chapter — the book's broader Turing arc handles that material.
- **No Cybernetics polemic.** The chapter must not present the teleprinter constraint as a deliberate strike against cybernetics. Turing's stated reason is operational ("tones of voice may not help the interrogator"); the deliberate-rivalry reading is interpretation, not text. (Mind §1 p. 433.)
- **No Dartmouth pre-emption.** The chapter must not anticipate Ch11's argument that "AI" was named at Dartmouth in 1956. Forward references to Ch11 are sparse pointers ("see Ch11 for the naming event"), not pre-arguments.
- **No "Turing Test as AGI finish-line" framing.** The 70%/five-minute/end-of-century prediction is a frequency claim, not a milestone declaration. The chapter must respect Turing's actual quantitative form rather than the popular slogan version.
- **Where secondary sources conflict** (e.g. Proudfoot 2011 vs. abstracted-test readings on whether the gender axis matters), the chapter should name the disagreement rather than picking a side.
