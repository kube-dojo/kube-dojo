# Brief: Chapter 37 — Distributing the Compute

## Thesis

The compute substrate that the deep-learning revolution would later inherit was not built for machine learning. It was built for web search. Between 2003 and 2004, two papers from Google — Ghemawat, Gobioff, and Leung's *Google File System* (SOSP 2003) and Dean and Ghemawat's *MapReduce* (OSDI 2004) — described an internally deployed pair of systems that made it routine to read and write hundreds of terabytes across thousands of commodity machines while assuming individual machines would fail. Those papers were not source code; they were specifications. Doug Cutting and Mike Cafarella, scaling the Apache Nutch web crawler, used them as exactly that — first re-implementing GFS as the Nutch Distributed File System (NDFS), then porting MapReduce, then in January 2006 splitting the result out of Nutch into a new Apache subproject Cutting named *Hadoop* after a stuffed yellow elephant his two-year-old son had named. The historical move that matters for AI is not the algorithm; it is that, by the time the 2012 ImageNet/AlexNet result triggered industry investment in deep learning, the open-source descendants of those 2003–2004 papers were already running on Yahoo's production homepage. The ML era did not have to invent a planet-scale compute substrate. It rented one that web indexing had already paid for.

## Scope

- IN SCOPE: GFS (2003) and MapReduce (2004) as specifications and as deployed Google systems; the Apache Nutch crawler's 2002–2005 scaling wall; Cutting and Cafarella's NDFS/MapReduce reimplementation; the January 2006 Apache JIRA INFRA-700 split of Hadoop out of Nutch; Cutting's January 2006 move to Yahoo and the naming origin of "Hadoop"; the Hadoop 0.1.0 (April 2006) era through Yahoo's production adoption (verified through Vance's NYT 2009 article); MapReduce's deployment numbers as Google reported them in August 2004 (29,423 jobs, 3,288 TB read, 758 TB intermediate, 193 TB output).
- OUT OF SCOPE: Bigtable, Spanner, Dremel, Mesos, YARN, Spark, and Kubernetes (later chapters in Parts 6 and 9); GPUs and CUDA (Part 7); the deep-learning algorithmic story (Part 7); the modern data-engineering / lakehouse era; Google's later TPU silicon (Ch49). Anything Hadoop became *after* Cloudera's 2008 founding and Yahoo's 2011 Hortonworks spin-out is summarized in one paragraph at most.

## Boundary Contract

This chapter must not present MapReduce/Hadoop as machine-learning infrastructure. They were web-indexing infrastructure that happened to fit a later workload. The MapReduce paper's own list of intended uses — "machine learning problems" appears in Section 6, alongside Google Zeitgeist, Google News clustering, and "extraction of properties of web pages" — is a 2004 footnote, not the system's purpose. The chapter must also avoid Doug Cutting hagiography. Cutting and Cafarella did the engineering work of porting two papers to Java for the open-source world; they did not design the underlying systems, and the GFS/MapReduce designs themselves rest on a much longer distributed-systems literature (Condor, River, NOW-Sort, Active Disks, Charlotte, BAD-FS — all cited in MapReduce's Related Work section).

The chapter must not invent dialogue, lab atmospherics, or internal motives for any participant. Every direct quote must come from a contemporaneous primary source: a paper, a JIRA ticket, a published interview, or an archived article. Reconstructed scenes (e.g., "Cutting reading the Google paper at his desk") are permitted only when at least one primary source supports the broad action and the chapter explicitly flags the reconstruction. The "yellow elephant" anecdote can be told because Cutting himself is on the record about it in the 2009 Vance NYT article and the 2013 Morris CNBC article — but it should not be embellished with details those sources do not contain.

Forward-references are sparse pointers only. The chapter ends with the OSDI paper's own forward-looking sentence (Section 8 conclusions) and a single paragraph on what came next. It does not anticipate Ch38's argument about labeled data, Ch41's GPU work, or Ch49's custom silicon.

## Scenes Outline

1. **The Four-Machine Ceiling (2002–2003).** Cutting and Cafarella's Apache Nutch crawler indexes about 100 web pages per second on a single machine, scales to four, and stalls there. The web in 2003 has on the order of a billion pages. The crawler's failure mode is not the algorithm; it is the distributed-systems plumbing — disk failures, node coordination, restartability — that nobody has the time to write from scratch.
2. **The Specifications (October 2003 – December 2004).** Google publishes GFS at SOSP 2003 and MapReduce at OSDI 2004. The papers describe two production systems running at scale: GFS clusters with over 1,000 storage nodes and 300+ TB of disk; MapReduce running 29,423 jobs and reading 3,288 TB of input data in August 2004 alone. The papers reveal three commitments — commodity hardware as the *assumption* not the optimization, transparent fault tolerance, and a programming model deliberately restricted enough for parallelism to be automatic.
3. **The Open-Source Port (2004–2005).** Cutting and Cafarella treat the GFS paper as a specification and reimplement it in Java as NDFS, the Nutch Distributed File System. In 2005 they port MapReduce on top of NDFS. The work happens inside Apache Nutch as a subdirectory; it is not yet a separate project.
4. **The Yellow Elephant (January–February 2006).** On 28 January 2006, Cutting opens Apache JIRA INFRA-700: *"new mailing lists request: hadoop. The Lucene PMC has voted to split part of Nutch into a new sub-project named Hadoop."* He has just joined Yahoo. The name comes from a stuffed yellow elephant his two-year-old son christened. Hadoop 0.1.0 ships in April 2006. Yahoo bets on it.
5. **The Substrate the AI Era Inherited (2007–2012, briefly).** By 2009, Hadoop is on Yahoo's homepage; Cloudera (2008) and Hortonworks (2011) commercialize it. The chapter's honest close: the deep-learning revolution that arrives in 2012 lands on a stack already battle-tested by web search. The compute that AI needed was waiting for it, named after a toddler's toy.

## Conflict Notes

- **The Hadoop split date.** The Bonaci 2015 timeline gives "February 2006" for the Hadoop subproject extraction. The Apache JIRA INFRA-700 ticket — Cutting's own action — is dated *28 January 2006 05:01 UTC*. Both can be true (the Lucene PMC vote and mailing-list request happened in late January; the actual code extraction and 0.1.0 release ran into February–April). Use the JIRA date as the single anchor for the announcement; treat "February 2006" as the broader release process.
- **Whether "Hadoop" was *first* used as a software name.** Cutting's 2013 CNBC interview is explicit that the name was a toddler's word for the stuffed elephant, *not* a software acronym. The chapter must not claim "Hadoop" stands for anything; it does not.
- **The 5,000 / 6,000 LOC factoring number.** Wikipedia's *Apache Hadoop* article gives "about 5,000 lines of code for HDFS and about 6,000 lines of code for MapReduce" with a footnote chain. Claude's verification could not trace this past the Wikipedia editor's claim itself (the cited Cutting/Cafarella/Lorica O'Reilly piece is paywalled). Treat as illustrative, Yellow.
- **The role of Mike Cafarella vs. Doug Cutting.** Most secondary sources name them as co-founders of the NDFS/MapReduce port. Cafarella was a University of Washington graduate student at the time (per Datanami, citing Bonaci). The chapter should credit both and not attribute the work to Cutting alone. Cafarella's specific division of labor is currently Yellow (no primary anchor verified yet).
- **Levy 2011 *In the Plex*.** The legacy Gemini-drafted chapter cited Levy as a secondary source for the Hadoop origin story. Claude could not verify specific page anchors in Levy 2011 without paywall access. Do not anchor any load-bearing claim to Levy's book until pages are obtained.
