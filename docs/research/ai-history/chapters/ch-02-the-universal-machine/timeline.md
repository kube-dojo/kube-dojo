## Notes on dating

- The May 28 / Aug 28 / Nov 12 chronology in 1936 is fully Green-anchored from Turing's own paper. The receipt-to-read gap of nearly six months — and the appendix coming three months after receipt — is itself the structural evidence that Turing learned of Church's work mid-process.
- The April 1936 Church publication date is fully Green-anchored from Church's own paper (volume cover + April-presentation footnote). The April 1935 AMS *presentation* date (Green) and the April 1936 *publication* date (Green) together establish that Church's ideas were circulating for a full year before Turing's manuscript reached the LMS.
- The "founding date of computability" question is not settled by this timeline. Plausible candidate dates include 1928 (Hilbert-Ackermann), 1931 (Gödel), April 1935 (Church AMS presentation), April 1936 (Church publication), May 1936 (Turing receipt), August 1936 (Turing appendix), 1937 (joint Church-Turing publication record). The chapter's stance per `brief.md` is that 1936 is the year *computability became a mathematical object*, not that any single date is "when computing was invented" — the latter takes engineers, vacuum tubes, and the rest of the book.
- Pagination across editions of secondary works can shift. Hodges 1983 first edition has different page numbers from the 2014 centenary reissue; Meltzer 1962 book pages differ from Hirzel 2000 original-journal-page references. Citations in `sources.md` track edition explicitly.
