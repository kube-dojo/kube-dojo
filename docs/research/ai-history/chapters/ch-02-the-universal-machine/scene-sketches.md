Avoid implying that *Hilbert was destroyed in 1931*; Hilbert's *Programm* was wounded in 1931 and finished in 1936.

**Scene-level note:** The chapter's narrative move at the end of Scene 2 is preparing the reader for Turing 1936 not as "the proof that mattered" but as "the proof that travelled." Church got there first; Church got there via a method that did not — at the time — communicate well to anyone outside lambda-calculus circles. The chapter must not patronize Church: his proof is rigorous and complete. The chapter must be careful to honor Church's priority while honestly explaining why Turing's same-result-different-method had different historical reach.

## Scene 3 — The Paper Tape (May - November 1936)

Anchored layer: **900-1,300 words: Turing's machine model and the unsolvability proof** (`brief.md` Prose Capacity Plan).

| Beat | Anchor | Status |
|---|---|---|
| At King's College Cambridge, the 23-year-old Alan Turing — an elected fellow on the strength of his 1934 dissertation on the Central Limit Theorem — has spent the previous year working on the *Entscheidungsproblem*. | Stanford Encyclopedia "Turing" Section 2 ("alone for a year until April 1936"); Hodges 1983 (specific Cambridge details pending page anchor) | Yellow until Hodges 1983 page anchors land. |
| Turing's manuscript "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the Entscheidungsproblem" is *received* by the London Mathematical Society on 28 May 1936. | Turing 1936 cover-page header p.230: "[Received 28 May, 1936.—Read 12 November, 1936.]" | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28 |
| In §1 (p.231) Turing introduces the *computing machine*: a finite-state device with a tape divided into squares, each capable of bearing a symbol, scanned one at a time. The machine's behavior is determined by its m-configuration and the scanned symbol. | Turing 1936 §1 p.231 ("the machine is supplied with a 'tape' (the analogue of paper) running through it, and divided into sections (called 'squares') each capable of bearing a 'symbol'... the possible behaviour of the machine at any moment is determined by the m-configuration q_n and the scanned symbol S(r)") | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28 |
| In §2 (p.232) Turing distinguishes *automatic machines* (a-machines, behavior wholly determined by configuration) from *choice machines* (c-machines, partially nondeterministic). The chapter focuses on a-machines. | Turing 1936 §2 p.232 ("If at each stage the motion of a machine (in the sense of §1) is completely determined by the configuration, we shall call the machine an 'automatic machine' (or a-machine)") | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28 |
| In §2 (p.233) Turing further distinguishes *circular* machines (which print only finitely many figures of the first kind) from *circle-free* machines (which print infinitely many). Computable numbers are those produced by circle-free machines. | Turing 1936 §2 p.233 ("if a computing machine never writes down more than a finite number of symbols of the first kind, it will be called circular. Otherwise it is said to be circle-free") | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28 |
| In §8 (p.247) Turing proves by diagonal argument: there can be no machine D that, supplied with the S.D of any computing machine M, decides whether M is circle-free. | Turing 1936 §8 p.247 ("Thus both verdicts are impossible and we conclude that there can be no machine D") | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28 |
| Turing then generalizes (§8 pp.247-248): there can be no machine E that decides whether an arbitrary M ever prints a given symbol (e.g. 0). | Turing 1936 §8 pp.247-248 ("We can show further that there can be no machine E which, when supplied with the S.D of an arbitrary machine M, will determine whether M ever prints a given symbol (0 say)") | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28 |
| Modern textbooks call this the **Halting Problem**. Turing himself does not. The "halting problem" name was popularized by Martin Davis in *Computability and Unsolvability* (1958). The chapter may use the modern name but must mark it. | Turing 1936 §8 (uses "circular" and "ever prints a given symbol"); Davis 1958 (later didactic name) | **Green** for Turing's own terms; **Yellow** for the Davis 1958 attribution until that book's specific page is anchored. |
| In §11 (pp.259-262) Turing applies the §8 result to the *Entscheidungsproblem*. He constructs, for every computing machine M, a formula `Un(M)` such that `Un(M)` is provable in the functional calculus K iff M ever prints 0. From §8 there is no general process for the latter, hence no general process for the former: the Hilbert *Entscheidungsproblem* has no solution. | Turing 1936 §11 p.259 ("the Hilbert Entscheidungsproblem can have no solution"); pp.260-262 (the construction of `Un(M)` from `Inst{q_i S_j S_k L q_l}` clauses, Lemma 1, Lemma 2) | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28 |
| Turing's paper is *read* before the LMS on 12 November 1936. | Turing 1936 cover-page header p.230 | **Green** — verified by Claude `pdftotext` 2026-04-28 |

**Scene-level note:** This scene is structurally the chapter's longest because the Turing machine has to be *taught*, not merely cited. The chapter should walk through the machine's anatomy (tape, scanner, m-configuration, table) using Turing's own §1-3 examples. Then the diagonal argument should be sketched without becoming a textbook chapter on computability. Avoid the temptation to "explain" the diagonal proof more than Turing himself does — Turing's §8 phrasing ("by applying the diagonal process argument correctly, we can show that there cannot be any such general process") is more compact than most textbook expositions. Cite Turing's §8 directly.

**Scene-level note:** Scene 5 is the chapter's resolution. The narrative work is: bring Church and Turing into the same room (literally — Princeton, autumn 1936), establish that the formal equivalence is settled, mark the philosophical thesis as a thesis, and pivot forward to the engineering chapters that follow. The chapter must NOT close with a triumphalist "and thus computers were invented" beat. The closing should mirror the boundary contract: the universal machine existed mathematically; the bridge to silicon was still ahead.

## Cross-scene notes

- **No invented dialogue.** Every quoted sentence must come from a primary source (paper, letter, contemporaneous note). The scene sketches mark all primary-quotable passages with explicit page anchors.
- **No "founding moment" framing.** The chapter argues 1936 is the *naming* of computability as a mathematical object — analogous to Ch11's argument that 1956 was the *naming* of AI. Multiple plausible "founding" moments exist (Hilbert 1928, Gödel 1931, Church April 1935, Church April 1936, Turing May 1936, Turing August 1936, joint 1936/1937 publication record, Newman-Turing-at-Princeton autumn 1936). The chapter must not pick one and call it "the moment computing was born."
- **Where secondary sources conflict** (e.g., on the precise date Turing sailed for Princeton, or on whether Newman or someone else first showed Turing the Church paper), the chapter should name the disagreement rather than picking a side without evidence.
- **The chapter should NOT include** a textbook explainer of λ-calculus, of Gödel numbering, or of the diagonal argument as abstract techniques. Those belong in a foundations-of-computability textbook. Ch2's narrow focus is the *historical* moment of the parallel discovery and the universal-machine architectural insight.
