# Brief: Chapter 2 — The Universal Machine

## Thesis

In 1936, two men working on opposite sides of the Atlantic — Alonzo Church at Princeton and Alan Turing at Cambridge — independently and almost simultaneously proved that David Hilbert's *Entscheidungsproblem* has no solution. Church got there first by a few months, using λ-calculus; Turing got there second, using a single hypothetical machine reading instructions off an infinite tape. Both proofs are formally equivalent. What made the chapter's century-defining moment was not which proof was right, but which idea travelled. Turing's "computing machine" — a single fixed device that reads its program off the same tape that holds its data — turned an abstract impossibility result into the architectural blueprint that engineers would, within a decade, build out of vacuum tubes. Five years before any physical stored-program computer existed, the universal machine had been fully described mathematically, and the long shadow of Hilbert's failed program had been replaced by a positive concept: software.

## Scope

- IN SCOPE: Hilbert's *Entscheidungsproblem* as posed in the *Grundzüge der theoretischen Logik* (Hilbert & Ackermann, 1928, ch.3); Gödel 1931 and the precise scope of what *Vollständigkeit* + *Widerspruchsfreiheit* incompleteness left standing for *Entscheidbarkeit*; Church's April 1935 AMS presentation and April 1936 *American Journal of Mathematics* publication of "An Unsolvable Problem of Elementary Number Theory"; Church's 1936 *Journal of Symbolic Logic* note extending the result to first-order logic; Turing's May 1936 manuscript receipt, August 1936 appendix, November 1936 LMS reading, and 1937 publication of "On Computable Numbers"; the a-machine / computing-machine model (§§1-2); circular vs. circle-free machines and the §8 diagonal-process unsolvability theorem; the universal computing machine (§6); the §11 reduction of the *Entscheidungsproblem* to the print-0 problem; Turing's appendix proof of λ-definability ↔ computability; Turing's arrival at Princeton in autumn 1936 and the formalization (post-1936) of what others would name the Church-Turing thesis.
- OUT OF SCOPE: Bletchley Park codebreaking (Ch3 / Ch7); Von Neumann's 1945 *First Draft* and the EDVAC stored-program architecture (Ch8); the actual physical realization of computing machines (Ch7, Ch8, Ch9); Post 1936 "Finite Combinatory Processes" (currently out of scope but flagged in `sources.md` Open Items as a candidate for inclusion); Turing 1948 "Intelligent Machinery" and 1950 "Computing Machinery and Intelligence" (Ch10); Cantor's diagonal argument and Russell's paradox (background, mentioned only as needed for Gödel/Turing); intuitionism vs. formalism more broadly (Hilbert's full *Programm* is touched only insofar as Gödel hit it).

## Boundary Contract

This chapter must not present Turing as the founder of computability theory. Church got there first. The chapter must not collapse Church's λ-calculus proof and Turing's machine-model proof into "Turing's result"; they are two different proofs of the same negative answer. The chapter must not claim that Gödel's incompleteness theorem "answered" the *Entscheidungsproblem* — Gödel 1931 demolished completeness and consistency-from-within, but Turing himself draws the distinction explicitly in §11 of his 1936 paper: the *Entscheidungsproblem* was still standing after Gödel.

The chapter must not anachronize the language. Turing called his machines "a-machines" (automatic machines) or "computing machines"; the name "Turing machine" was Church's coinage in his 1937 *JSL* review. Turing's §8 result is about *circle-freeness* and the *print-symbol problem*; "the halting problem" is a later didactic name (Martin Davis, *Computability and Unsolvability*, 1958). The chapter may use the modern terms but must mark them as later reformulations.

The chapter must not claim that the universal computing machine *was* the architectural blueprint of EDVAC, ENIAC, or any specific physical machine. The connection between Turing's UCM and Von Neumann's stored-program architecture runs through several intervening steps and belongs to Chapter 8. The chapter's stance: Turing 1936 *mathematically described* a machine in which programs and data live on the same tape; *whether and how* engineers later built such machines is a separate story.

The chapter must not invent dialogue or interior life for any historical figure. Every quoted sentence must come from a primary source (paper, letter, contemporaneous note). Reconstructed atmospherics are permitted only where multiple independent sources converge.

## Scenes Outline

1. **The Unsolvable Problem (1928-1931).** Hilbert poses the *Entscheidungsproblem* via the *Grundzüge der theoretischen Logik* (Hilbert & Ackermann 1928, ch.3) and the parallel 1928 Bologna ICM address; the problem asks for a "general process" deciding the provability of any first-order formula. Three years later, Kurt Gödel's 1931 *Monatshefte* paper proves Theorem (Proposition) VI: any ω-consistent recursive extension of *Principia Mathematica* contains arithmetical propositions that are neither provable nor disprovable within the system. Hilbert's *Vollständigkeit* and *Widerspruchsfreiheit* goals are demolished. But — Turing himself notes this in §11 of his 1936 paper — the *Entscheidungsproblem* (decidability) is *not* settled by Gödel.
2. **The Parallel Discovery (April 1935 - April 1936).** Alonzo Church, at Princeton, builds on his and Kleene's λ-calculus work (Annals of Math. 1933; Am. J. Math. 1935) to formalize "effective calculability" as λ-definability (equivalently, recursiveness). On April 19, 1935 he presents "An Unsolvable Problem of Elementary Number Theory" to the AMS; it appears in *Am. J. Math.* 58(2) in April 1936. The paper proves Theorem XIX (no recursive function decides convertibility of two λ-formulas) and concludes with a corollary: the *Entscheidungsproblem* of *Principia Mathematica* is unsolvable. Church publishes the more compact *Journal of Symbolic Logic* note in March 1936. The result is correct, complete, and almost entirely lost on the engineering world.
3. **The Paper Tape (May - November 1936).** At King's College Cambridge, the 23-year-old Alan Turing — having spent a year on the same problem — submits "On Computable Numbers, with an Application to the *Entscheidungsproblem*" to the London Mathematical Society. Receipt: 28 May 1936. He defines the **a-machine** (automatic machine): a finite-state device with a one-way-extending tape, a scanner, and a finite table of m-configurations (§§1-2, pp.231-233). He distinguishes circular from circle-free machines (§2, p.233). He then proves by diagonal argument (§8, pp.246-248) that no machine can decide whether an arbitrary description-number is the D.N of a circle-free machine, generalizing this to the print-symbol problem ("there can be no machine E... will determine whether M ever prints a given symbol (0 say)"). In §11 (pp.259-262) he reduces the *Entscheidungsproblem* to this latter problem and concludes the Hilbert decision problem has no solution.
4. **The Birth of Software (§6, June 1936).** Inside the same paper, in §6 (pp.241-242), Turing describes a single machine — the universal computing machine — that, "supplied with a tape on the beginning of which is written the S.D of some computing machine M," computes the same sequence as M. The instructions defining M's behavior are encoded as symbols on the tape, alongside whatever data M operates on. The hardware is fixed; the program is data; the data is also program. This is the architectural concept that — five years before any physical stored-program machine existed — would become the substrate of every software industry to follow. The chapter must say what Turing's text does say (a single machine reads any computable sequence given the right description-number) and must NOT say what later authors say it implies (this *was* the EDVAC/ENIAC architecture).
5. **The Princeton Connection (August 1936 - August 1938).** Sometime in summer 1936 — between his manuscript's May receipt and the appendix dated 28 August 1936 — Turing learns of Church's parallel work. He writes the appendix to his own paper (pp.263-265), proving the formal equivalence of λ-definability and Turing-computability. The appendix is signed from "The Graduate College, Princeton University." Turing remains at Princeton through 1938, completing his Ph.D. ("Systems of Logic Based on Ordinals") under Church. Church accepts (in his 1937 *JSL* review of Turing's paper, p.43) that Turing's machine model "has the advantage of making the identification with effectiveness in the ordinary (not explicitly defined) sense evident immediately." The two formulations — λ-definable, Turing-computable, and Gödel/Kleene-recursive — collapse into one mathematical concept. The *philosophical* claim that this concept captures intuitive "effective calculability" — the **Church-Turing thesis** — is asserted by Church in §7 of his 1936 paper and is *not a theorem*. The chapter must preserve the theorem-vs-thesis distinction.

## Conflict Notes

- **Priority order vs. imagination-capturing.** Strict publication ordering: Church April 1936 → Turing 1936 (received 28 May 1936, read 12 November 1936, published 1937). Church was first by a few months. The chapter's *interpretive* framing — that Turing's machine model "captured the engineering imagination" Church's λ-calculus did not — is supported by Church's own 1937 *JSL* review acknowledging Turing's machine model "evidently" captures effectiveness. The chapter must preserve both the priority order *and* the imagination-capturing claim, with citations.
- **Hilbert-Ackermann edition.** Turing 1936 cites "Hilbert and Ackermann's *Grundziige der Theoretischen Logik* (Berlin, 1931), chapter 3." First edition is dated 1928 (Springer); a 1931 second printing of the 1st edition exists; the 2nd revised edition was 1938. The chapter should cite the 1928 first edition for the original formulation, with a footnote about Turing's 1931-printing citation.
- **"Turing machine."** Turing called his construction the *a-machine* or *computing machine*. The name "Turing machine" was Church's coinage in his 1937 *JSL* review. The chapter must avoid anachronism in *Turing's* voice.
- **"Halting problem."** Turing's §8 result is about circle-freeness and the print-symbol problem; "halting problem" is the later didactic name (Davis 1958). The chapter may use the modern term but must mark it.
- **What Gödel proved.** The first incompleteness theorem (Theorem VI, Gödel 1931) demolished Hilbert's *Vollständigkeit* and *Widerspruchsfreiheit* goals; it did NOT settle the *Entscheidungsproblem* (decidability), as Turing himself notes in §11 p.259-260. The chapter must phrase Gödel's role carefully and not over-attribute.
- **"Church-Turing thesis."** The *theorem* of equivalence (λ-definable ↔ recursive ↔ Turing-computable) is a theorem. The *thesis* (that this formal notion captures intuitive "effective calculability") is a philosophical claim. The chapter must preserve this distinction.
- **Wiener's invention vs. discovery.** Some popular AI-history accounts call 1936 "the year computing was invented." The chapter's stance: 1936 is the year computability became a *mathematical* object. The *invention* of computing as a physical engineering practice took the rest of the 1940s.
