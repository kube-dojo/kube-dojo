## Conflict Notes

- **Hilbert-Ackermann edition citation.** Turing 1936 cites the book as "(Berlin, 1931), chapter 3." The 1st edition is dated 1928 (Springer, Berlin); a 2nd printing of the 1st edition appeared in 1931; the 2nd revised edition was 1938. The chapter should cite *Grundzüge der theoretischen Logik*, 1st ed. (Berlin: Springer, 1928) for the original formulation, with a footnote that Turing himself cited the 1931 reprint. Do not draft a sentence that depends on the exact 1928-vs-1931 distinction unless the book itself is accessed.
- **"Turing machine" naming.** Turing called his construction the "a-machine" (automatic machine) — verbatim, p.232. The name "Turing machine" was first applied by Church in his 1937 JSL review (per Stanford Encyclopedia anchor). The chapter must avoid anachronism: Turing did not write about "Turing machines"; he wrote about "computing machines" or "a-machines."
- **"Halting problem" naming.** Turing's §8 result is about *circle-freeness* (whether a machine prints infinitely many symbols of the first kind) and the *print-symbol generalization* (whether a machine ever prints 0). The phrase "Halting Problem" was popularized by Martin Davis in *Computability and Unsolvability* (1958). Turing did not use this phrase. The chapter may use "halting problem" as the modern reformulation but must mark the terminological distinction.
- **Priority order.** Church's "Unsolvable Problem" was *presented* to AMS on April 19, 1935 — fourteen months before Turing's manuscript was *received*. Church was *published* April 1936; Turing was *received* May 28, 1936. So in the strict publication ordering, Church is first. But the deeper historical claim — that Church's λ-calculus method "did not capture the engineering imagination" the way Turing's machine model did — is interpretive. Stanford Encyclopedia anchors this interpretation (Copeland citing Church 1937a: 43 — Church himself agreed Turing's machine model was more "evidently" effective). The chapter should preserve both the priority order and the imagination-capturing claim with citation discipline.
- **What Gödel's 1931 result *proved* and what it *implied***. Gödel 1931 proved Theorem VI — the existence of arithmetical propositions undecidable within ω-consistent recursive extensions of *PM*. This is the *first* incompleteness theorem. The *second* incompleteness theorem — that consistency of *PM* cannot be proved within *PM* — appears in Gödel 1931 but is in section 3-4, which the Hirzel translation omits. The chapter should phrase Gödel's blow to Hilbert's program carefully: Gödel demolished the *Vollständigkeit* and *Widerspruchsfreiheit* claims (completeness and consistency-from-within), but did not by itself answer the *Entscheidungsproblem* (decidability) — Turing's own paper draws this distinction explicitly (§11 p.259-260).
- **Church's λ-calculus proof "did not capture imagination"** — this is the chapter's interpretive framing, not a fact. The Stanford Encyclopedia (Copeland) supports a softer version: "Church's approach did not mention computing machinery, whereas Turing's introduced the 'Turing machine'... computability by a Turing machine has the advantage of making the identification with effectiveness in the ordinary (not explicitly defined) sense evident immediately" (Church 1937a: 43, quoted in Stanford Encyclopedia). The chapter may use this framing if it cites the source and avoids implying Church's proof was less rigorous.
