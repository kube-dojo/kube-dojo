# Brief: Chapter 20 - Project MAC

## Thesis

Project MAC was the institutional and technical machine room behind much of
1960s and early-1970s AI. It did not "solve" intelligence; it changed the
working conditions under which AI could be attempted. ARPA-funded
time-sharing, CTSS, Multics, ITS, PDP-6/PDP-10 hardware, MACLISP, MATHLAB,
PLANNER, and early ARPANET links turned computation from a batch resource into
an interactive research environment. That environment made symbolic AI feel
practical: programs could be edited, run, debugged, shared, and connected while
communities of researchers watched each other's tools evolve.

## Scope

This chapter should bridge Ch19's rule-based/knowledge-engineering turn to
Ch21's commercial expert-system fortune. The focus is infrastructure: why
time-sharing and institutional funding mattered for AI's style, speed, and
ambition. Project MAC should be treated as a shared platform containing multiple
currents: Corbato/Fano systems work, Minsky's AI group, Multics, ITS,
MACLISP/MATHLAB/MACSYMA, PLANNER, and ARPANET/resource-sharing experiments.

## Boundary Contract

- Do not turn Project MAC into a pure AI laboratory. The 1964-65 report frames
  it as both Machine-Aided Cognition and Multiple-Access Computer systems,
  with participation from many MIT departments and laboratories.
- Do not make time-sharing merely a cost-saving trick. Fano's 1967 framing and
  the Project MAC reports present it as a new mode of man-machine interaction,
  community memory, tool sharing, and real-time research practice.
- Do not collapse CTSS, Multics, and ITS into one operating system. CTSS was
  the IBM 7094 service/laboratory foundation; Multics was the second-generation
  utility-scale system; ITS was the AI group's different PDP-6/PDP-10
  environment optimized for high-interaction AI work.
- Do not claim Project MAC invented all interactive computing. Use "major,"
  "central," "large-scale," or "influential"; acknowledge CTSS roots at the MIT
  Computation Center and related time-sharing work elsewhere.
- Do not narrate Ch21's DEC/XCON business story here. Ch20 may show why
  infrastructure made knowledge-intensive programs plausible; Ch21 owns the
  corporate expert-system fortune.
- Do not narrate Ch22's Lisp-machine commercialization here. Ch20 may mention
  PDP-10/MACLISP and the AI hardware appetite as preconditions; the Lisp
  machine bubble belongs to Ch22.
- Do not treat ARPA funding as neutral background. The project existed under
  ONR/ARPA contracts, later with NSF and other agency support; funding shaped
  scale, equipment, and the ability to sustain a research community.

## Narrative Spine

1. **The Utility Dream:** Fano/Licklider/Corbato imagine computing as a shared
   intellectual utility rather than a batch machine.
2. **A Project, Not a Lab:** Project MAC is organized to pull MIT departments
   into one experiment without making everyone abandon their home labs.
3. **CTSS as Practice Ground:** the IBM 7094 CTSS system becomes both a service
   facility and a laboratory for man-machine interaction.
4. **Forking Futures:** Multics and ITS reveal two different futures for
   time-sharing: reliable utility service versus intensely interactive AI work.
5. **AI on the Machine:** MACLISP, MATHLAB/MACSYMA, PLANNER, vision, robotics,
   and symbolic manipulation show why AI needed this kind of environment.
6. **Networked Utility:** Multics/ITS and ARPANET work move the utility idea
   from campus time-sharing toward resource sharing across sites.
7. **Handoff to the Boom:** by the early 1970s, Project MAC has split/reorganized
   into systems, programming technology, and automatic programming currents; the
   infrastructure is ready for Ch21's expert-system commercialization.
