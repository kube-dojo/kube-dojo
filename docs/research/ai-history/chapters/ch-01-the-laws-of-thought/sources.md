## Conflict Notes

- **Distance of the rain walk: two vs three miles.** Macfarlane 1916 (p.39) and MacTutor say "two miles." Britannica online says "three miles." Both are tertiary or near-contemporary; Macfarlane's chain (via Robert Harley's R.S.L. *Proceedings* notice) is the older lineage. Use the locution "from his residence to the College in heavy rain" without committing to a distance, OR follow Macfarlane and footnote Britannica.
- **Date of the rain walk.** Britannica gives "November 24, 1864"; Macfarlane and MacTutor give only "one day in 1864." Treat the November-24 date as Yellow (single-source) and use the locution "in late November 1864" unless a second source surfaces.
- **Cause of death wording.** Macfarlane: "feverish cold which soon fell upon his lungs." SEP and Britannica: "pneumonia." Both are clinically consistent (pneumonia was the descendant condition). Use "pneumonia" — it's the disease modern readers will recognise — and footnote the Macfarlane wording if quoting at length.
- **Boole's age at death.** Macfarlane: "in the 50th year of his age" (Victorian ordinal-year idiom). SEP and Britannica: "age 49." The two are equivalent: Boole was born November 2, 1815, and died December 8, 1864, so he was 49 years and 36 days old. Use "age 49."
- **Mary Everest Boole's role after 1864.** This is the chapter's most-overstated claim in the legacy literature. The *evidence-supported* version: she became a mathematics educator and librarian at Queen's College London, invented curve stitching, and wrote on mathematical pedagogy from the 1880s. The *unsupported* version: she "preserved George's mathematical legacy" or "kept his work in academic circulation." The chapter must take the first version and explicitly mark the second as folk history, on the open-access evidence currently available.
- **"Class algebra vs propositional logic" framing.** Boole's *Laws of Thought* contains BOTH: primary propositions (class algebra) in Chs. II-X, and secondary propositions (propositional logic) in Chs. XI-XII. The chapter's previous "Boole did class algebra; modern computer scientists rediscovered propositional logic" framing is wrong-by-omission. The correct framing is: Boole's `+` was a partial operation defined only for disjoint classes; Jevons (1864) replaced the partial `+` with an inclusive `+` and abandoned Boole's framework; the modern Boolean algebra of computer science descends from Jevons-Peirce-Schröder, not from Boole 1854 unmodified.
- **"Engineers ignored Boole for decades" framing.** Not anchored in any verified source consulted. The substantive Boolean-algebra-→-switching-circuit transmission is the subject of Ch3 (the physical bridge) and is out of scope here.
