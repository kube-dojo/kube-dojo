# Brief: Chapter 1 — The Laws of Thought

## Thesis

In 1854 the self-taught Lincoln mathematician George Boole published *An Investigation of the Laws of Thought* and, in fewer than ninety pages of its core symbolic chapters, did three things almost no one before him had attempted: he asserted that the operations of reasoning have *fundamental laws*; he assigned those laws to a calculus of two values (`0` for the empty class, `1` for the universe of discourse) and three operations (`×` for class intersection, `+` for the union of disjoint classes, `1 − x` for class complement); and he proved that the whole calculus is governed by a single second-degree equation, `x² = x`, which he called the *law of duality* and which forces — as Boole himself argued in print — that the natural mode of analysis under his system is dichotomy.

Boole's `+` was a *partial* operation, defined only when its operands were disjoint; he himself supplied the inclusive workaround `x + y(1 − x)` in Chapter IV, but he did not redefine `+` as union. That redefinition was W. S. Jevons's 1864 move, after Boole had refused it in correspondence; modern Boolean algebra — the kind taught in computer-science textbooks — descends from Jevons, Peirce, and Schröder, not from Boole 1854 unmodified. This chapter is the story of the calculus Boole *did* publish: a calculus of classes and propositions, presented as a "science of the mind," ten years before its author walked through November rain in Cork and never recovered.

The chapter's claim against the founding myth is modest but firm. Boole did not invent computer science. He did not anticipate switching circuits (that bridge belongs to Chapter 3). He did not engineer a thinking machine (he did not engineer anything). What he did was harder to see at the time and easier to see now: he gave the first system in which "every operation of language as an instrument of reasoning" could be represented in symbols and solved by algebraic manipulation, and he wrote, *in the book itself*, that this algebra reduces to two values because reasoning itself reduces to dichotomy. That single argument — Boole's, not Shannon's, not McCulloch's, not McCarthy's — is the chapter's load-bearing claim, and it is anchored to specific pages of his own book.

## Scope

- **IN SCOPE**: George Boole's biography from Lincoln through Cork (1815-1864), with the verified facts only; the publication and structure of *An Investigation of the Laws of Thought* (1854); the calculus itself — symbols, operations, the law of duality; the partial-`+` problem and Boole's own `x + y(1 − x)` workaround in Chapter IV; W. S. Jevons's 1864 abandonment of Boole's framework and his inclusive-`+` reinvention (as the historical hinge that takes us from Boole's class algebra to modern Boolean algebra); Boole's elections (FRS 1857), marriage (Mary Everest, 1855), and death (December 8, 1864, of pneumonia, in Ballintemple, Co. Cork); the documented post-1864 life of Mary Everest Boole as a mathematics educator and inventor of curve stitching; one explicit honesty close, naming what the chapter does *not* claim about her.
- **OUT OF SCOPE**: physical implementation of Boolean logic in switching circuits (Ch 3, *The Physical Bridge*); Shannon's 1937 MIT thesis (Ch 3); the formalisation of computation by Turing, Church, Post, Gödel (Ch 2, *The Universal Machine*); the McCulloch-Pitts neural abstraction (Ch 5); cybernetic feedback (Ch 6); the broader history of formal logic from Aristotle through Frege, Peano, Whitehead, and Russell — these are referenced only where load-bearing for Boole's own context (e.g. the Aristotelian principle of contradiction, which Boole quotes by name in Prop. IV); the long historiographical question of whether Boolean algebra was "dormant" between Boole and Shannon — that framing is *out of scope and rejected* (see Boundary Contract).

## Boundary Contract

This chapter must not present Boole as the inventor of computer science or as the unconscious engineer of the digital computer. He framed his project, in the opening paragraph of *Laws of Thought*, as the investigation of "the fundamental laws of those operations of the mind by which reasoning is performed" — a science of the mind, on a par with (and arguably superior to) the contemporary science of probability. The chapter is allowed to draw the line from Boole's calculus to later switching-circuit work *only by gesture*; it must not anticipate the Shannon-1937 argument, must not dramatise Boole-as-prophet-of-computers, and must close before Boole's death without making either a switching-circuit prediction or a "dormant blueprint" claim.

This chapter must not reproduce the popular framing that "Boolean algebra was ignored by engineers for eighty years until Shannon." That framing is contested by the verified historical record (Jevons 1864, C. S. Peirce from 1867, Schröder's *Vorlesungen* 1890-95, Frege, Peano, Whitehead, Russell — none of them in scope here, but their existence falsifies the dormancy claim). Mathematical logicians worked continuously on and against Boole's calculus throughout the period; what was *not* attempted before Shannon was the engineering interpretation, and that absence is Chapter 3's story, not Chapter 1's.

This chapter must not promote Mary Everest Boole into the role of "preserver of George Boole's mathematical legacy." The verified open-access record describes her doing other things — librarianship at Queen's College London, mathematical pedagogy for women and children, the invention of curve stitching, and a long publishing career on the philosophy of mathematical education — none of which is the preservation of Boole's *logic*. The chapter must take the documented version of her life and explicitly note, in the prose, that the "preserver" framing is folk history rather than documented history on the present open-access evidence. If MacHale 1985/2014 supplies primary anchors that revise this, Scene 5 can be rewritten — until then, the chapter stays honest about what we do and do not know.

This chapter must not invent dialogue, internal states, or workshop scenes for any participant. Every quoted sentence must come from a primary source — Boole's text, Macfarlane's lecture, the SEP entry, or a successfully-anchored archival document. Atmospheric reconstruction (e.g. the rain walk to Queen's College Cork) is permitted only because the rain walk itself is documented in three independent biographical sources (Macfarlane 1916, MacTutor, Britannica); the *details* of route, distance, and date that diverge across those sources must be drawn carefully or omitted.

The chapter is the first of the AI-history book and is the first place readers will encounter the project's standard of evidence. It is therefore especially important here to demonstrate the standard rather than describe it: the prose should let primary anchors do the work, name the workings of the calculus from Boole's own pages, and refuse the rhetorical flourish (Boole-as-prophet, Mary-as-preserver, Boole-as-dormant-blueprint) wherever the flourish would outrun the source.

## Scenes Outline

1. **Lincoln (1815-1849).** Boole's birth in Lincoln on November 2, 1815; the shoemaker-tradesman father with a sideline in optical instruments and mathematics; the self-taught Latin and Greek; the school-teaching from age sixteen; his own school in Lincoln from age twenty; the 1844 *Transactions of the Royal Society of London* memoir on a general method in analysis and the Royal Medal it earned; the 1847 *Mathematical Analysis of Logic*; the 1849 appointment to the chair of mathematics at the new Queen's College, Cork, in Ireland.
2. **The Calculus of Classes (Chapter II of *Laws of Thought*).** Boole's three-element symbol system (literal symbols `x, y, ...`; signs of operation `+, −, ×`; the sign of identity `=`); the move from "names" to "classes" (the substantive "men," the adjective "good things") and the famous "white sheep" worked example; the commutative law `xy = yx`; the strict-disjointness restriction on `+` ("the words 'and,' 'or,' interposed between the terms descriptive of two or more classes of objects, imply that those classes are quite distinct, so that no member of one is found in another").
3. **Two Symbols, One Law (Chapter III).** The assignment of `0` to "Nothing" and `1` to "the Universe" as the two limits of class extension; the definition of complement `1 − x` as "the contrary or supplementary class of objects"; the derivation of the *fundamental law of thought* `x² = x`, equivalently `x(1 − x) = 0`; Boole's identification of this equation with the Aristotelian principle of contradiction; his crucial observation that *because the equation is of the second degree, the natural mode of analysis under his system is dichotomy* — Boole's own argument for why two values, not three or many, is the right number; his coinage of the name "law of duality" for `x² = x`.
4. **The Disjoint-Sum Problem (Chapter IV; Jevons 1864).** Boole's own awareness that strict-disjoint `+` is restrictive; his Chapter IV §7-8 construction `x + y(1 − x)` for the inclusive case (the form that modern readers recognise as `x ∨ y`); a note that Boole's *Laws of Thought* itself contains both class algebra (primary propositions, Chs. II-X) and propositional logic (secondary propositions, Chs. XI-XII), so the popular "Boole did class algebra; computer scientists use propositional logic" framing is wrong by omission; W. S. Jevons's 1864 letter proposing `x + x = x` (i.e. redefining `+` as union); Boole's reply ("it is not true that in Logic `x + x = x`"); Jevons's subsequent abandonment of Boole's framework and his construction of "the first version of modern Boolean algebra" (per Burris, SEP §5.2); the chapter's load-bearing point — the Boolean algebra of modern computer science descends through Jevons, Peirce, and Schröder, not directly from Boole 1854 unmodified.
5. **Cork, Rain, and What We Owe Mary Everest Boole.** The 1855 marriage to Mary Everest, niece of Sir George Everest (after whom the mountain is named) and of Boole's Cork colleague Dr. Ryall; the 1857 election to Fellowship of the Royal Society; the November 1864 walk in heavy rain from his residence to Queen's College Cork; the lecture given in wet clothes; the descent into a "feverish cold which soon fell upon his lungs" (Macfarlane); death at Ballintemple on December 8, 1864, of pneumonia, at age 49. An honest close on what we know about Mary Everest Boole's life *after* 1864 — Queen's College London librarian, mathematics educator, inventor of curve stitching, writer on mathematical pedagogy — and an explicit refusal of the popular but undocumented "she preserved his mathematical legacy" framing.

## Conflict Notes

- **Distance of the rain walk: two miles vs three miles.** Macfarlane 1916 (p.39) and MacTutor say two miles; Britannica online says three miles. Macfarlane's chain (via Robert Harley's 1866-67 R.S.L. *Proceedings* notice) is the older lineage. Use a locution that does not commit to a distance, OR follow Macfarlane and footnote.
- **Date of the rain walk.** Britannica gives "November 24, 1864"; Macfarlane and MacTutor give only "one day in 1864." The November-24 date is single-source (Yellow) and should be used carefully or replaced by "in late November 1864."
- **Cause of death wording.** Macfarlane: "feverish cold which soon fell upon his lungs." SEP and Britannica: "pneumonia." The two are clinically consistent. Use "pneumonia" in the prose and footnote Macfarlane's wording if quoting the rain walk at length.
- **Age at death.** "In the 50th year of his age" (Macfarlane) is the Victorian ordinal-year idiom for "age 49"; SEP and Britannica give "age 49." Use "age 49."
- **Mary Everest Boole's role.** Folk history says "she preserved his mathematical legacy"; MacTutor records pedagogy, librarianship, and curve stitching, not preservation of Boole's logic; Macfarlane 1916 (p.40) is contemporaneously hostile, dismissing her published work as "a paradoxical book of the false kind." The chapter takes the MacTutor-supported record and explicitly flags the folk-history framing as unsupported on present open-access evidence. If MacHale 1985/2014 revises this, Scene 5 is rewritten.
- **Class algebra vs propositional logic.** *Laws of Thought* contains both — primary propositions (Chs. II-X) are class algebra, secondary propositions (Chs. XI-XII) are propositional logic. The chapter must reflect this rather than reproduce the lossy popular framing.
- **Modern-Boolean-algebra lineage.** The chapter takes Burris's SEP framing: modern Boolean algebra descends from Jevons (1864) through Peirce and Schröder, not from Boole 1854 unmodified.
