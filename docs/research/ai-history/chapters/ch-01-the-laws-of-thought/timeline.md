# Timeline: Chapter 1 — The Laws of Thought

Dates verified against Macfarlane 1916 (Project Gutenberg #9942), Boole 1854 (Project Gutenberg #15114), the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy entry on Boole (Burris 2024), the MacTutor (St Andrews) entries on George and Mary Everest Boole, and the Britannica online entry on Boole. See `sources.md` for full citations and verification status.

## In scope (the chapter narrates these)

- **1815, November 2** — George Boole born in Lincoln, Lincolnshire, England, to a tradesman who pursued mathematics and the construction of optical instruments as an avocation. *(Macfarlane p.37; SEP §1; Britannica.)*
- **c. 1827** — At age twelve, Boole's metrical translation of an ode of Horace, published by his father in a local Lincoln journal, prompts a literary controversy when a neighbouring schoolmaster denies the work could have been done by one so young. *(Macfarlane pp.37-38.)*
- **c. 1831-1835** — Boole works as an assistant teacher, first at Doncaster in Yorkshire, then at Waddington near Lincoln, devoting his leisure to modern languages and patristic literature. *(Macfarlane p.38.)*
- **c. 1835** — At age twenty, Boole opens his own school in Lincoln. *(Macfarlane p.38.)*
- **1844** — Boole's memoir on a general method in analysis is published in the *Transactions of the Royal Society of London*; the Society awards him a Royal Medal. *(Macfarlane pp.38-39.)*
- **1847** — *The Mathematical Analysis of Logic* is published. *(SEP §3 lists this as MAL = Mathematical Analysis of Logic 1847; Macfarlane p.39 dates it "1848" by what is most likely a clerical slip.)*
- **1849** — Boole appointed to the chair of mathematics at the newly founded Queen's College, Cork, Ireland (one of the three Queen's Colleges established under the 1845 Act, the other two being Belfast and Galway). *(Macfarlane p.39; SEP §1.)*
- **1853, November 30** — Boole signs the preface to *An Investigation of the Laws of Thought* at "5, Grenville-place, Cork" — the dating attached to the printed front-matter of the 1854 edition. *(Boole 1854, preface, Project Gutenberg PDF p.iv.)*
- **1854** — *An Investigation of the Laws of Thought, on which are founded the Mathematical Theories of Logic and Probabilities* is published in London by Walton & Maberly, dedicated to Dr. John Ryall, Vice-President and Professor of Greek at Queen's College, Cork. *(Boole 1854, title page; Macfarlane p.39.)*
- **1855** — Boole marries Mary Everest, niece of Sir George Everest (after whom the mountain is named) and of Dr. Ryall. *(SEP §1; Macfarlane p.39; Britannica.)*
- **1857** — Boole elected Fellow of the Royal Society. *(MacTutor (St Andrews); Britannica.)*
- **1864, November (late, possibly the 24th — Yellow, Britannica only)** — Boole walks from his residence to Queen's College Cork in heavy rain (two miles per Macfarlane and MacTutor; three miles per Britannica) and lectures in his wet clothes. He develops a "feverish cold which soon fell upon his lungs." *(Macfarlane p.39; MacTutor; Britannica.)*
- **1864, December 8** — Boole dies at Ballintemple, County Cork, of pneumonia, aged 49 (Macfarlane: "in the 50th year of his age," the Victorian ordinal-year idiom for the same age). *(SEP §1; Macfarlane p.39; Britannica.)*

## Adjacent / forward-pointing (the chapter does *not* narrate these, but cites them as boundary references)

- **1864 (post-Boole)** — W. S. Jevons publishes work that proposes redefining `+` so that `x + x = x` and abandons Boole's strict-disjoint `+` framework, constructing what Burris (SEP §5.2) calls "the first version of modern Boolean algebra." Boole had rejected the move in correspondence with Jevons before his death: "it is not true that in Logic `x + x = x`" (SEP §5.1). *(SEP §§5.1-5.2.)*
- **1866-67** — Robert Harley's biographical notice of Boole appears in the *Proceedings of the Royal Society of London* (the source from which Macfarlane 1916 draws his biographical material; Macfarlane p.40). *Yellow — not yet directly consulted.*
- **1870** — Benjamin Peirce (Harvard) renames Boole's "law of duality" as the "idempotent law." *(SEP §3.3.)*
- **1880s onward** — Mary Everest Boole publishes prolifically on the philosophy of mathematical education and the pedagogy of mathematics for women and children; she invents "curve stitching" (string geometry) as a teaching aid. *(MacTutor (St Andrews) Mary Everest Boole entry.)* Listed here, but **not** narrated as preservation of George Boole's logical legacy — see `brief.md` Boundary Contract and `open-questions.md` Q3.
- **1890-95** — Schröder publishes *Vorlesungen über die Algebra der Logik*, the volume in which the modern Jevons-Peirce-Schröder Boolean algebra reaches its mature form. *Out of scope; cited only to falsify the "dormant for 80 years" framing.*
- **1916** — Alexander Macfarlane's *Lectures on Ten British Mathematicians of the Nineteenth Century* (lecture 4, "George Boole," delivered April 19, 1901) is published posthumously, drawing on Robert Harley's 1866-67 *R.S.L. Proceedings* notice for biographical data. *(Project Gutenberg #9942.)* This is the chapter's main biographical primary anchor.

## Excluded entirely

- The Shannon 1937 thesis and the engineering interpretation of Boolean algebra in switching circuits — Chapter 3 (`The Physical Bridge`).
- The 1879 *Begriffsschrift* of Frege, the *Principia Mathematica* of Whitehead and Russell, and the Peano arithmetic — adjacent histories of formal logic that do not involve Boole's own work directly.
- The "AI history" framing of Boole's text — Boole was not writing toward an artificial-intelligence research programme. The chapter is allowed to gesture at the long arc only in its closing paragraphs and only by indicating what *did not yet exist* in 1864 (machinery), not by importing later vocabulary back onto Boole.
