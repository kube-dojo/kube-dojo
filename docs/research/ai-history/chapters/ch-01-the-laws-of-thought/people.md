# People: Chapter 1 — The Laws of Thought

Verified facts only. Where the verified record is thinner than the popular legend, this file says so.

## Verified Protagonists

### George Boole (1815-1864)

- Born November 2, 1815, in Lincoln, Lincolnshire, England, to a tradesman of limited means with a serious avocational interest in mathematics and the construction of optical instruments. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.37; SEP §1.)*
- Self-taught; his father instructed him in elementary mathematics and the construction of optical instruments, and a friendly Lincoln bookseller in the Latin grammar; the rest — Greek, German, French, then Laplace's *Mécanique céleste* and Lagrange's *Mécanique analytique* — Boole worked through alone, alongside his school-teaching and (from age twenty) the running of his own school in Lincoln. *(Macfarlane 1916, pp.37-38.)*
- 1844: published a memoir on a general method in analysis in the *Transactions of the Royal Society of London* and was awarded a Royal Medal. *(Macfarlane 1916, pp.38-39.)*
- 1847: published *The Mathematical Analysis of Logic*. *(SEP §3.)*
- 1849: appointed to the chair of mathematics at the newly founded Queen's College, Cork, Ireland. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.39; SEP §1.)*
- 1854: published *An Investigation of the Laws of Thought, on which are founded the Mathematical Theories of Logic and Probabilities* (Walton & Maberly, London), dedicated to Dr. John Ryall, Vice-President and Professor of Greek at Queen's College, Cork. *(Boole 1854, title page; Macfarlane 1916, p.39.)*
- 1855: married Mary Everest. *(SEP §1; Macfarlane 1916, p.39.)*
- 1857: elected Fellow of the Royal Society. *(MacTutor; Britannica.)*
- 1864, December 8: died at Ballintemple, County Cork, of pneumonia, aged 49, after walking through heavy rain in late November (Britannica gives November 24) and lecturing in wet clothes. *(SEP §1; Macfarlane 1916, p.39; Britannica.)*

In the chapter, Boole's voice is allowed only when supported by direct quotation from his own writing — *Laws of Thought* itself, which is the strongest such source the chapter can cite. The chapter must not invent his motives, his thoughts on cybernetics, or his reaction to the rain in 1864.

### Mary Everest Boole (1832-1916)

- Niece of Sir George Everest (Surveyor General of India 1830-43; the mountain was named for him in 1865) and of Dr. John Ryall (Vice-President of Queen's College Cork, the colleague to whom George Boole dedicated *Laws of Thought*). *(SEP §1; Macfarlane 1916, p.39; Britannica.)*
- Married George Boole in 1855; they had five daughters together. *(SEP §1; Britannica.)*
- After George Boole's death in December 1864, became a librarian at Queen's College, London (with the support of Frederick Denison Maurice). *(MacTutor: Mary Everest Boole entry.)*
- Taught mathematics to women and children using methods derived from her own tutor Monsieur Deplace; invented "curve stitching" (string geometry) as a pedagogical tool. *(MacTutor: Mary Everest Boole entry.)*
- From the 1880s onward, published prolifically on the philosophy of mathematical education and on a sometimes-mystical philosophy of mind; titles include *Logic Taught By Love* (1889) and *The Preparation of the Child for Science* (1904). *(MacTutor: Mary Everest Boole entry. Yellow on titles until directly consulted.)*

The chapter's load-bearing claim about Mary Everest Boole is what the *MacTutor* entry says she did: librarianship, teaching, curve stitching, and educational philosophy. The popular framing — "she preserved George Boole's mathematical legacy" / "kept his work in academic circulation" — is not anchored in any open-access source consulted for this contract; the only near-contemporary opinion the chapter has access to is Macfarlane 1916 p.40, which is explicitly hostile ("a paradoxical book of the false kind"). The chapter therefore takes the documented version of her life and explicitly notes that the preservation framing is folk history. See `open-questions.md` Q3 and `brief.md` Boundary Contract.

## Verified Supporting Cast

### John Ryall

- Vice-President and Professor of Greek at Queen's College, Cork. *(Boole 1854, dedication page; Macfarlane 1916, p.39.)*
- The dedicatee of *Laws of Thought*. *(Boole 1854, dedication.)*
- Mary Everest's uncle on her mother's side, and the human bridge between Boole's Cork colleagues and the Everest family. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.39: "perhaps as a result of the dedication, he married Miss Everest, the niece of that colleague.")*

### Sir George Everest (1790-1866)

- Surveyor General of India, 1830-1843. *(Britannica online; standard biographical sources.)*
- The Himalayan peak Peak XV was renamed in his honour in 1865, just after Boole's death. *(Britannica online.)*
- Mary Everest Boole's uncle. *(Britannica online; SEP §1; Macfarlane 1916, p.39.)*

The chapter is allowed to mention George Everest only as a biographical fact for Mary Everest Boole and only briefly. Sir George is not a character in the chapter; the mountain's renaming a year after Boole's death is a coincidence of the historical calendar, not a meaningful narrative beat.

### Duncan F. Gregory (1813-1844)

- A younger member of the Cambridge mathematical school and the founding editor of the *Cambridge Mathematical Journal*. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.38.)*
- Encouraged Boole's early publication and suggested (unsuccessfully) that Boole take the regular mathematical course at Cambridge. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.38.)*
- Boole's first major mathematical influence after his self-taught reading of Laplace and Lagrange. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.38.)*

Gregory belongs in Scene 1 only, as a name in Boole's path to the *Trans. R.S.L.* memoir of 1844. The chapter must not promote him to a major character.

### Augustus De Morgan (1806-1871)

- London mathematician, contemporary of Boole, and the friend whose extant correspondence with Boole is the strongest near-contemporary witness to Boole's place in the British logical tradition. *(Macfarlane 1916, pp.39-40, on De Morgan's Boole correspondence and his "shall be glad to see his work [Laws of Thought] out, for he has, I think, got hold of the true connection of algebra and logic" comment to Hamilton.)*
- The chapter is allowed to use De Morgan only as a *witness* — a brief reference confirming that Boole's work was recognised by the most-qualified British contemporary observer. The chapter must not give De Morgan substantive page-time; he is a character in Chapter 4 (statistical roots) and elsewhere.

## Forward-Reference Cast (named only as boundary markers; no narrative beats)

- **William Stanley Jevons (1835-1882)** — the man who, after Boole's death in 1864, redefined `+` as inclusive disjunction and built what Burris (SEP §5.2) calls the first version of modern Boolean algebra. The chapter mentions Jevons in Scene 4 as the historical hinge from Boole's framework to the modern one, and stops there.
- **Charles Sanders Peirce (1839-1914)** — Harvard logician who, with Schröder, carried the Jevons-modified Boolean algebra into the late nineteenth century. Mentioned in Scene 3 only as the namer of the "idempotent law" in 1870 (SEP §3.3) and not otherwise in scope here.
- **Benjamin Peirce (1809-1880)** — C. S. Peirce's father, Harvard mathematician; cited only for the "idempotent law" coinage (SEP §3.3).
- **Ernst Schröder (1841-1902)** — author of *Vorlesungen über die Algebra der Logik* (1890-95). Out of scope; cited only as evidence against the "dormant for 80 years" framing.
- **Robert Harley** — the friend of Boole who wrote the 1866-67 *Proceedings of the Royal Society of London* biographical notice that Macfarlane 1916 explicitly draws on (Macfarlane p.40). The chapter does not narrate Harley but credits him as the lineage of biographical material.
- **Claude Shannon (1916-2001)** — Out of scope. The chapter explicitly does not anticipate Shannon. He is a Chapter 3 character.
- **Alan Turing (1912-1954)** — Out of scope. Chapter 2.

## Excluded

- Aristotle is mentioned only because Boole himself quotes the Aristotelian principle of contradiction in Greek in Prop. IV (Boole 1854 Ch. III §15 footnote, book p.35). The chapter cites Boole's own quotation; it does not provide a parallel reading of the *Metaphysics*.
- Frege, Whitehead, Russell, Peano — out of scope; their work is the subject of separate chapters or sits outside the AI-history book's concern.
