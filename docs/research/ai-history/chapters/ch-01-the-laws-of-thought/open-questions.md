# Open Questions: Chapter 1 — The Laws of Thought

This file lists the questions whose answers would let the chapter's Yellow rows in `sources.md` move toward Green, plus the historiographical questions whose answers would let the chapter take a firmer position.

## Q1 — What does Hailperin 1986 actually argue, on which pages?

The class-algebra-vs-propositional-logic reading of Boole is associated in the secondary literature with Theodore Hailperin's *Boole's Logic and Probability* (Reidel, 2nd ed. 1986). Gemini's earlier PR #425 cited "pp. 61-63" without independent verification, and that anchor was removed from this contract. The substantive claim is independently Green via Boole 1854 (Chs. II-IV) plus the Stanford Encyclopedia of Philosophy (Burris, §4 and §6.3), so prose drafting can begin without Hailperin — but the chapter is stronger if Hailperin's specific page anchor is supplied. **What is needed:** library access to Hailperin 1986 (or 1976 first edition); identification of the page range that develops the partial-algebra reading of Boole's `+`; addition of the page anchor to `sources.md`. Tractable but not blocking.

## Q2 — What can be anchored in MacHale 1985 / 2014?

Desmond MacHale's *George Boole: His Life and Work* (Boole Press, 1985; Cork University Press revised ed. 2014) is the definitive book-length biography. Several Scene 1 and Scene 5 anchors that currently rest on Macfarlane 1916 alone could be cross-confirmed and deepened. **What is needed:** library or used-book access to MacHale; in particular, the specifics of the rain-walk date (which Britannica alone gives as November 24, 1864), the Boole household at Cork in 1864, and any documentary evidence for the post-1864 disposition of Boole's mathematical archive. Tractable; would let several Yellow rows in `sources.md` move to Green.

## Q3 — What did Mary Everest Boole *actually* do with George Boole's mathematical archive?

This is the chapter's most contentious historiographical question and the load-bearing one for Scene 5. The popular legend says she "preserved his mathematical legacy." The verified open-access record (MacTutor entry on Mary Everest Boole) describes her doing other things — librarianship at Queen's College London, mathematics teaching for women and children, the invention of curve stitching, and a long career of writing on mathematical pedagogy and philosophy. Macfarlane 1916 p.40 is contemporaneously hostile, describing her published work as "a paradoxical book of the false kind." The chapter currently takes the documented version and explicitly flags the legend as folk history.

**What would change this position?** Documentary evidence of Mary Everest Boole's specific actions on George Boole's logical work — correspondence with academic mathematicians arranging republication, letters to the Royal Society about his papers, archived family donations to a library, an introduction or editorial role in any republication of *Laws of Thought* between 1864 and her death in 1916. Some of this material may be in the Boole family papers (held by Cork University Library and elsewhere); some may be in MacHale 1985/2014. **What is needed:** archival access (Boole family papers, Royal Society of London archive) or, at minimum, a careful read of MacHale on Mary Everest Boole specifically. **Status until answered:** the chapter holds Scene 5's "preservation" framing at Red and explicitly flags this in the prose.

## Q4 — Is the Robert Harley 1866-67 *Proceedings of the Royal Society* obituary digitally accessible?

Macfarlane 1916 p.40 explicitly cites Robert Harley's biographical notice in the *Proceedings of the Royal Society of London* as the source for most of his Boole biographical data. Harley was Boole's friend and a near-contemporary witness; if his obituary is digitised by the Royal Society or by JSTOR, several Scene 1 / Scene 5 anchors that currently rest on Macfarlane (a 1901 lecture published 1916) could be promoted to true primary status, and the lineage shortened by half a century. **What is needed:** a search of the Royal Society's *Proceedings* index for vol. 15 (1866-67), pp. i-xxiv; check JSTOR availability and open-access status. Tractable.

## Q5 — Should Scene 4 cite Grattan-Guinness 2000 directly?

Ivor Grattan-Guinness's *The Search for Mathematical Roots, 1870-1940* (Princeton, 2000) is the standard scholarly history of the Jevons-Peirce-Schröder transmission. The scene's load-bearing claim — that modern Boolean algebra descends from Jevons (1864) onward, not from Boole 1854 unmodified — is independently Green via SEP §§5.1-5.2. Grattan-Guinness would deepen the citation but is not essential. **What is needed:** library access; identification of the chapter or page range covering the Jevons-Peirce hinge. Polish item, not a blocker.

## Q6 — Does the date "November 24, 1864" for the rain walk have a second source?

Britannica gives this date; Macfarlane and MacTutor do not specify the date. **What is needed:** a second open-access or accessible source that either confirms or refutes November 24. Cork institutional records, weather logs from the Cork meteorological station for late November 1864, or a passing mention in MacHale 1985/2014 would all work. Until then, the date is Yellow and the chapter uses "in late November 1864" if it needs to commit, or omits the specific date.

## Q7 — What is the actual distance from Boole's residence to Queen's College Cork?

The 1864 rain-walk distance is reported as two miles by Macfarlane 1916 and by MacTutor, and as three miles by Britannica online. Boole's residence at the time is ascertainable from Cork directories or the Boole papers. **What is needed:** a verifiable address for Boole's Cork residence in November 1864; geocoding to Queen's College Cork (now University College Cork) via a contemporary 1860s street map. The discrepancy is small and not load-bearing; the chapter currently uses a locution that does not commit. Could become Green with one focused archival check.

## Q8 — Is the chapter's "secondary propositions = propositional logic" framing in fact load-bearing for the AI-history arc?

A historiographical question rather than a sources question. The chapter uses Burris's framing (SEP §6.3) that Boole's "secondary propositions" are recognisable as propositional logic. The AI-history book's later chapters work mostly with propositional logic and predicate logic; the question is whether the chapter should make this connection a centerpiece (giving Boole credit for the form of logic that AI later relied on) or whether the connection should be one paragraph and Scene 4's main weight should stay on the disjoint-`+` / Jevons hinge. **What is needed:** an editorial decision; the sources support either treatment. Currently Scene 4 keeps the propositional-logic frame as a one-paragraph reframing rather than a centerpiece, on the principle that load-bearing claims for Ch1 should be about Boole's own book and not about later vocabulary.

## Q9 — Should the chapter quote Aristotle's Greek directly?

Boole quotes the *Metaphysica* III.3 in Greek in his Prop. IV footnote (book p.35, footnote 1). The chapter's prose has the option to (a) reproduce the Greek faithfully, (b) translate to English in the prose with the Greek in a footnote, or (c) cite Boole's own English paraphrase ("It is impossible that the same quality should both belong and not belong to the same thing"). **What is needed:** an editorial decision; all three are defensible. Recommendation: option (c) for accessibility, with a footnote pointing the reader to Boole's own page-35 footnote and to *Metaphysica* III.3.

## Q10 — Does the AI-history book have a single canonical translation policy for nineteenth-century French / German / Greek primary sources?

Boole reads Laplace and Lagrange in French; he quotes Aristotle in Greek; later chapters in the book may cite Frege in German, Cantor in German, etc. **What is needed:** a book-level editorial policy. The chapter currently translates Boole's English-prose passages verbatim and gives English glosses for the few Greek and French phrases. If the book's editor sets a different policy, this chapter follows it.

## What is *not* an open question

- **The Project Gutenberg #15114 PDF as a faithful witness to Boole 1854.** It is. The 2022 MiKTeX regeneration preserves the 1854 typesetting's pagination in running heads; cross-spot-checked against the LaTeX source (`15114-t.tex`) and against Internet Archive scans of the 1854 first edition.
- **Whether Boolean algebra was "dormant for 80 years" until Shannon.** It was not — Jevons (1864), C. S. Peirce (from 1867), Schröder (1890-95), Frege, Peano, Whitehead, Russell all worked on or with Boolean algebra in the period. The chapter does not draft this beat. *(See `brief.md` Boundary Contract; `sources.md` Conflict Notes.)*
- **Whether Boole "anticipated" Shannon's switching-circuit interpretation.** He did not, and the chapter does not draft this beat. The interpretation belongs to Chapter 3.
