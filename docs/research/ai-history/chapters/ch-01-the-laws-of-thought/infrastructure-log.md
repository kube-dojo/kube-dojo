# Infrastructure Log: Chapter 1 — The Laws of Thought

This chapter is the only one in the AI-history book whose load-bearing infrastructure is **paper, ink, and the Royal Mail**. *Laws of Thought* (1854) is a mathematical text, not a machine; everything Boole built was a system of symbols on a page, intended for a human reader equipped with a pencil. The chapter's "infrastructure" therefore is publishing, postal correspondence, and the small-scale academic logistics of a brand-new Irish college, not switching circuits or computing hardware. Anything technological the chapter says about machinery must be off-stage and gestural — Chapter 3 (`The Physical Bridge`) is where the engineering interpretation enters.

## Per-Scene Infrastructure

### Scene 1 — Lincoln, self-instruction, the road to Cork

- **Books available to Boole as a self-taught provincial mathematician (c. 1830-1845).** Macfarlane 1916 p.38 names two specifically: Laplace's *Mécanique céleste* (in French) and Lagrange's *Mécanique analytique* (also in French). Boole's path into modern higher mathematics was through these French texts in the original language, supported by Latin and Greek he had also taught himself. *(Macfarlane 1916, pp.37-38.)*
- **The *Cambridge Mathematical Journal* (founded 1837)** as the publication channel that admitted Boole's early work, edited by Duncan F. Gregory. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.38.)*
- **The *Transactions of the Royal Society of London* (founded 1665)** as the channel that admitted Boole's 1844 memoir on a general method in analysis, the publication that earned him the Royal Medal. *(Macfarlane 1916, pp.38-39.)*
- **The Queen's Colleges of Ireland (established under the 1845 Queen's Colleges Act).** Three colleges (Belfast, Cork, Galway) opened in 1849; Boole was appointed to the chair of mathematics at the Cork college. The Cork college was a non-denominational, government-founded institution in a country whose existing university provision (Trinity College Dublin, Maynooth) was sectarian. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.39; SEP §1.)*

### Scene 2 — The calculus of classes (Chapter II of *Laws of Thought*)

- **Boole's symbolic infrastructure** consists of three element-classes, enumerated in Chapter II Proposition I §4 of the book itself (book p.18): literal symbols `x, y, ...` for things; signs of operation `+, −, ×` for the combination or resolution of conceptions; the sign of identity `=`. The page is, in this strict sense, the infrastructure of the chapter. *(Boole 1854, Ch. II Prop. I §4, book p.18.)*
- **No physical apparatus.** Boole's text contains no diagrams of circuits, no truth tables in the modern sense, no proposed machinery. The "white sheep" example (Ch. II §6, book p.20) is a *thought example*, worked on paper. *(Boole 1854, Ch. II §6, book p.20.)*

### Scene 3 — Two symbols, one law (Chapter III)

- **The two values `0` and `1`.** Boole assigns them in Chapter III Proposition II §13 (book pp.33-34) as Nothing and Universe. The fact that exactly two values are needed is *derived* in the same chapter from the second-degree equation `x² = x` — Boole's own argument for why dichotomy is the natural method (Ch. III §15, book p.35). The chapter must let Boole's argument do the work; it must not project Shannon-era binary back onto the 1854 page. *(Boole 1854, Ch. III Prop. II §13, book pp.33-34; Ch. III §15, book p.35.)*
- **The sign system as a complete formal infrastructure.** With the three-element symbol system (Ch. II Prop. I) plus the two interpretations `0 = Nothing, 1 = Universe` (Ch. III Prop. II), Boole has — *in the text itself, on paper* — everything he needs to mechanise propositional and class reasoning. The mechanisation he never built. The chapter's honesty is to say what is *on the page* and stop.

### Scene 4 — The disjoint-sum problem and the inclusive-or workaround

- **Boole's `+` is a partial operation.** Chapter II §11 (book p.23, equation 3, lines 1248-1264 of the layout-text extraction) restricts `+` to disjoint classes: "the words 'and,' 'or,' interposed between the terms descriptive of two or more classes of objects, imply that those classes are quite distinct, so that no member of one is found in another." *(Boole 1854, Ch. II §11, book p.23.)*
- **The inclusive workaround `x + y(1 − x)`** is constructed in Chapter IV §7-8 (book pp.40-41, lines 2023-2054). Boole himself supplies the form modern readers would call `x ∨ y`. *(Boole 1854, Ch. IV §7-8, book pp.40-41.)*
- **The Jevons hinge (1864).** What Jevons did, after corresponding with Boole, was to *redefine the symbol* `+` so that `x + x = x` and abandon Boole's partial-operation framework. The infrastructure here is private correspondence (Boole's reply, "it is not true that in Logic `x + x = x`," survives via SEP §5.1) and Jevons's published response. *(SEP §§5.1-5.2.)*
- **Secondary propositions = propositional logic.** *Laws of Thought* contains both class algebra (Chs. II-X, primary propositions) and propositional logic (Chs. XI-XII, secondary propositions). The chapter must reflect this. *(Boole 1854, Chs. XI-XII, book pp.124-142; SEP §6.3.)*

### Scene 5 — Cork, rain, and the post-Boole record

- **Queen's College Cork as a workplace.** The college sits on the western edge of Cork city, on the south bank of the River Lee. Boole's residence was within walking distance — Macfarlane and MacTutor say two miles, Britannica says three. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.39; MacTutor; Britannica.)*
- **The route, the rain, the lecture.** "One day in 1864 he walked from his residence to the College, a distance of two miles, in the drenching rain, and lectured in wet clothes." *(Macfarlane 1916, p.39.)* Britannica gives the date as November 24, 1864 — a Yellow anchor; Macfarlane and MacTutor do not specify.
- **The disease.** SEP and Britannica name the cause as pneumonia. Macfarlane gives the etiological narrative ("feverish cold which soon fell upon his lungs"). The two are clinically consistent. *(SEP §1; Macfarlane 1916, p.39; Britannica.)*
- **Mary Everest Boole's post-1864 institutional life.** Queen's College, London (where she was librarian under F. D. Maurice); private mathematics teaching for women and children; her own published output from the 1880s, on mathematical pedagogy and philosophy. *(MacTutor: Mary Everest Boole entry.)*

## Infrastructure the chapter does NOT touch

- **Switching circuits, relays, and the engineering interpretation of Boolean algebra.** Chapter 3 (`The Physical Bridge`).
- **The Difference Engine, the Analytical Engine, and Babbage.** Adjacent history not in the AI-history book's chapter ladder for Part 1.
- **Telegraphy and binary signalling.** Adjacent infrastructure but not part of Boole's own world; mentioned only if a reviewer specifically asks for the mid-nineteenth-century communications context, and only to set Boole's *paper-and-ink* infrastructure against it.
- **Truth tables in the modern (Wittgenstein/Post 1921-23) sense.** Out of scope; Boole's tabular work in Chs. VI-IX of *Laws of Thought* is a different (and much earlier) construction with different conventions.
