# Scene Sketches: Chapter 1 — The Laws of Thought

Five scenes, mapped 1:1 to the layers of the Prose Capacity Plan in `brief.md`. Every beat in this file must be supported by an anchored entry in `sources.md`. Beats marked "OFF-PAGE" are background context, set briefly but not narrated as scenes.

## Scene 1 — Lincoln, self-instruction, the road to Cork

**Capacity layer:** 600-900 words. **Anchor chain:** Macfarlane 1916 pp.37-39; SEP §1 *Life and Work*.

- **Setting**: Lincoln, Lincolnshire, England, in the 1820s and 1830s. A small cathedral city; Boole's father a tradesman whose serious avocational interest is mathematics and the construction of optical instruments. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.37.)*
- **Action 1**: The twelve-year-old Boole's metrical translation of an ode of Horace appears in a Lincoln journal, planted there by his proud father, and a neighbouring schoolmaster writes in to deny that one so young could have produced it. *(Macfarlane 1916, pp.37-38.)*
- **Action 2**: Between sixteen and twenty Boole is an assistant teacher at Doncaster and at Waddington-near-Lincoln, devoting his leisure to modern languages and patristic literature; at twenty he opens his own school in Lincoln to support his ageing parents. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.38.)*
- **Action 3**: Boole works through Laplace's *Mécanique céleste* and Lagrange's *Mécanique analytique* in the original French, by himself, alongside the school. Out of his Lagrange notes a first mathematical memoir emerges. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.38.)*
- **Action 4**: Duncan F. Gregory, founding editor of the *Cambridge Mathematical Journal*, helps Boole into print. Gregory and others suggest Boole take the regular Cambridge mathematics course; Boole declines because he cannot afford to leave his parents and his school. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.38.)*
- **Action 5**: The 1844 *Transactions of the Royal Society of London* memoir on a general method in analysis — applying the Cambridge "separation of symbols" technique to differential equations with variable coefficients — earns Boole a Royal Medal. *(Macfarlane 1916, pp.38-39.)*
- **Action 6**: 1847 — *The Mathematical Analysis of Logic*, a slim volume responding to the Hamilton-De Morgan controversy on the quantification of terms. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.39; SEP §3.)*
- **Action 7**: 1849 — appointment to the chair of mathematics at the new Queen's College, Cork. The Cork environment ("more suitable", in Macfarlane's understatement) gives Boole the time he had not had in Lincoln. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.39; SEP §1.)*
- **OFF-PAGE**: the Cambridge mathematical school he never joined; the ecclesiastical career he had once considered.
- **Closing beat**: Boole at his Cork desk in late 1853, signing the preface of the book he has been preparing for five years at "5, Grenville-place, Cork, Nov. 30th. 1853." *(Boole 1854, preface, Project Gutenberg PDF p.iv.)*

## Scene 2 — The Calculus of Classes (Chapter II of *Laws of Thought*)

**Capacity layer:** 800-1,100 words. **Anchor chain:** Boole 1854 Ch. II §§4-11 (book pp.18-23); SEP §4.

- **Opening**: Chapter I of *Laws of Thought* states the project — "to investigate the fundamental laws of those operations of the mind by which reasoning is performed; to give expression to them in the symbolical language of a Calculus." *(Boole 1854, Ch. I §1, book p.1.)* The chapter quotes this verbatim.
- **Action 1 — the three-element symbol system**: Chapter II Proposition I §4 enumerates the elements: literal symbols `x, y, ...` for things; signs of operation `+, −, ×` for combination and resolution; the sign of identity `=`. *(Boole 1854, Ch. II Prop. I §4, book p.18.)*
- **Action 2 — from name to class**: §6 (book p.20) — "Let *x* represent 'all men,' or the class 'men.'" The "white sheep" worked example, verbatim, in Boole's own text. *(Boole 1854, Ch. II §6, book p.20.)*
- **Action 3 — multiplication as intersection, with commutativity**: §7 derives `xy = yx` (book pp.20-21, equation 1). *(Boole 1854, Ch. II §7.)*
- **Action 4 — the strict-disjointness restriction on `+`**: §11 (book p.23) — "the words 'and,' 'or,' interposed between the terms descriptive of two or more classes of objects, imply that those classes are quite distinct, so that no member of one is found in another." Equation (3): `x + y = y + x`, valid only for disjoint classes. *(Boole 1854, Ch. II §11, book p.23.)* This is the beat that distinguishes Boole's `+` from modern inclusive-OR.
- **OFF-PAGE**: the longer narrative of why Boole is committed to disjoint `+` (it is the conjunction "and" / "or" "in strictness" — a linguistic-philosophical claim about how natural language uses the words). The chapter can gesture at this without making it a scene.
- **Closing beat**: Boole has, by the end of Chapter II, given a calculus of classes. He has not yet given the rules that make it work. That is Chapter III.

## Scene 3 — Two Symbols, One Law (Chapter III)

**Capacity layer:** 900-1,200 words. **Anchor chain:** Boole 1854 Ch. III Props. II-IV §§13-16 (book pp.33-35); SEP §3.3.

- **Opening**: Chapter III is titled "Derivation of the Laws." It opens by observing that the literal symbols of Logic are universally subject to the law `x² = x`, and that of all numerical symbols only `0` and `1` satisfy it. *(Boole 1854, Ch. III, opening of Prop. I-II, book p.33.)*
- **Action 1 — `0` is Nothing, `1` is Universe**: Proposition II §13 establishes "the respective interpretations of the symbols 0 and 1 in the system of Logic are Nothing and Universe." *(Boole 1854, Ch. III Prop. II §13, book pp.33-34.)*
- **Action 2 — the complement is `1 − x`**: Proposition III §14 — "If x represent any class of objects, then will 1 − x represent the contrary or supplementary class of objects, i.e. the class including all objects which are not comprehended in the class x." *(Boole 1854, Ch. III Prop. III §14, book p.34.)*
- **Action 3 — the fundamental law**: Proposition IV §15 — `x² = x`, equivalently `x(1 − x) = 0`, is the algebraic form of the Aristotelian principle of contradiction. Boole quotes Aristotle in Greek in a footnote (book p.35, footnote 1) — *Metaphysica* III.3, "It is impossible that the same quality should both belong and not belong to the same thing." *(Boole 1854, Ch. III Prop. IV §15, book pp.34-35.)*
- **Action 4 — the second-degree argument**: §15 closes with Boole's most-quotable line, and the chapter's load-bearing pull-quote: "it is a consequence of the fact that the fundamental equation of thought is of the second degree, that we perform the operation of analysis and classification, by division into pairs of opposites, or, as it is technically said, by dichotomy." *(Boole 1854, Ch. III §15, book p.35.)* Boole goes on to argue that *if* the equation had been of the third degree, "the mental division must have been threefold in character, and we must have proceeded by a species of trichotomy, the real nature of which it is impossible for us, with our existing faculties, adequately to conceive."
- **Action 5 — the name "law of duality"**: §16 (book p.35) — "The law of thought expressed by the equation (1) will, for reasons which are made apparent by the above discussion, be occasionally referred to as the 'law of duality.'" *(Boole 1854, Ch. III §16, book p.35.)* Boole's own term for what later (1870) Benjamin Peirce would rename the "idempotent law" *(SEP §3.3)*.
- **OFF-PAGE**: the long mid-twentieth-century philosophical argument over whether human reasoning is *really* dichotomous — out of scope; Boole's claim is *about his calculus*, not about cognition.
- **Closing beat**: with Chapter III closed, Boole has — on paper — a calculus of two values, three operations, and one fundamental law. He has, in this strict sense, *finished the symbolic core of the book* in fewer than twenty pages of running text. The remaining nineteen chapters are applications. The chapter prose may, at this point, reflect briefly on what has and has not been built: a calculus, not a machine; a science of reasoning, not an engineering blueprint.

## Scene 4 — The Disjoint-Sum Problem (Chapter IV; Jevons 1864)

**Capacity layer:** 700-900 words. **Anchor chain:** Boole 1854 Ch. IV §§7-8 (book pp.40-41); SEP §§5.1-5.2; SEP §6.3.

- **Opening**: a brief return to the partial-`+` problem set up in Scene 2 — Boole's `+` is defined only when its operands are disjoint. *(Boole 1854, Ch. II §11.)*
- **Action 1 — Boole's own inclusive workaround**: Chapter IV §7-8 (book pp.40-41) constructs `x + y(1 − x)` for the non-disjoint case. The construction is in Boole's text: "the classes denoted by x and y are exclusive, by `x + y(1 − x)` when they are not [exclusive]." *(Boole 1854, Ch. IV §7-8, book p.41.)*
- **Action 2 — secondary propositions = propositional logic**: a one-paragraph reframing — *Laws of Thought* contains both class algebra (Chs. II-X, primary propositions) and propositional logic (Chs. XI-XII, secondary propositions). The popular framing "Boole did class algebra; modern computer scientists rediscovered propositional logic" is wrong by omission. *(Boole 1854, Chs. XI-XII; SEP §6.3.)*
- **Action 3 — the Jevons hinge**: in 1864 W. S. Jevons writes to Boole proposing `x + x = x` — i.e. that `+` should be redefined as inclusive disjunction (set union). Boole replies: "it is not true that in Logic `x + x = x`." Jevons subsequently "abandoned [Boole's algebra of logic] to create the first version of modern Boolean algebra." *(SEP §§5.1, 5.2.)*
- **Action 4 — the lineage**: the Boolean algebra of modern computer science is the Jevons-Peirce-Schröder development, not Boole 1854 unmodified. The chapter says this directly. The 1870 renaming of "law of duality" → "idempotent law" by Benjamin Peirce is the small, dateable beat that punctuates the transition. *(SEP §3.3.)*
- **OFF-PAGE**: Schröder's 1890-95 *Vorlesungen* is named only to falsify the "dormant for 80 years" myth, not narrated.
- **Closing beat**: by the end of 1864 the Jevons argument has displaced the Boolean partial-`+`, and the algebra modern computer scientists know is on its way. Boole, at this date, is dead. The chapter pivots to Scene 5.

## Scene 5 — Cork, Rain, and What We Owe Mary Everest Boole

**Capacity layer:** 500-700 words. **Anchor chain:** Macfarlane 1916 p.39; SEP §1; MacTutor (Boole) and MacTutor (Mary Everest Boole); Britannica.

- **Opening**: 1855, Boole marries Mary Everest, niece of Sir George Everest and of Boole's Cork colleague Dr. Ryall. The Booles have five daughters in the next decade. *(SEP §1; Macfarlane 1916, p.39; Britannica.)*
- **Action 1 — Royal Society election**: 1857, Fellow of the Royal Society. *(MacTutor; Britannica.)*
- **Action 2 — the rain walk**: in late November 1864 (Britannica gives November 24, single-source Yellow), Boole walks from his residence to Queen's College Cork in heavy rain — two miles per Macfarlane and MacTutor, three miles per Britannica — and lectures in his wet clothes. The chapter notes the conflict in distance briefly and uses a locution that does not commit to either figure. *(Macfarlane 1916, p.39; MacTutor; Britannica.)*
- **Action 3 — death**: a "feverish cold which soon fell upon his lungs" (Macfarlane); pneumonia (SEP, Britannica). Boole dies at Ballintemple, County Cork, on December 8, 1864, at age 49. *(SEP §1; Macfarlane 1916, p.39; Britannica.)*
- **Action 4 — what the record says about Mary Everest Boole**: she becomes a librarian at Queen's College, London, with the support of Frederick Denison Maurice; teaches mathematics to women and children using methods derived from her own tutor Monsieur Deplace; invents curve stitching (string geometry) as a teaching aid; from the 1880s onward publishes prolifically on mathematical pedagogy and the philosophy of mathematical education. *(MacTutor: Mary Everest Boole entry.)*
- **Action 5 — the honesty close**: the chapter says, in plain prose, that the popular "she preserved his mathematical legacy" framing is folk history not supported by the verified open-access record on present evidence; that the *documented* version of her life is in mathematics education, not in the preservation of Boole's logic; and that this distinction matters because it is the AI-history book's first occasion to demonstrate its evidence standard. *(See `brief.md` Boundary Contract; `open-questions.md` Q3.)*
- **OFF-PAGE**: the long road from Boole's calculus to Shannon's switching circuits — mentioned only as a forward-looking gesture, not as a beat. Chapter 3 is responsible.
- **Closing beat**: a sentence or two that lets the reader leave the chapter with a *calculus* — Boole's own — in their hand, and a clear line that the engineering interpretation belongs to the next part of the book.

## Cross-scene rules

- Every primary quotation must be a direct lift from `sources.md` Green-anchored material. Approximations are not permitted.
- The chapter must close before the engineering interpretation begins. Shannon does not appear in this chapter.
- Mary Everest Boole's "preservation" framing must be flagged in the prose, not silently retained.
- The chapter must use Boole's own term "law of duality" when discussing Boole's own writing, with a parenthetical note that "idempotent law" is the later (1870, Benjamin Peirce) name.
