# People: Chapter 1

## Verified Protagonists

- **George Boole (1815–1864):** A self-taught English mathematician. In 1854, he published *The Laws of Thought*, inventing Boolean algebra. He believed human reasoning could be reduced to mathematical equations. His work was largely ignored by practical engineers for eighty years.
- **David Hilbert (1862–1943):** One of the most influential mathematicians of the 19th and early 20th centuries. In 1928, he formalized the *Entscheidungsproblem* (Decision Problem), asking if there was a mechanical procedure to determine mathematical truth, inadvertently kicking off the theoretical search for a universal computer.
- **Alan Turing (1912–1954):** Brilliant English mathematician. In 1936, he answered Hilbert's challenge not with an equation, but with a conceptual machine (the Turing Machine). He proved that a single, universal machine could simulate any computable process.
- **Alonzo Church (1903–1995):** American mathematician and Turing's doctoral advisor at Princeton. He developed the lambda calculus, proving the same limits of computation as Turing, but without the mechanical metaphor of the tape and read/write head.
- **Claude Shannon (1916–2001):** The father of information theory. As a graduate student at MIT in 1937, he recognized that the obscure, 80-year-old Boolean algebra was the exact mathematical language needed to design and optimize complex electrical relay circuits. He bridged the gap between pure math and physical hardware infrastructure.
